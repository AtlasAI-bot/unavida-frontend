# Chapter 10: Antivirals (Sections 10.1–10.6)

> **Nursing focus:** Antiviral pharmacotherapy is highly diagnosis- and timing-dependent. Nursing practice emphasizes (1) verifying the correct viral syndrome and patient risk status, (2) initiating therapy within evidence-supported windows when applicable, (3) preventing medication errors and clinically significant drug–drug interactions, (4) monitoring organ function and virologic response, and (5) supporting adherence and harm-reduction education.

---

## 10.1 Foundations of Antiviral Pharmacology

### 10.1.1 Expanded Clinical Practice Framework: Antiviral Fundamentals

### 10.1.2 Bedside risk stratification workflow
Nursing teams should use a repeatable risk workflow that starts with timeline, host vulnerability, organ reserve, and medication complexity. In antiviral fundamentals, triage errors usually occur when one of these dimensions is skipped.

1. **Timeline precision**
   - Determine exact start of sentinel symptoms, not just presentation day.
   - Ask when function changed (missed work/school, reduced intake, confusion onset).
   - Capture exposure timing and prophylaxis history where relevant.
   - Record previous partial treatment that may alter resistance risk.
2. **Host vulnerability profile**
   - Age extremes, pregnancy/postpartum status, frailty, and chronic diseases.
   - Immune status, transplant history, chemotherapy, steroid burden, or advanced HIV.
   - Baseline renal/hepatic reserve and recent organ-function trends.
   - Social determinants that affect timely treatment completion.
3. **Medication safety profile**
   - Full reconciliation: prescriptions, OTC products, minerals, antacids, herbals.
   - Identify narrow-therapeutic-index medications with interaction danger.
   - Clarify prior intolerance or severe hypersensitivity events.
   - Confirm swallowing ability, emesis burden, or need for non-oral route.
4. **Escalation profile**
   - Define red flags that require urgent clinician reassessment.
   - Pre-plan after-hours contact route and emergency thresholds.
   - Include caregiver observation instructions when cognition is impaired.

### 10.1.3 Contraindication and caution checklist
For antiviral fundamentals, nurses should screen consistently for contraindication domains before first administration:
- pregnancy/teratogenic considerations when regimen-specific
- severe renal impairment without adjusted plan
- severe hepatic impairment when metabolism is liver-dependent
- unresolved interaction conflicts that may produce toxicity or treatment failure
- inability to obtain full course due to access barriers

When any domain is positive, nursing documentation should clearly state the concern, action taken, and clinician notification response time.

### 10.1.4 Dosing logic teaching (non-prescriptive)
A practical patient-facing explanation model:
- “The dose is chosen for your kidney/liver function so the medicine is strong enough to work and safe enough to tolerate.”
- “Taking extra doses does not speed cure and can increase harm.”
- “Taking doses late or skipping doses can reduce effectiveness.”
- “If vomiting or missed-dose events occur, call us for same-day advice.”

Nursing error traps:
- outdated renal function used for current dosing
- confusion between different formulations/prodrugs
- unclear interval instructions in shift-to-shift handoff
- discharge medication list that omits temporary interaction holds

### 10.1.5 Monitoring matrix for first 72 hours, first 2 weeks, and long-term follow-up
**First 72 hours (acute safety):**
- tolerance and administration success
- evolving adverse effects (GI, neurocognitive, rash, hemodynamic changes)
- hydration status and urine output
- early response trend versus deterioration

**First 2 weeks (treatment execution):**
- adherence integrity and refill access
- lab follow-up completion
- dose adjustments after changing renal/hepatic function
- emerging interaction effects after medication additions

**Long-term (when chronic therapy applies):**
- virologic/laboratory response sustainability
- cumulative organ toxicity surveillance
- prevention counseling refresh (vaccines, safer sex, safer injection)
- quality-of-life and mental health support

### 10.1.6 Adverse effect communication model
Use a three-tier script to avoid underreporting and over-alarm.

- **Expected/manageable effects:** mild nausea, headache, transient fatigue; provide self-management guidance.
- **Call same day:** persistent vomiting, severe diarrhea, reduced intake, rash progression, new dizziness.
- **Emergency:** breathing difficulty, chest pain, severe confusion, seizure, anuria/near-anuria, signs of anaphylaxis.

Document patient teach-back: “Patient accurately repeated emergency warning signs and callback process.”

### 10.1.7 Interaction prevention protocol
In antiviral fundamentals, interaction harm can be prevented by making reconciliation operational, not optional.

Protocol elements:
1. one standardized med-list source of truth per encounter
2. mandatory supplement/herbal prompt
3. pharmacy review trigger for high-risk combinations
4. explicit temporary hold/restart instructions in writing
5. callback within 48–72 hours if major medication changes were made

### 10.1.8 Equity and access lens
Clinical efficacy depends on completion feasibility. Nurses should assess:
- transportation reliability and pharmacy distance
- insurance status and prior authorization delays
- housing security for medication storage
- literacy/language barriers requiring translated instructions
- ability to attend laboratory and infusion appointments

Mitigation strategies include mail-order pharmacy, social work referral, interpreter-supported counseling, and simplified reminder systems.

### 10.1.9 Transition-of-care bundle
Before discharge or care transfer in antiviral fundamentals, confirm the following “no-miss” items:
- diagnosis and indication clearly stated
- exact start/stop dates or chronic continuation plan
- dose rationale if adjusted for organ function
- pending labs with owner and due date
- interaction plan and temporary medication holds
- emergency thresholds and contact numbers
- follow-up appointment date/time and transport plan

### 10.1.10 Nursing documentation phrases that improve safety
- “Antiviral start eligible per symptom timeline and risk profile; provider notified at [time].”
- “Comprehensive med reconciliation completed including OTC/supplement/herbal products.”
- “Renal/hepatic function reviewed; dose-verification concern escalated before administration.”
- “Teach-back completed: patient verbalized purpose (diagnostic accuracy, timing windows, interaction screening, and organ-function-based dosing), schedule, and red-flag symptoms.”
- “Discharge handoff includes pending labs, refill logistics, and interaction hold/restart plan.”

### 10.1.11 Case-based micro scenarios
1. **Access barrier scenario:** patient cannot pay at pickup. Nursing intervention: same-day financial navigation and alternative dispensing path.
2. **Cognitive barrier scenario:** patient cannot recall instructions. Nursing intervention: caregiver-inclusive counseling and written pictorial schedule.
3. **Interaction scenario:** patient starts new OTC remedy. Nursing intervention: immediate review and clarified timing/avoidance instructions.
4. **Toxicity scenario:** new confusion and oliguria. Nursing intervention: urgent escalation, hold-per-order, and repeat renal labs.
5. **Adherence scenario:** repeated missed evening doses. Nursing intervention: schedule redesign to stable morning anchor and reminder support.

### 10.1.12 Patient-centered communication examples
- “We are choosing this plan because your risk factors suggest benefit if we act quickly.”
- “Your questions about side effects are important; we will monitor together and adjust if needed.”
- “If cost or transport becomes a problem, tell us immediately so we can keep treatment on track.”
- “This treatment goal is diagnostic accuracy, timing windows, interaction screening, and organ-function-based dosing; today’s plan is one step in that larger goal.”

### 10.1.13 Quality-improvement opportunities for nursing units
Unit-level improvements in antiviral fundamentals can include:
- protocolized symptom-onset documentation fields
- automatic high-risk alerts for renal dosing review
- pharmacist cosign triggers for interaction-heavy regimens
- standard discharge checklist embedded in EHR
- follow-up call programs for high-risk outpatients

These improvements reduce preventable delays, interaction injuries, and readmissions while improving antiviral effectiveness in real-world care.


### 10.1.14 Pathophysiology-to-treatment linkage: what nurses should visualize at the bedside
When nurses assess a patient with a suspected viral illness, it is helpful to mentally separate disease into three overlapping phases: (1) inoculation and early replication, (2) peak replication with symptom evolution, and (3) host inflammatory/convalescent phase. This framing improves clinical judgment about when antivirals are most likely to help.

- **Early replication phase:** viral burden rises quickly, often before severe symptoms. Antivirals are most biologically effective here.
- **Peak replication phase:** antivirals may still reduce progression in selected high-risk patients, especially those with impaired immunity.
- **Inflammatory phase:** organ injury may become more immune-mediated than virus-mediated; antiviral benefit may diminish while supportive or anti-inflammatory care becomes more central.

Nursing assessment points that anchor this model:
- precise symptom clock (first fever, first respiratory symptom, first vesicle, first exposure)
- host risk profile (age, pregnancy, chronic disease, immunosuppression)
- target organ risk (CNS, eye, liver, kidney, lung)
- route feasibility (oral tolerance, enteral access, IV access, home infusion feasibility)

### 10.1.15 Dosing logic principles (educational, non-prescriptive)
Nurses do not independently prescribe in many settings, but nursing practice is central to safe dosing execution and error interception.

Core principles:
1. **Dose follows pharmacokinetics and organ clearance.** Renally cleared antivirals require prompt dose adjustment when kidney function changes.
2. **Loading vs maintenance logic.** Some agents require rapid attainment of therapeutic concentrations followed by lower ongoing doses.
3. **Time-above-threshold vs total exposure.** For some drugs, missed intervals matter as much as total daily dose.
4. **Schedule integrity matters for suppression therapy.** In chronic infections (HIV/HBV), consistency prevents rebound replication and resistance selection.
5. **Do not extrapolate one formulation to another.** Prodrugs and different salts are not mg-for-mg interchangeable.

High-yield nursing checks before first dose:
- most recent creatinine/eGFR and trends, not just single values
- hepatic function when relevant to metabolism or toxicity risk
- pregnancy status when regimen-dependent
- full medication reconciliation including minerals, antacids, herbals
- ability to obtain refill continuity after discharge

### 10.1.16 Monitoring architecture: baseline, early follow-up, longitudinal surveillance
A practical nursing framework is to categorize monitoring into three buckets.

**Baseline (before/at start):**
- confirmation of diagnosis or pretest probability sufficient for empiric therapy
- organ function profile (renal/hepatic/hematologic)
- symptom severity and red flags
- psychosocial adherence risks (housing instability, food insecurity, stigma, health literacy)

**Early follow-up (24–96 hours for acute regimens; 1–4 weeks for chronic):**
- tolerability, administration success, adverse-effect surveillance
- response trajectory (fever curve, lesion burden, oxygen need, GI symptoms)
- interaction consequences (new confusion, bradycardia, unexplained INR changes, etc.)

**Longitudinal surveillance (chronic suppression):**
- virologic response markers
- organ toxicity trend detection
- adherence durability and refill gaps
- prevention counseling updates (vaccination, safer sex, needle safety)

### 10.1.17 Patient education scripts nurses can use
- **Timing script:** “This medicine works best when started early. Your symptom timeline helps us decide if you are still in the benefit window.”
- **Adherence script:** “For this antiviral, consistency is the treatment. Missed doses can let the virus multiply and can reduce future options.”
- **Interaction script:** “Please call before adding supplements, antacids, or herbal products. Some can make the antiviral much less effective.”
- **Toxicity script:** “If you notice reduced urine, severe vomiting, new confusion, yellowing skin, or severe rash, call urgently or seek emergency care.”

### 10.1.18 Transitions-of-care safety bundle for antivirals
At discharge or transfer, nurses should verify:
- final antiviral start date/time and planned stop date
- renal/hepatic dose rationale documented for the receiving team
- required labs and who is responsible for follow-up
- interaction-sensitive home medications reconciled
- refill logistics, prior authorization status, and pharmacy stock
- plain-language written instructions with red-flag symptoms

### 10.1.19 Case-based nursing pearls
**Case A: delayed presentation with influenza-like illness**
A high-risk older adult presents day 4 after symptom onset. Even outside ideal windows, severe-risk status may still justify treatment. Nursing role: escalate risk profile, avoid therapeutic nihilism, and support guideline-concordant clinician decisions.

**Case B: recurrent HSV with frequent outbreaks**
A patient reports monthly outbreaks and relationship stress. Nursing role: normalize stigma-sensitive communication, discuss suppressive therapy goals, and coach on trigger recognition and adherence strategies.

**Case C: chronic suppression with refill gaps**
A patient misses medication because pay cycles and transportation conflict with pharmacy hours. Nursing role: coordinate 90-day fills, mail pharmacy options, social work linkage, and refill reminders.


### 10.1.20 Why antivirals are different from antibacterials
Viruses rely on host cellular machinery to replicate. Unlike bacteria, viruses do not have ribosomes, independent metabolic pathways, or cell walls that can be selectively targeted without affecting human cells. As a result, antiviral therapy tends to:
- **Target narrow viral-specific enzymes or proteins** (e.g., viral polymerases, proteases, neuraminidase, integrase).
- **Carry higher risk of host toxicity** when targets overlap with human cellular processes.
- **Require precise timing**: many antivirals work best early in infection, before peak viral replication and before host inflammation dominates symptoms.
- **Select for resistance** when viral replication continues under subtherapeutic drug pressure (e.g., missed doses, drug interactions lowering concentrations).

**Nursing implication:** Confirm **indication**, **time since symptom onset/exposure**, and **risk category** before assuming an antiviral will benefit the patient.

---

### 10.1.21 Core strategies: direct-acting antivirals vs host-directed therapies
**Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs)** inhibit viral proteins essential for replication (e.g., hepatitis C polymerase inhibitors; HIV protease inhibitors). DAAs often have:
- **High potency**
- **High specificity**
- **Resistance risk** (especially if used as monotherapy when combination therapy is standard)

**Host-directed therapies** modify host immune responses or block host entry factors (e.g., interferons; some monoclonal antibodies in specific settings). Potential advantages include broader activity, but disadvantages include immune-mediated adverse effects and variable patient response.

**Nursing implication:** DAAs usually demand careful **adherence counseling** and **interaction screening**; host-directed therapies demand vigilant monitoring for **systemic adverse effects** and **contraindications**.

---

### 10.1.22 Treatment goals: cure, suppression, or prevention
Antivirals fall into three practical outcome categories:

1. **Cure (eradication)**
   - Most clearly achieved with many **hepatitis C** DAA regimens (sustained virologic response, SVR).
2. **Suppression (long-term control)**
   - Common in **HIV** and **chronic hepatitis B** where the goal is to reduce viral load, prevent progression, and reduce transmission risk.
3. **Prevention**
   - **Pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP)** and **post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP)** for HIV
   - **Seasonal influenza antivirals** in selected prophylaxis contexts
   - **RSV monoclonal antibodies** to prevent severe disease in infants and some high-risk patients (not classic “antivirals” but antiviral immunoprophylaxis)

**Nursing implication:** Patient education should align expectations: “This drug suppresses the virus” vs “This drug cures the infection” vs “This reduces the chance of infection or severe disease.”

---

### 10.1.23 Antiviral timing: the “replication window”
Many acute viral infections have a narrow window where viral replication predominates early, followed by host inflammatory response later. Antivirals typically show the most benefit when started:
- **Early in symptom onset** (e.g., influenza neuraminidase inhibitors within ~48 hours for greatest effect; benefit may extend in severe/hospitalized cases).
- **Early after exposure** for prophylaxis (e.g., HIV PEP within 72 hours).
- **Early in illness in high-risk patients** for COVID-19 oral antiviral therapy (time-limited initiation windows).

**Nursing implication:** Triage and workflow matter. Nurses often identify symptom onset timing, exposure risk, and barriers to rapid access (transportation, pharmacy access, insurance delays). These data can determine whether therapy is still indicated.

---

### 10.1.24 Resistance: how it happens and how nursing care prevents it
Resistance emerges when a virus replicates in the presence of antiviral pressure and accumulates mutations that reduce drug susceptibility. Risk factors include:
- **Suboptimal adherence** (missed doses, inconsistent timing)
- **Drug–drug interactions** that lower antiviral concentrations
- **Inadequate dosing** (renal dosing errors are common sources of under- or overdosing)
- **Monotherapy** when combination therapy is required (e.g., HIV)

**Nursing strategies to reduce resistance**
- Teach the “**no gaps**” concept for suppression therapy (especially HIV): consistent daily dosing prevents viral replication.
- Perform medication reconciliation with a focus on:
  - OTC medications (e.g., antacids, supplements)
  - Herbal products (e.g., St. John’s wort—major interaction risk with some antiretrovirals).
- Confirm renal and hepatic function for dosing and safety.
- Encourage timely follow-up labs and viral load monitoring.

---

### 10.1.25 Safety themes across antiviral therapy
Across antiviral classes, nurses repeatedly monitor for:
- **Renal toxicity** (e.g., acyclovir crystal nephropathy; tenofovir-associated kidney injury in susceptible patients)
- **Hepatotoxicity** (numerous antivirals; also immune reconstitution or hepatitis flare phenomena)
- **Bone marrow suppression** (e.g., ribavirin-related anemia; interferon effects; zidovudine historically)
- **Neuropsychiatric effects** (e.g., interferon; some older influenza agents; ART-related CNS effects in some patients)
- **Teratogenicity and pregnancy considerations** (e.g., ribavirin is teratogenic; pregnancy-safe ART selection requires careful regimen design)

**Clinical mindset:** Antiviral regimens are often safe when used correctly, but harm frequently arises from *mismatched patient factors* (renal impairment, pregnancy), *interaction oversight*, or *delayed recognition of toxicity*.

### 10.2.14 Expanded nursing assessment by syndrome
Herpesvirus presentations vary by host and site. Nursing triage quality often determines outcomes.

- **Mucocutaneous HSV:** assess pain, urinary retention risk, hydration, and secondary bacterial superinfection.
- **Orolabial disease:** evaluate oral intake, dehydration risk, and immunocompromised status.
- **Genital HSV:** include trauma-informed sexual history, pregnancy status, dysuria severity, and psychosocial distress.
- **Herpes zoster:** map dermatomes, check for ophthalmic/otic involvement, and screen for disseminated lesions in immunocompromised patients.
- **CMV in immunocompromised hosts:** assess constitutional symptoms, visual changes, GI bleeding/diarrhea, respiratory symptoms, and marrow suppression signs.

### 10.2.15 Contraindications and caution contexts
- severe renal dysfunction without dose adjustment
- known severe hypersensitivity to class agents
- inability to maintain hydration during nephrotoxic regimens
- concurrent nephrotoxins (aminoglycosides, amphotericin, NSAID burden in vulnerable patients)
- advanced marrow suppression when considering ganciclovir/valganciclovir

### 10.2.16 Monitoring details: practical bedside checklist
**Daily or per protocol in higher-acuity settings:**
- creatinine trend and urine output
- mental status changes suggestive of neurotoxicity
- CBC with differential for marrow-suppressive regimens
- electrolyte panel if foscarnet is used
- lesion response trajectory and pain control adequacy

**Outpatient follow-up priorities:**
- adherence and dose timing
- recurrence frequency and trigger review
- adverse effects that threaten continuation
- sexual health counseling and partner safety planning

### 10.2.17 Pregnancy and neonatal considerations (nursing perspective)
- late-pregnancy recurrent genital HSV management often aims to reduce lesions at delivery
- intrapartum communication is crucial when active lesions/symptoms are present
- neonatal HSV risk education should be explicit and calm: fever, poor feeding, lethargy, vesicular rash require urgent evaluation
- postpartum counseling should include hand hygiene and lesion-covering precautions for caregivers with active lesions

### 10.2.18 Pain and quality-of-life nursing care in zoster
Shingles pain can be severe and persistent. Nursing care includes:
- early multimodal pain planning
- skin care teaching (clean, dry lesions; avoid occlusive irritants)
- sleep and mood screening
- counseling on postherpetic neuralgia risk and follow-up pathways

### 10.2.19 Resistant disease and escalation cues
Escalate promptly when:
- lesions progress despite adherence
- viral syndrome worsens in a profoundly immunocompromised patient
- renal injury or neurotoxicity limits first-line agents
- vision symptoms, severe headache, focal neurologic deficits, or confusion emerge

### 10.2.20 Patient teaching scripts for herpesvirus care
- “This treatment controls outbreaks; it does not remove latent virus from the body.”
- “Start medication at the first sign of recurrence if your plan includes episodic treatment.”
- “Drink enough fluids while on this medication unless your kidney team gave different instructions.”
- “Call right away for eye pain, vision changes, severe headache, or confusion.”

### 10.2.21 Case pearls
**Transplant recipient with diarrhea on valganciclovir:** monitor CBC and ANC trend closely; neutropenia can require protocol-guided adjustment and infection precautions.

**Older adult on high-dose valacyclovir with new delirium:** suspect accumulation in renal impairment until proven otherwise; verify renal dosing before attributing symptoms solely to infection.


### 10.2.22 Clinical overview: why herpesviruses matter
Herpesviruses establish **latency** with intermittent reactivation. Antivirals typically:
- Reduce **symptom severity and duration**
- Reduce **viral shedding** and transmission risk (notably HSV suppressive therapy)
- Prevent **complications** (e.g., neonatal HSV, ocular involvement, disseminated VZV, CMV disease in immunocompromised patients)

**Nursing priorities**
- Identify high-risk presentations requiring urgent therapy:
  - **Ocular HSV** (risk of vision loss)
  - **HSV encephalitis** (high morbidity/mortality)
  - **Disseminated VZV** in immunocompromised patients
  - **Neonatal HSV** or pregnancy-associated concerns
- Implement infection control precautions appropriate to presentation (e.g., disseminated zoster).

### 10.2.23 Acyclovir, valacyclovir, famciclovir (HSV/VZV)

### 10.2.24 Mechanism of action (high-level)
These agents are nucleoside analogs that inhibit viral DNA polymerase after activation (acyclovir requires viral thymidine kinase for initial phosphorylation, contributing to selectivity).

### 10.2.25 Common indications
- **HSV-1/HSV-2**: primary genital herpes, recurrent outbreaks, chronic suppressive therapy
- **VZV**: shingles (herpes zoster), varicella in selected patients
- **Prophylaxis** in immunocompromised patients (protocol-dependent)

### 10.2.26 Administration considerations
- **Valacyclovir** is a prodrug of acyclovir with improved oral bioavailability; often preferred for outpatient adherence (less frequent dosing).
- **Famciclovir** (prodrug of penciclovir) is another oral option.

### 10.2.27 Adverse effects and nursing monitoring
- **Nephrotoxicity (acyclovir)**: risk increases with IV therapy, dehydration, rapid infusion, and renal impairment.
  - *Nursing actions:* ensure adequate hydration, monitor urine output, review creatinine/eGFR, follow infusion rate protocols.
- **Neurotoxicity** (confusion, tremor, hallucinations) can occur especially with renal impairment.
  - *Nursing actions:* assess mental status changes; verify renal dosing; escalate promptly.
- **GI effects** (nausea, diarrhea), headache.

### 10.2.28 Dosing and renal adjustment
These drugs require **renal dose adjustment**. Failure to adjust may cause neurotoxicity and renal injury.

**High-yield nursing safety check:** If an older adult with reduced kidney function develops new confusion while receiving acyclovir/valacyclovir, treat this as a medication safety signal and assess for dose-related toxicity.

---

### 10.2.29 Ganciclovir and valganciclovir (CMV)

### 10.2.30 Indications
- **CMV retinitis**, CMV colitis/esophagitis, and CMV disease in immunocompromised patients (e.g., transplant, advanced HIV).
- **Prophylaxis** or preemptive therapy in transplant protocols.

### 10.2.31 Key adverse effects
- **Bone marrow suppression** (neutropenia, anemia, thrombocytopenia)
  - *Nursing actions:* monitor CBC with differential; assess for infection signs; institute neutropenic precautions when appropriate.
- **Renal toxicity** (dose adjustment required)
- **Teratogenicity/reproductive risks**: counsel about contraception as appropriate per protocol.

### 10.2.32 Nursing considerations in immunocompromised patients
- Watch for overlapping toxicities with other marrow-suppressing agents.
- Screen for symptoms of CMV organ disease:
  - Visual changes (retinitis)
  - Dysphagia/odynophagia (esophagitis)
  - Abdominal pain/diarrhea (colitis)

### 10.2.33 Foscarnet and cidofovir (resistant HSV/CMV; last-line profiles)

### 10.2.34 Why these are “high vigilance” medications
Foscarnet and cidofovir are used when resistance or intolerance limits first-line options. They carry high toxicity burdens.

**Foscarnet**
- Major risks: **nephrotoxicity**, electrolyte disturbances (e.g., Ca/Mg/K abnormalities), seizures secondary to electrolyte shifts.
- *Nursing actions:* frequent renal labs; electrolyte monitoring; hydration protocols; seizure precautions if indicated.

**Cidofovir**
- Major risks: **nephrotoxicity**, uveitis/ocular toxicity; requires careful protocols and often co-administration strategies to reduce nephrotoxicity (protocol dependent).
- *Nursing actions:* confirm pre-hydration and protective measures per institutional policy; monitor for ocular symptoms.

### 10.2.35 Patient teaching: HSV suppressive therapy and stigma-sensitive care
HSV is common and often associated with shame or fear. Nursing education should be factual and nonjudgmental:
- Explain outbreak triggers and that antivirals can reduce outbreaks and viral shedding.
- Teach safer sex practices and partner communication strategies.
- Clarify that suppressive therapy reduces risk but does not guarantee zero transmission.
- Encourage adherence and follow-up, especially when psychosocial distress is prominent.

## 10.3 Antivirals for Influenza and Other Common Respiratory Viruses (Including RSV)

### 10.3.1 Expanded Clinical Practice Framework: Respiratory Antiviral Care

### 10.3.2 Bedside risk stratification workflow
Nursing teams should use a repeatable risk workflow that starts with timeline, host vulnerability, organ reserve, and medication complexity. In respiratory antiviral care, triage errors usually occur when one of these dimensions is skipped.

1. **Timeline precision**
   - Determine exact start of sentinel symptoms, not just presentation day.
   - Ask when function changed (missed work/school, reduced intake, confusion onset).
   - Capture exposure timing and prophylaxis history where relevant.
   - Record previous partial treatment that may alter resistance risk.
2. **Host vulnerability profile**
   - Age extremes, pregnancy/postpartum status, frailty, and chronic diseases.
   - Immune status, transplant history, chemotherapy, steroid burden, or advanced HIV.
   - Baseline renal/hepatic reserve and recent organ-function trends.
   - Social determinants that affect timely treatment completion.
3. **Medication safety profile**
   - Full reconciliation: prescriptions, OTC products, minerals, antacids, herbals.
   - Identify narrow-therapeutic-index medications with interaction danger.
   - Clarify prior intolerance or severe hypersensitivity events.
   - Confirm swallowing ability, emesis burden, or need for non-oral route.
4. **Escalation profile**
   - Define red flags that require urgent clinician reassessment.
   - Pre-plan after-hours contact route and emergency thresholds.
   - Include caregiver observation instructions when cognition is impaired.

### 10.3.3 Contraindication and caution checklist
For respiratory antiviral care, nurses should screen consistently for contraindication domains before first administration:
- pregnancy/teratogenic considerations when regimen-specific
- severe renal impairment without adjusted plan
- severe hepatic impairment when metabolism is liver-dependent
- unresolved interaction conflicts that may produce toxicity or treatment failure
- inability to obtain full course due to access barriers

When any domain is positive, nursing documentation should clearly state the concern, action taken, and clinician notification response time.

### 10.3.4 Dosing logic teaching (non-prescriptive)
A practical patient-facing explanation model:
- “The dose is chosen for your kidney/liver function so the medicine is strong enough to work and safe enough to tolerate.”
- “Taking extra doses does not speed cure and can increase harm.”
- “Taking doses late or skipping doses can reduce effectiveness.”
- “If vomiting or missed-dose events occur, call us for same-day advice.”

Nursing error traps:
- outdated renal function used for current dosing
- confusion between different formulations/prodrugs
- unclear interval instructions in shift-to-shift handoff
- discharge medication list that omits temporary interaction holds

### 10.3.5 Monitoring matrix for first 72 hours, first 2 weeks, and long-term follow-up
**First 72 hours (acute safety):**
- tolerance and administration success
- evolving adverse effects (GI, neurocognitive, rash, hemodynamic changes)
- hydration status and urine output
- early response trend versus deterioration

**First 2 weeks (treatment execution):**
- adherence integrity and refill access
- lab follow-up completion
- dose adjustments after changing renal/hepatic function
- emerging interaction effects after medication additions

**Long-term (when chronic therapy applies):**
- virologic/laboratory response sustainability
- cumulative organ toxicity surveillance
- prevention counseling refresh (vaccines, safer sex, safer injection)
- quality-of-life and mental health support

### 10.3.6 Adverse effect communication model
Use a three-tier script to avoid underreporting and over-alarm.

- **Expected/manageable effects:** mild nausea, headache, transient fatigue; provide self-management guidance.
- **Call same day:** persistent vomiting, severe diarrhea, reduced intake, rash progression, new dizziness.
- **Emergency:** breathing difficulty, chest pain, severe confusion, seizure, anuria/near-anuria, signs of anaphylaxis.

Document patient teach-back: “Patient accurately repeated emergency warning signs and callback process.”

### 10.3.7 Interaction prevention protocol
In respiratory antiviral care, interaction harm can be prevented by making reconciliation operational, not optional.

Protocol elements:
1. one standardized med-list source of truth per encounter
2. mandatory supplement/herbal prompt
3. pharmacy review trigger for high-risk combinations
4. explicit temporary hold/restart instructions in writing
5. callback within 48–72 hours if major medication changes were made

### 10.3.8 Equity and access lens
Clinical efficacy depends on completion feasibility. Nurses should assess:
- transportation reliability and pharmacy distance
- insurance status and prior authorization delays
- housing security for medication storage
- literacy/language barriers requiring translated instructions
- ability to attend laboratory and infusion appointments

Mitigation strategies include mail-order pharmacy, social work referral, interpreter-supported counseling, and simplified reminder systems.

### 10.3.9 Transition-of-care bundle
Before discharge or care transfer in respiratory antiviral care, confirm the following “no-miss” items:
- diagnosis and indication clearly stated
- exact start/stop dates or chronic continuation plan
- dose rationale if adjusted for organ function
- pending labs with owner and due date
- interaction plan and temporary medication holds
- emergency thresholds and contact numbers
- follow-up appointment date/time and transport plan

### 10.3.10 Nursing documentation phrases that improve safety
- “Antiviral start eligible per symptom timeline and risk profile; provider notified at [time].”
- “Comprehensive med reconciliation completed including OTC/supplement/herbal products.”
- “Renal/hepatic function reviewed; dose-verification concern escalated before administration.”
- “Teach-back completed: patient verbalized purpose (early triage, high-risk phenotype recognition, route selection, and deterioration surveillance), schedule, and red-flag symptoms.”
- “Discharge handoff includes pending labs, refill logistics, and interaction hold/restart plan.”

### 10.3.11 Case-based micro scenarios
1. **Access barrier scenario:** patient cannot pay at pickup. Nursing intervention: same-day financial navigation and alternative dispensing path.
2. **Cognitive barrier scenario:** patient cannot recall instructions. Nursing intervention: caregiver-inclusive counseling and written pictorial schedule.
3. **Interaction scenario:** patient starts new OTC remedy. Nursing intervention: immediate review and clarified timing/avoidance instructions.
4. **Toxicity scenario:** new confusion and oliguria. Nursing intervention: urgent escalation, hold-per-order, and repeat renal labs.
5. **Adherence scenario:** repeated missed evening doses. Nursing intervention: schedule redesign to stable morning anchor and reminder support.

### 10.3.12 Patient-centered communication examples
- “We are choosing this plan because your risk factors suggest benefit if we act quickly.”
- “Your questions about side effects are important; we will monitor together and adjust if needed.”
- “If cost or transport becomes a problem, tell us immediately so we can keep treatment on track.”
- “This treatment goal is early triage, high-risk phenotype recognition, route selection, and deterioration surveillance; today’s plan is one step in that larger goal.”

### 10.3.13 Quality-improvement opportunities for nursing units
Unit-level improvements in respiratory antiviral care can include:
- protocolized symptom-onset documentation fields
- automatic high-risk alerts for renal dosing review
- pharmacist cosign triggers for interaction-heavy regimens
- standard discharge checklist embedded in EHR
- follow-up call programs for high-risk outpatients

These improvements reduce preventable delays, interaction injuries, and readmissions while improving antiviral effectiveness in real-world care.


### 10.3.14 Respiratory viral pathophysiology and timing logic
In uncomplicated influenza, viral replication peaks early; systemic symptoms (fever, myalgias) are driven by both viral load and cytokine signaling. In severe disease, host inflammation and secondary complications (bacterial pneumonia, decompensation of chronic disease) become major drivers.

Nursing implication: antiviral treatment decisions should integrate both **clock time** and **clinical severity trajectory** rather than relying on a rigid binary window.

### 10.3.15 High-risk phenotype identification at triage
At first contact, nurses should screen for:
- age extremes
- pregnancy/postpartum status
- obesity with cardiometabolic disease
- chronic pulmonary or cardiac disease
- immunosuppression
- neurologic conditions affecting airway clearance
- residence in congregate settings

These variables change treatment urgency, monitoring intensity, and threshold for escalation.

### 10.3.16 Interaction and administration pitfalls
- oseltamivir suspension dosing errors in pediatrics can occur with unit confusion
- zanamivir technique errors reduce delivery and effectiveness
- baloxavir can fail when taken with polyvalent cations near dosing time
- emesis after oral dose may require protocol-based guidance on redosing

### 10.3.17 Nursing management of severe influenza in hospitalized adults
Core bundles include:
- rapid antiviral initiation per protocol
- oxygenation monitoring and escalation triggers
- fluid/electrolyte management
- delirium prevention in older adults
- early mobility where feasible
- surveillance for secondary bacterial infection

### 10.3.18 RSV and prevention-focused counseling
For infants and selected high-risk children, RSV prevention biologics are immunoprophylaxis rather than treatment of active disease.

Nursing counseling points:
- clarify purpose: prevention of severe lower respiratory disease
- explain expected local/systemic post-injection symptoms
- reinforce routine infection prevention (hand hygiene, smoke-free environment, limiting sick contacts)

### 10.3.19 Discharge teaching scripts for respiratory antivirals
- “This medicine may shorten illness and reduce complications, but you still need hydration, rest, and warning-sign monitoring.”
- “If breathing worsens, chest pain develops, confusion appears, or urine drops significantly, seek urgent care.”
- “Finish the prescribed course even if fever improves quickly.”
- “Your flu shot remains important for future seasons.”

### 10.3.20 Case-based nursing pearls
**Case: pregnant patient with flu symptoms and high fever**
Do not delay escalation while awaiting confirmatory testing if institutional guidance supports empiric treatment in high-risk patients.

**Case: adolescent with vomiting after oseltamivir**
Assess dehydration risk, administer with food when appropriate, and clarify antiemetic strategy if ordered.

**Case: COPD patient considered for zanamivir**
Flag airway reactivity risk early and discuss alternatives before discharge prescriptions are finalized.


### 10.3.21 Influenza: disease burden and who benefits most from antivirals
Influenza can cause severe illness, especially in:
- Adults ≥65
- Pregnant patients
- Young children
- Patients with chronic cardiopulmonary disease, immunosuppression, diabetes, renal disease
- Residents of long-term care facilities

Antivirals may:
- Reduce symptom duration (modest average benefit in uncomplicated cases)
- Reduce complications and hospitalization risk in high-risk groups and severe disease (evidence varies by population and study design).

**Nursing triage focus:** Identify high-risk patients early and expedite testing/therapy within recommended initiation windows.

---

### 10.3.22 Neuraminidase inhibitors: oseltamivir, zanamivir, peramivir

### 10.3.23 Oseltamivir (oral)
- **Indications:** treatment and prophylaxis of influenza A and B.
- **Timing:** greatest benefit when started early; still recommended in severe/hospitalized cases even if later.
- **Adverse effects:** nausea/vomiting (take with food), rare neuropsychiatric events (monitor behavior changes, especially in adolescents).
- **Renal dosing:** required.

**Nursing pearls**
- Reinforce that vaccination remains primary prevention; antivirals are adjunctive.
- Assess hydration status and ability to tolerate oral meds.
- Teach to complete full course even if symptoms improve.

### 10.3.24 Zanamivir (inhaled)
- **Contraindications/precautions:** bronchospasm risk; caution in asthma/COPD.
- *Nursing actions:* assess pulmonary history; teach inhaler technique; ensure rescue bronchodilator access if prescribed.

### 10.3.25 Peramivir (IV)
- Used when oral/inhaled routes are not feasible; often in hospitalized patients.
- *Nursing actions:* monitor infusion reactions, renal function, and clinical response.

---

### 10.3.26 Cap-dependent endonuclease inhibitor: baloxavir marboxil (oral)
Baloxavir is a single-dose oral antiviral for influenza in selected patients.

**Practical considerations**
- **Single-dose** may improve adherence.
- **Drug–food/mineral interactions:** avoid co-administration with polyvalent cations (e.g., calcium, magnesium, iron, some supplements) which may reduce absorption.
- Evaluate appropriateness based on age, pregnancy status, and guideline recommendations.

---

### 10.3.27 Older influenza agents (adamantanes) and resistance
Amantadine and rimantadine historically targeted influenza A (M2 ion channel) but are largely not recommended due to widespread resistance.

**Nursing implication:** If a patient reports using an older influenza medication, verify current guideline recommendations and consult prescriber/pharmacist.

---

### 10.3.28 RSV: ribavirin and immunoprophylaxis (palivizumab, nirsevimab)
RSV can cause severe lower respiratory disease in infants, older adults, and immunocompromised patients.

### 10.3.29 Ribavirin (selected cases)
- Used in limited settings; administration route and indication depend on institutional protocols.
- Key toxicity: **hemolytic anemia**, teratogenicity (strict pregnancy avoidance measures).
- *Nursing actions:* CBC monitoring; pregnancy screening/contraception counseling per protocol; assess fatigue, pallor, dyspnea.

### 10.3.30 RSV monoclonal antibodies (prevention in infants/high-risk populations)
- Agents such as palivizumab and newer long-acting options (e.g., nirsevimab) are preventive immunotherapy rather than treatment for active infection.

**Nursing role**
- Verify eligibility criteria (age, gestational age, comorbidities, seasonality, local recommendations).
- Educate caregivers: prophylaxis reduces severe disease risk but does not replace standard infection prevention strategies.

---

### 10.3.31 Supportive care remains central in respiratory viral illness
Even with antivirals, supportive care drives outcomes:
- Hydration, fever control, rest
- Monitoring for respiratory distress and hypoxemia
- Escalation for signs of pneumonia, sepsis, or asthma/COPD exacerbation

**Nursing escalation triggers**
- Increased work of breathing, new hypoxemia
- Persistent fever with worsening cough or chest pain
- Confusion, dehydration, poor oral intake
- High-risk patient with rapid clinical decline

### 10.4.14 Chronic viral hepatitis: nursing framing
HBV and HCV management requires long-horizon care. The nurse helps patients understand that laboratory response, fibrosis risk, and prevention behaviors are as important as symptom control.

### 10.4.15 HBV disease context and goals
HBV outcomes range from inactive carrier states to progressive fibrosis/cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma risk. Antiviral suppression aims to reduce hepatic inflammation and long-term complications.

Nursing priorities:
- reinforce long-term follow-up even when asymptomatic
- monitor adherence during symptom-free periods
- educate that stopping therapy abruptly can trigger flare in selected patients

### 10.4.16 HCV cure pathways and reinfection prevention
HCV DAAs can achieve cure in most patients with proper regimen selection and adherence.

Nursing roles:
- prepare patients for finite-course success mindset
- verify interaction-sensitive medications before first dose
- provide reinfection prevention counseling without stigma
- coordinate post-treatment SVR testing milestones

### 10.4.17 Contraindications/cautions across HBV/HCV therapy
- decompensated cirrhosis limits some protease inhibitor use
- renal impairment modifies drug choice and dose in some regimens
- pregnancy status affects regimen eligibility, especially where ribavirin is considered
- HBV coinfection status must be clarified before HCV DAA initiation

### 10.4.18 Monitoring map for hepatitis antivirals
**Baseline:** viral load, genotype (if needed), fibrosis staging, hepatic function, renal function, coinfection status, pregnancy assessment as relevant.

**On-treatment:** adherence, transaminase trends, adverse effects, interaction surveillance.

**Post-treatment:**
- HBV: ongoing suppression/flare surveillance and HCC screening where indicated
- HCV: SVR confirmation and continued liver surveillance in advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis

### 10.4.19 Patient education scripts

- “For hepatitis C, many modern regimens can cure infection if doses are taken consistently for the full course.”

### 10.4.22 Hepatitis B (HBV): goals and treatment logic
HBV can become chronic, increasing the risk of cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma. Treatment goals include:
- Suppress HBV replication (lower HBV DNA)
- Reduce hepatic inflammation and fibrosis progression
- Prevent decompensation and reduce transmission risk

**Key point:** Many patients require **long-term or indefinite therapy**, and stopping therapy can cause **HBV flare** in certain contexts.

### 10.4.23 HBV antivirals: tenofovir (TDF/TAF), entecavir, pegylated interferon

### 10.4.24 Nucleos(t)ide analogs (tenofovir, entecavir)
These inhibit HBV polymerase and reduce viral replication.

**Tenofovir**
- Available as formulations with different safety profiles (e.g., TDF vs TAF).
- Nursing monitoring focuses on:
  - **Renal function** (creatinine/eGFR, urine markers as protocol indicates)
  - **Bone health** considerations in at-risk patients (especially with certain formulations).

**Entecavir**
- Generally well tolerated; renal dosing required.
- Monitor hepatic labs and viral load per protocol.

**Adherence emphasis**
- Long-term therapy means adherence fatigue is common. Nurses can normalize challenges and provide structured problem-solving (pill boxes, reminders, aligning dosing with routines).

### 10.4.25 Pegylated interferon (selected patients)
Interferon stimulates immune-mediated antiviral activity and can achieve durable responses in selected patients.

**High-yield adverse effects**
- Flu-like symptoms
- Depression/irritability
- Cytopenias
- Thyroid dysfunction

**Nursing actions**
- Screen mood and suicidality history before and during treatment.
- Monitor CBC and thyroid labs as ordered.
- Teach symptom management strategies (e.g., acetaminophen for flu-like symptoms when appropriate).

---

### 10.4.26 Hepatitis C (HCV): the era of curative DAAs
HCV is commonly curable with oral DAAs, usually in 8–12 weeks depending on regimen, genotype, liver disease stage, and prior treatment history.

**Treatment goals**
- Achieve SVR (sustained virologic response), associated with markedly reduced risk of liver-related complications.

**Nursing care focus**
- Ensure baseline workup completion (labs, fibrosis staging, medication reconciliation).
- Support adherence to a finite regimen.
- Coordinate follow-up testing to confirm SVR.
- Promote harm reduction and reinfection prevention (e.g., safe injection practices, substance use treatment linkage).

---

### 10.4.27 Common HCV DAA components and regimen concepts
Rather than memorizing every product, nurses should understand common “building blocks”:
- **NS5B polymerase inhibitors** (e.g., sofosbuvir)
- **NS5A inhibitors** (e.g., ledipasvir, velpatasvir, pibrentasvir)
- **Protease inhibitors (NS3/4A)** (e.g., glecaprevir, voxilaprevir)

**Class-wide nursing considerations**
- **Drug–drug interactions are central**:
  - Acid-reducing agents can reduce absorption of certain DAAs (product-specific).
  - Some anticonvulsants and rifamycins can markedly reduce DAA levels (contraindicated or not recommended).
- **Hepatic impairment:** certain protease inhibitor–containing regimens are contraindicated in decompensated cirrhosis.
- **Adverse effects:** often mild (headache, fatigue), but nurses should not dismiss symptoms; evaluate for anemia, depression, or hepatic decompensation when clinically indicated.

**Patient teaching**
- Missing doses increases risk of treatment failure.
- Do not start OTC/herbal products without checking interactions.
- Continue liver-protective behaviors (alcohol reduction/avoidance; vaccination where indicated).

---

### 10.4.28 Special issue: HBV reactivation during HCV DAA therapy
HBV reactivation has been reported during/after HCV DAA treatment in patients with current or prior HBV infection. Screening and monitoring protocols are recommended.

**Nursing actions**
- Verify HBV screening status before HCV therapy initiation.
- Educate patients to report jaundice, dark urine, severe fatigue, RUQ pain.
- Ensure ordered HBV DNA and liver panel monitoring is completed.

### 10.5.6 Adverse effect communication model
Use a three-tier script to avoid underreporting and over-alarm.

- **Expected/manageable effects:** mild nausea, headache, transient fatigue; provide self-management guidance.
- **Call same day:** persistent vomiting, severe diarrhea, reduced intake, rash progression, new dizziness.
- **Emergency:** breathing difficulty, chest pain, severe confusion, seizure, anuria/near-anuria, signs of anaphylaxis.

Document patient teach-back: “Patient accurately repeated emergency warning signs and callback process.”

### 10.5.7 Interaction prevention protocol
In HIV treatment and prevention care, interaction harm can be prevented by making reconciliation operational, not optional.

Protocol elements:
1. one standardized med-list source of truth per encounter
2. mandatory supplement/herbal prompt
3. pharmacy review trigger for high-risk combinations
4. explicit temporary hold/restart instructions in writing
5. callback within 48–72 hours if major medication changes were made

### 10.5.8 Equity and access lens
Clinical efficacy depends on completion feasibility. Nurses should assess:
- transportation reliability and pharmacy distance
- insurance status and prior authorization delays
- housing security for medication storage
- literacy/language barriers requiring translated instructions
- ability to attend laboratory and infusion appointments

Mitigation strategies include mail-order pharmacy, social work referral, interpreter-supported counseling, and simplified reminder systems.

### 10.5.9 Transition-of-care bundle
Before discharge or care transfer in HIV treatment and prevention care, confirm the following “no-miss” items:
- diagnosis and indication clearly stated
- exact start/stop dates or chronic continuation plan
- dose rationale if adjusted for organ function
- pending labs with owner and due date
- interaction plan and temporary medication holds
- emergency thresholds and contact numbers
- follow-up appointment date/time and transport plan

### 10.5.10 Nursing documentation phrases that improve safety
- “Antiviral start eligible per symptom timeline and risk profile; provider notified at [time].”
- “Comprehensive med reconciliation completed including OTC/supplement/herbal products.”
- “Renal/hepatic function reviewed; dose-verification concern escalated before administration.”
- “Teach-back completed: patient verbalized purpose (durable adherence support, viral suppression monitoring, and stigma-sensitive counseling), schedule, and red-flag symptoms.”
- “Discharge handoff includes pending labs, refill logistics, and interaction hold/restart plan.”

### 10.5.11 Case-based micro scenarios
1. **Access barrier scenario:** patient cannot pay at pickup. Nursing intervention: same-day financial navigation and alternative dispensing path.
2. **Cognitive barrier scenario:** patient cannot recall instructions. Nursing intervention: caregiver-inclusive counseling and written pictorial schedule.
3. **Interaction scenario:** patient starts new OTC remedy. Nursing intervention: immediate review and clarified timing/avoidance instructions.
4. **Toxicity scenario:** new confusion and oliguria. Nursing intervention: urgent escalation, hold-per-order, and repeat renal labs.
5. **Adherence scenario:** repeated missed evening doses. Nursing intervention: schedule redesign to stable morning anchor and reminder support.

### 10.5.12 Patient-centered communication examples
- “We are choosing this plan because your risk factors suggest benefit if we act quickly.”
- “Your questions about side effects are important; we will monitor together and adjust if needed.”
- “If cost or transport becomes a problem, tell us immediately so we can keep treatment on track.”
- “This treatment goal is durable adherence support, viral suppression monitoring, and stigma-sensitive counseling; today’s plan is one step in that larger goal.”

### 10.5.13 Quality-improvement opportunities for nursing units
Unit-level improvements in HIV treatment and prevention care can include:
- protocolized symptom-onset documentation fields
- automatic high-risk alerts for renal dosing review
- pharmacist cosign triggers for interaction-heavy regimens
- standard discharge checklist embedded in EHR
- follow-up call programs for high-risk outpatients

These improvements reduce preventable delays, interaction injuries, and readmissions while improving antiviral effectiveness in real-world care.


### 10.5.14 HIV pathophysiology and therapeutic logic for nurses
HIV infects CD4+ T cells and establishes reservoirs rapidly. ART suppresses replication, preserves immune function, reduces opportunistic infection risk, and prevents sexual transmission when sustained viral suppression is achieved.

Nursing translation: ART success is less about occasional perfect days and more about long-term routine reliability, interaction prevention, and consistent monitoring.

### 10.5.15 Class mechanisms in bedside language
- **NRTIs:** chain termination during reverse transcription
- **NNRTIs:** noncompetitive reverse transcriptase inhibition
- **INSTIs:** block proviral integration into host DNA
- **PIs:** inhibit viral protease-mediated maturation
- **Entry/fusion/capsid agents:** block earlier replication steps

### 10.5.16 Rapid start and readiness support
Many programs support rapid ART start. Nursing readiness work includes:
- immediate barrier assessment (housing, food, transportation)
- stigma and disclosure counseling
- medication literacy using plain language
- short-interval follow-up plans for side-effect troubleshooting

### 10.5.17 Monitoring priorities in the first months
- viral load decline trajectory
- CD4 recovery trends
- renal/hepatic/metabolic tolerability
- interaction checks when new meds are added
- mental health and sleep effects

### 10.5.18 PrEP nursing workflow
PrEP requires structured follow-up:
- baseline HIV testing and renal assessment per protocol
- STI screening and prevention counseling
- adherence support framed as prevention empowerment
- reminder systems for interval testing and refills

### 10.5.19 PEP urgency pathway
PEP is time-critical and typically a 28-day course.

Nursing actions:
- identify exposure type and timing immediately
- expedite baseline testing and first dose access
- counsel on temporary risk-reduction behaviors during follow-up period
- ensure completion and repeat testing schedule clarity

### 10.5.20 Interaction management pearls
- polyvalent cations can reduce absorption of some INSTIs
- enzyme inducers can reduce ART exposure
- boosters increase interaction complexity with cardiovascular and psychotropic agents
- herbals, especially St. John’s wort, can be high-risk

### 10.5.21 Patient teaching scripts
- “Undetectable viral load is both a health goal and a prevention goal.”
- “Do not stop ART without discussing it with your HIV team.”
- “If you miss a dose, follow your clinic’s plan and avoid repeated missed doses.”
- “Call before starting antacids, supplements, or herbal products.”

### 10.5.22 Special populations and nursing nuance
- pregnancy: prioritize regimens with maternal efficacy and fetal safety data
- older adults: polypharmacy and renal function demand closer review
- adolescents: confidentiality, school schedules, and stigma-sensitive adherence supports
- substance-use comorbidity: integrate harm reduction and nonjudgmental care

### 10.5.23 Case pearls
**Case: newly diagnosed patient overwhelmed at discharge**
A same-week nursing follow-up call can rescue adherence by troubleshooting nausea, insurance barriers, and fear-based nonadherence.

**Case: suppressed patient starts over-the-counter supplement stack**
Medication reconciliation at each visit prevents rebound from unrecognized interactions.


### 10.5.24 HIV pharmacology in practice: combination therapy is the rule
HIV replicates rapidly and mutates frequently. Effective treatment requires **combination antiretroviral therapy (ART)** to:
- Achieve and maintain undetectable viral load
- Restore/maintain immune function (increase CD4 count)
- Prevent progression to AIDS
- Reduce transmission (U=U: undetectable equals untransmittable via sex, under defined conditions).

**Nursing implication:** ART is lifelong for most patients; adherence support and stigma-sensitive care are as important as pharmacology.

---

### 10.5.25 Major ART classes and key nursing points

### 10.5.26 NRTIs (nucleoside/nucleotide reverse transcriptase inhibitors)
Often serve as the “backbone” of ART regimens.
- Monitor renal function for agents with renal considerations (agent-specific).
- Monitor for lactic acidosis/hepatic steatosis (rare with modern regimens but serious).
- Teach patients to report unexplained abdominal pain, persistent nausea, severe fatigue, or dyspnea.

### 10.5.27 NNRTIs (non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors)
- Common issue: drug–drug interactions (CYP enzyme effects vary by agent).
- Some agents associated with rash or hepatotoxicity.
- *Nursing actions:* assess for rash severity; monitor LFTs as ordered; counsel on interaction risks.

### 10.5.28 INSTIs (integrase strand transfer inhibitors)
Often preferred in many first-line regimens due to potency and tolerability (regimen selection depends on guidelines and patient factors).
- Interaction issue: chelation with polyvalent cations (antacids, iron, calcium) can reduce absorption of certain INSTIs.
  - *Teaching:* separate dosing as directed; do not “stack” supplements with ART without instruction.

### 10.5.29 Protease inhibitors (PIs)
Potent but associated with metabolic effects and significant interactions (often “boosted” with pharmacokinetic enhancers).
- Monitor for GI intolerance, lipid/glucose changes.
- High interaction burden—meticulous medication reconciliation required.

### 10.5.30 Entry and fusion inhibitors; CCR5 antagonists (specialized roles)
Used in selected scenarios (e.g., resistance, intolerance).
- Nursing considerations depend on route (some are injectable) and monitoring requirements.

### 10.5.31 Long-acting injectable ART (selected patients)
Long-acting regimens can improve adherence for some patients but require reliable clinic attendance.
- *Nursing actions:* confirm appointment adherence ability; manage injection site reactions; coordinate scheduling and missed-dose plans.

### 10.5.32 Initiating ART: baseline assessments and patient-centered counseling
**Baseline assessments** often include:
- HIV viral load and CD4 count
- Genotype resistance testing (when recommended)
- Renal and hepatic function
- Hepatitis A/B/C status, STI screening, pregnancy testing as appropriate
- Review of comorbidities and concurrent medications

**Nursing counseling priorities**
- Explain the purpose of combination therapy and “viral load to undetectable” as the goal.
- Establish an adherence plan tailored to the patient’s routine and barriers.
- Discuss side effects realistically: what is expected, what is urgent.
- Provide trauma-informed, stigma-aware support:
  - Ask permission before discussing sensitive topics.
  - Normalize the chronic-care model.
  - Emphasize confidentiality protections.

---

### 10.5.33 Adherence: the central clinical skill in HIV nursing
Adherence is not simply “patient willpower.” It is influenced by:
- Housing insecurity, food insecurity
- Mental health conditions and substance use disorders
- Transportation and pharmacy access
- Medication cost and insurance interruptions
- Stigma and fear of disclosure
- Complex regimens or intolerable adverse effects

**Nursing interventions**
- Use open, non-punitive questions: “How many doses did you miss in the last week?” rather than “You take it every day, right?”
- Offer practical tools: alarms, blister packs, mail-order pharmacy, synchronized refills.
- Collaborate with case management and social work.
- Address adverse effects early to prevent self-discontinuation.

---

### 10.5.34 Monitoring ART: labs, clinical outcomes, and safety
Common monitoring includes:
- HIV viral load (to confirm suppression)
- CD4 count (especially early in treatment or if clinically indicated)
- Renal function, LFTs, lipid/glucose depending on regimen and comorbidities
- STI screening per risk and guideline recommendations

**Immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS)**
In some patients with advanced immunosuppression, immune recovery after starting ART can unmask or worsen inflammatory responses to occult infections (e.g., TB).

**Nursing red flags**
- New fever, lymphadenopathy, respiratory symptoms, neurologic changes shortly after ART initiation in high-risk patients.
- Ensure prompt evaluation rather than assuming “viral illness.”

---

### 10.5.35 HIV prevention pharmacotherapy: PrEP and PEP

### 10.5.36 Pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP)
PrEP is used by HIV-negative individuals at risk to prevent HIV acquisition.

**Nursing workflow**
- Confirm HIV-negative status before initiation (test per protocol).
- Assess renal function and hepatitis B status for specific PrEP agents (agent-specific).
- Counsel on adherence: effectiveness depends on consistent use.
- Continue risk-reduction counseling and STI screening.

### 10.5.37 Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP)
PEP is an emergency intervention after a potential exposure.
- **Time-critical:** initiate as soon as possible, ideally within hours; generally not recommended beyond a defined time window (often 72 hours).
- Typically a **3-drug regimen** for a defined duration (often 28 days) per guidelines.

**Nursing actions**
- Rapidly assess exposure type and source risk when possible.
- Arrange baseline labs (HIV, hepatitis testing, pregnancy test if appropriate).
- Provide adherence coaching and manage side effects proactively.
- Ensure follow-up testing schedule is understood and feasible.

## 10.6 Antivirals for COVID-19 and Selected Emerging Viral Threats (Clinical Nursing Perspective)

### 10.6.1 Expanded Clinical Practice Framework: Covid And Emerging-Virus Antiviral Care

### 10.6.2 Bedside risk stratification workflow
Nursing teams should use a repeatable risk workflow that starts with timeline, host vulnerability, organ reserve, and medication complexity. In COVID and emerging-virus antiviral care, triage errors usually occur when one of these dimensions is skipped.

1. **Timeline precision**
   - Determine exact start of sentinel symptoms, not just presentation day.
   - Ask when function changed (missed work/school, reduced intake, confusion onset).
   - Capture exposure timing and prophylaxis history where relevant.
   - Record previous partial treatment that may alter resistance risk.
2. **Host vulnerability profile**
   - Age extremes, pregnancy/postpartum status, frailty, and chronic diseases.
   - Immune status, transplant history, chemotherapy, steroid burden, or advanced HIV.
   - Baseline renal/hepatic reserve and recent organ-function trends.
   - Social determinants that affect timely treatment completion.
3. **Medication safety profile**
   - Full reconciliation: prescriptions, OTC products, minerals, antacids, herbals.
   - Identify narrow-therapeutic-index medications with interaction danger.
   - Clarify prior intolerance or severe hypersensitivity events.
   - Confirm swallowing ability, emesis burden, or need for non-oral route.
4. **Escalation profile**
   - Define red flags that require urgent clinician reassessment.
   - Pre-plan after-hours contact route and emergency thresholds.
   - Include caregiver observation instructions when cognition is impaired.

### 10.6.3 Contraindication and caution checklist
For COVID and emerging-virus antiviral care, nurses should screen consistently for contraindication domains before first administration:
- pregnancy/teratogenic considerations when regimen-specific
- severe renal impairment without adjusted plan
- severe hepatic impairment when metabolism is liver-dependent
- unresolved interaction conflicts that may produce toxicity or treatment failure
- inability to obtain full course due to access barriers

When any domain is positive, nursing documentation should clearly state the concern, action taken, and clinician notification response time.

### 10.6.4 Dosing logic teaching (non-prescriptive)

- “The dose is chosen for your kidney/liver function so the medicine is strong enough to work and safe enough to tolerate.”

- “Taking doses late or skipping doses can reduce effectiveness.”
- “If vomiting or missed-dose events occur, call us for same-day advice.”

### 10.6.22 Clinical framework: outpatient vs inpatient COVID-19 therapy
COVID-19 management is stratified by:
- Disease severity (mild/moderate vs severe/critical)
- Time since symptom onset
- Patient risk factors for progression
- Immunocompromised status
- Potential drug–drug interactions and organ function

**Nursing triage essentials**
- Document symptom onset date/time.
- Screen for high-risk conditions (age, immunosuppression, chronic disease, pregnancy).
- Obtain medication list focusing on high-interaction drugs.
- Assess renal/hepatic function availability for safe prescribing.

### 10.6.23 Nirmatrelvir/ritonavir (oral antiviral; outpatient use in eligible patients)
Nirmatrelvir inhibits SARS-CoV-2 protease; ritonavir “boosts” levels via CYP inhibition, which drives many drug–drug interactions.

### 10.6.24 Indications (general)
- Mild-to-moderate COVID-19 in high-risk outpatients within a specific time window from symptom onset.

### 10.6.25 Major nursing concern: drug–drug interactions
Ritonavir can significantly alter levels of many medications, potentially causing toxicity or therapeutic failure. Examples of interaction categories include:
- Certain antiarrhythmics
- Certain anticonvulsants
- Anticoagulants/antiplatelets (agent-specific)
- Statins (some are contraindicated; others may need temporary hold)
- Transplant immunosuppressants (high-risk; requires specialist management)

**Nursing actions**
- Perform high-quality medication reconciliation including OTCs and supplements.
- Use institutional interaction-checking resources and escalate complex cases to pharmacy.
- Provide clear written instructions for any temporary holds or dose adjustments (and ensure restart plan is explicit).

### 10.6.26 Renal dosing
Requires renal assessment and dose adjustment; may be contraindicated below certain eGFR thresholds depending on product labeling and guidance.

### 10.6.27 Patient teaching
- Start as early as possible within the recommended window.
- Complete the full course.
- Report severe diarrhea, jaundice, allergic reactions, or worsening symptoms.
- Discuss “rebound” symptom recurrence as a possibility described in clinical practice; provide guidance on isolation and return precautions per current public health recommendations.

Remdesivir is an IV nucleotide analog prodrug with activity against SARS-CoV-2, used in specific outpatient and inpatient contexts depending on severity and timing.

**Nursing considerations**
- IV access planning and infusion monitoring.
- Baseline and follow-up labs (renal and hepatic parameters as ordered).
- Monitor for infusion-related reactions and clinical response.

### 10.6.29 Molnupiravir (oral antiviral; alternative in limited situations)
Molnupiravir may be used when preferred options are not available or appropriate, depending on evolving guideline recommendations.

**Key nursing considerations**
- Reproductive counseling is central due to potential embryo-fetal risk considerations and guideline restrictions (agent- and policy-specific).
- Reinforce use only when clinically indicated and prescribed.

### 10.6.30 Special populations

### 10.6.31 Pregnancy
- Risk of severe viral disease may be higher in pregnancy for some infections; therapy selection must balance maternal benefit and fetal safety.
- Nursing role: confirm gestational age, coordinate obstetric consultation, and provide patient-centered counseling that avoids both minimization and alarmism.

### 10.6.32 Immunocompromised patients
- May have prolonged viral replication and atypical presentations.
- Higher risk for severe disease and for drug–drug interactions (transplant regimens, oncology therapies).
- Nursing role: ensure early escalation and interdisciplinary coordination.

### 10.6.33 Older adults and polypharmacy
- Interaction screening and renal dosing are frequent determinants of safe prescribing.
- Nursing role: verify actual medication use (what the patient truly takes), not just the chart list.

### 10.6.34 Antiviral stewardship and patient education in the era of evolving evidence
COVID-19 and other emerging viral threats highlight a broader principle: antiviral recommendations may change as:
- Variants shift susceptibility patterns
- New trial data emerge
- Real-world safety signals are detected

**Nursing responsibilities**
- Use the most current institutional protocols and authoritative guidance.
- Communicate uncertainty transparently: “Based on current guidance, this is recommended for you because…”
- Encourage vaccination and nonpharmacologic prevention as complementary strategies (where applicable).

**Practical patient teaching checklist**
- When to start (timing window)
- How to take it (with/without food; separation from supplements if relevant)
- What interactions to avoid
- Which symptoms require urgent care
- How follow-up and return-to-work/school guidance will be handled

4. **Toxicity scenario:** new confusion and oliguria. Nursing intervention: urgent escalation, hold-per-order, and repeat renal labs.
5. **Adherence scenario:** repeated missed evening doses. Nursing intervention: schedule redesign to stable morning anchor and reminder support.

### 10.7.19 Overview: why timing matters
Influenza is an acute respiratory viral illness that can cause severe disease (especially in older adults, pregnant patients, immunocompromised patients, and those with chronic cardiopulmonary conditions). Antiviral medications can:

- Shorten symptom duration when started early (generally best within 48 hours of symptom onset)  
- Reduce complications in high-risk patients and those with severe or progressive illness  
- Reduce viral shedding duration (varies by agent and host factors)

**Clinical reality:** Many patients present after 48 hours. Antiviral therapy may still be indicated for:
- Hospitalized patients
- Severe, complicated, or progressive illness
- High-risk patients (even if >48 hours since onset)  
Institutional and public health guidance should be followed.

### 10.7.20 Drug classes used for influenza
Two main mechanistic families are used in contemporary practice:

1. **Neuraminidase inhibitors (NAIs)**  
   - **Oseltamivir** (oral)  
   - **Zanamivir** (inhaled)  
   - **Peramivir** (IV)

2. **Cap-dependent endonuclease inhibitor**  
   - **Baloxavir marboxil** (oral; single-dose)

> **Older agents:** Amantadine and rimantadine (M2 inhibitors) are not routinely recommended in many regions due to widespread resistance in influenza A and lack of activity against influenza B.

### 10.7.21 Oseltamivir
**Mechanism:** Neuraminidase inhibitor → interferes with release of new virions from infected cells.

**Common uses:**
- Treatment of uncomplicated influenza (outpatient)
- Treatment of severe influenza (hospitalized)
- Post-exposure prophylaxis in selected patients/outbreak settings

**Route:** Oral capsule or suspension.

**Key nursing considerations**
- **Start as soon as possible** when influenza is suspected in high-risk patients; do not delay for confirmatory testing if clinical suspicion is high and local guidance supports empiric treatment.
- **Renal dosing:** Requires adjustment in renal impairment. Confirm current creatinine clearance/eGFR and dose per protocol.
- **Adherence:** Usually a multi-day course. Reinforce “keep taking even if you feel better” to prevent rebound symptoms and reduce complications.

**Adverse effects**
- **GI:** Nausea, vomiting (often improved with food).
- **Neuropsychiatric events:** Rare reports (confusion, hallucinations), more often described in pediatric populations; causality is debated. Escalate concerning behaviors or acute mental status changes.
- **Hypersensitivity:** Rash, anaphylaxis (rare).

**Adverse event escalation cues (nursing “stop-and-call” triggers)**
- Signs of anaphylaxis: swelling of lips/tongue, wheeze/stridor, hypotension
- Severe or persistent vomiting → dehydration risk, inability to take oral meds
- New confusion, agitation, hallucinations, seizures
- Worsening respiratory status (increasing work of breathing, cyanosis, inability to speak full sentences)

**Patient education script (plain language)**
> “This medicine works best when started early. Take it exactly as prescribed, even if you start feeling better. If you get bad vomiting, a rash, trouble breathing, or feel suddenly confused, call us right away or seek urgent care.”

---

### 10.7.22 Zanamivir
**Mechanism:** Neuraminidase inhibitor.

**Route:** Inhaled powder.

**Key nursing considerations**
- **Airway risk:** Avoid or use extreme caution in patients with reactive airway disease (asthma/COPD) due to risk of bronchospasm. Ensure rescue bronchodilator access if used.
- **Technique matters:** Proper inhalation technique is essential; poor technique reduces efficacy.

**Adverse effects**
- Cough, throat irritation
- Bronchospasm (can be severe)
- Hypersensitivity reactions (rare)

**Escalation cues**
- Wheezing, chest tightness, shortness of breath after dosing
- Inability to use inhaler device effectively (cognitive/physical limitations)

**Patient education script**
> “Because this is inhaled, it must be used correctly to work. If you have asthma or COPD, let us know—this medicine can sometimes trigger wheezing.”

---

### 10.7.23 Peramivir
**Mechanism:** Neuraminidase inhibitor.

**Route:** IV (typically single dose in uncomplicated cases; may be used in hospitalized patients per protocol).

**Nursing considerations**
- Useful when oral/inhaled administration is not feasible (e.g., vomiting, intubation, inability to swallow).
- Monitor for infusion reactions and renal function (dose adjustments may be needed).

**Adverse effects**
- Diarrhea
- Hypersensitivity (rare)
- Neuropsychiatric events (rare)

---

### 10.7.24 Baloxavir marboxil
**Mechanism:** Inhibits viral cap-dependent endonuclease → blocks viral mRNA synthesis.

**Route:** Oral, **single dose** (weight-based).

**Distinct nursing points**
- **Single-dose adherence advantage** for patients likely to miss multi-day regimens.
- **Drug/food interactions:** Absorption can be reduced by polyvalent cations (e.g., calcium, magnesium, iron, aluminum). Avoid taking with dairy, antacids, or mineral supplements at the same time (follow local guidance for spacing).
- **Resistance:** Viral mutations can emerge; stewardship and guideline-based use matter.

**Adverse effects**
- Generally well tolerated; possible GI upset, headache.

**Patient education script**
> “This is a one-time dose. Don’t take it with milk, calcium, iron, magnesium, or antacids at the same time—those can make it less effective.”

### 10.7.25 Nursing workflow: suspected influenza (outpatient or ED triage)
1. **Assess severity**
   - Vitals, oxygen saturation, respiratory effort
   - Hydration status, mental status
2. **Identify high-risk features** (age, pregnancy, immunocompromise, chronic disease, nursing facility residence)
3. **Determine timing**
   - Symptom onset time
4. **Collect testing as ordered** (rapid molecular/NAAT where available), but **do not delay empiric therapy** for high-risk/severe presentations if recommended locally.
5. **Select therapy** based on:
   - Route feasibility (oral vs inhaled vs IV)
   - Contraindications (reactive airway disease for zanamivir)
   - Renal function (oseltamivir/peramivir adjustments)
6. **Provide self-care education**
   - Hydration, rest, fever control, warning signs
7. **Infection prevention**
   - Masking, hand hygiene, isolation guidance, return-to-work/school guidance per policy

## 10.8 Antivirals for Hepatitis B (HBV)

### 10.8.1 Expanded Clinical Practice Framework: Hbv Suppression Management

### 10.8.2 Bedside risk stratification workflow
Nursing teams should use a repeatable risk workflow that starts with timeline, host vulnerability, organ reserve, and medication complexity. In HBV suppression management, triage errors usually occur when one of these dimensions is skipped.

1. **Timeline precision**
   - Determine exact start of sentinel symptoms, not just presentation day.
   - Ask when function changed (missed work/school, reduced intake, confusion onset).
   - Capture exposure timing and prophylaxis history where relevant.
   - Record previous partial treatment that may alter resistance risk.
2. **Host vulnerability profile**
   - Age extremes, pregnancy/postpartum status, frailty, and chronic diseases.
   - Immune status, transplant history, chemotherapy, steroid burden, or advanced HIV.
   - Baseline renal/hepatic reserve and recent organ-function trends.
   - Social determinants that affect timely treatment completion.
3. **Medication safety profile**
   - Full reconciliation: prescriptions, OTC products, minerals, antacids, herbals.
   - Identify narrow-therapeutic-index medications with interaction danger.
   - Clarify prior intolerance or severe hypersensitivity events.
   - Confirm swallowing ability, emesis burden, or need for non-oral route.
4. **Escalation profile**
   - Define red flags that require urgent clinician reassessment.
   - Pre-plan after-hours contact route and emergency thresholds.
   - Include caregiver observation instructions when cognition is impaired.

### 10.8.3 Contraindication and caution checklist
For HBV suppression management, nurses should screen consistently for contraindication domains before first administration:
- pregnancy/teratogenic considerations when regimen-specific
- severe renal impairment without adjusted plan
- severe hepatic impairment when metabolism is liver-dependent
- unresolved interaction conflicts that may produce toxicity or treatment failure
- inability to obtain full course due to access barriers

When any domain is positive, nursing documentation should clearly state the concern, action taken, and clinician notification response time.

### 10.8.20 Clinical overview
Hepatitis B virus (HBV) can cause acute hepatitis, chronic infection, cirrhosis, and hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC). Antiviral therapy for chronic HBV aims to:

- Suppress HBV DNA replication
- Reduce hepatic inflammation and fibrosis progression
- Reduce risk of cirrhosis decompensation and HCC (risk reduction is incomplete; surveillance may still be needed)

**Key concept:** Most therapy is **long-term**. Stopping HBV antivirals can trigger **HBV flare** (rebound viral replication and hepatic injury), particularly in patients with advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis.

### 10.8.21 Major drug categories for HBV
1. **Nucleos(t)ide analog reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NRTIs for HBV)**  
   Common first-line agents in many guidelines include:
   - **Tenofovir** (formulations differ; see below)
   - **Entecavir**
   Other agents may include lamivudine, adefovir, telbivudine (often limited by resistance/toxicity).

2. **Interferons**
   - **Pegylated interferon alfa** (selected cases; finite course but significant adverse effects)

This section focuses on nucleos(t)ide analogs commonly used and nursing implications.

---

### 10.8.22 Tenofovir (HBV therapy)
**Mechanism:** Nucleotide analog that inhibits HBV polymerase/reverse transcriptase → terminates DNA chain elongation.

**Formulations**
- **Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF)**
- **Tenofovir alafenamide (TAF)**

These are not interchangeable mg-for-mg; they have different dosing and toxicity profiles. TAF is often associated with improved renal and bone safety compared with TDF, but selection depends on patient factors and formulary/guidelines.

**Nursing considerations**
- **Baseline labs:** Renal function (SCr/eGFR), hepatitis panels, HBV DNA, liver enzymes, pregnancy status as appropriate, and bone risk assessment (history of fractures, osteoporosis risk). Local protocol may specify.
- **Renal monitoring:** Tenofovir can affect renal tubular function (more so with TDF). Watch for rising creatinine, proteinuria, phosphate wasting, or symptoms of proximal tubulopathy.
- **Bone health:** Particularly with TDF; monitor risk factors and counsel on bone-friendly behaviors.

**Adverse effects**
- GI upset
- Renal toxicity (especially TDF)
- Reduced bone mineral density (especially TDF)
- Rare lactic acidosis/hepatic steatosis warnings for nucleos(t)ide analogs

**Escalation cues**
- New/worsening fatigue, dyspnea, abdominal pain with vomiting (concern for lactic acidosis—rare; urgent evaluation)
- Marked jaundice, dark urine, confusion (concern for acute liver decompensation)
- Decreased urine output, swelling, significant rise in creatinine

**Patient education script**
> “This medicine controls the virus but usually doesn’t cure it, so it’s important not to stop it on your own. Stopping suddenly can make hepatitis flare and damage the liver. Tell us right away if you notice yellowing of the skin/eyes, severe fatigue, or swelling.”

---

### 10.8.23 Entecavir
**Mechanism:** Guanosine nucleoside analog inhibiting HBV DNA polymerase.

**Nursing considerations**
- **Administration timing:** Often recommended on an empty stomach (per product guidance) to optimize absorption.
- **Renal dosing:** Adjust in renal impairment.
- **Resistance history:** Prior lamivudine exposure can increase resistance risk; provider selection may account for this.

**Adverse effects**
- Headache, fatigue, dizziness, nausea
- Rare lactic acidosis/hepatic steatosis warning

**Escalation cues**
- Symptoms suggesting liver decompensation (confusion/asterixis, ascites, GI bleeding)
- Severe abdominal pain, persistent vomiting
- New jaundice

---

### 10.8.24 Pegylated interferon alfa (selected patients)
**Mechanism:** Immunomodulatory antiviral activity; finite-course option.

**Why it matters in nursing practice**
Interferon therapy has **systemic** adverse effects and requires vigilant screening and monitoring:
- Neuropsychiatric effects (depression, irritability)
- Flu-like symptoms
- Bone marrow suppression (neutropenia, thrombocytopenia)
- Thyroid dysfunction
- Autoimmune exacerbation

**Nursing workflow highlights**
- Screen mental health history; establish baseline mood assessment.
- Monitor CBC, thyroid function, liver enzymes per protocol.
- Teach injection technique if home administration.
- Provide symptom-management guidance (hydration, acetaminophen/NSAID per provider, dosing timing to sleep through peak symptoms if appropriate).

**Escalation cues**
- Suicidal ideation or severe depression
- Fever with neutropenia symptoms
- Easy bruising/bleeding
- Severe fatigue limiting function

---

### 10.8.25 Nursing workflow: chronic HBV medication safety
1. **Baseline assessment**
   - Liver disease severity (signs of cirrhosis/decompensation)
   - Renal function, pregnancy status, comorbidities
2. **Medication reconciliation**
   - Check nephrotoxic meds (NSAIDs, certain antibiotics), supplements
   - Evaluate adherence barriers (cost, language, housing insecurity)
3. **Education**
   - No abrupt discontinuation
   - Avoid alcohol; discuss acetaminophen max daily dose per liver status
   - Vaccination counseling for household contacts (HBV vaccine)
4. **Ongoing monitoring**
   - Labs per protocol (HBV DNA, ALT/AST, renal markers)
   - Assess for signs of hepatic decompensation
5. **Referral coordination**
   - Hepatology/infectious disease follow-up
   - HCC surveillance scheduling when indicated

- educate on flare warning symptoms (jaundice, dark urine, right upper quadrant pain, profound fatigue)

### 10.9.17 Reinfection prevention and stigma-free counseling
- discuss safer injection supplies access and treatment for substance use disorders
- reinforce condom use and STI screening where relevant
- avoid moralizing language; emphasize risk reduction and re-engagement if reinfection occurs

### 10.9.18 Patient script
“Most people can be cured with this course. The key is taking doses every day and finishing the full regimen, then coming back for the cure-confirmation blood test.”

### 10.9.19 Case pearls
**Case: patient misses 4 days due to pharmacy delay**
Immediate team contact is safer than self-adjusting or doubling doses.

**Case: patient reports cure means no liver follow-up needed despite cirrhosis history**
Clarify that cure removes active HCV but not preexisting cirrhosis risk.


### 10.9.20 Clinical overview: cure is the goal
Unlike chronic HBV, chronic hepatitis C (HCV) can often be **cured** with direct-acting antiviral (DAA) regimens. Cure is defined as sustained virologic response (SVR), typically measured by undetectable HCV RNA at a defined time after treatment completion (commonly 12 weeks).

**Why nurses matter:** DAAs are highly effective when taken correctly, but adherence, drug–drug interactions, and follow-up testing determine success.

---

### 10.9.21 Core DAA targets (conceptual)
DAAs inhibit proteins essential to HCV replication:
- **NS3/4A protease inhibitors** (often end in **-previr**)
- **NS5A inhibitors** (often end in **-asvir**)
- **NS5B polymerase inhibitors** (often end in **-buvir**)

Most regimens use **combination therapy** to prevent resistance and improve cure rates.

> **Practice note:** Specific combinations and durations vary by genotype, fibrosis stage, prior treatment, renal function, and drug interactions. Nurses should follow institutional protocols and specialist plans; avoid ad-hoc changes.

---

### 10.9.22 Common nursing priorities for HCV DAAs
### 10.9.23 Confirm baseline status (per protocol)
- HCV RNA, genotype (if required for regimen selection), fibrosis assessment
- Hepatic function panel (ALT/AST, bilirubin, INR, albumin)
- CBC, renal function
- Screen for HBV coinfection (HBsAg, anti-HBc, anti-HBs) due to **risk of HBV reactivation during/after DAA therapy**
- Pregnancy status when relevant (some regimens and adjuncts are contraindicated)

### 10.9.24 Identify drug–drug interactions (high-stakes nursing check)
Many DAAs interact with:
- Acid-reducing agents (PPIs/H2 blockers/antacids) for certain regimens
- Antiarrhythmics (notably amiodarone with some sofosbuvir-containing regimens—serious bradycardia reported)
- Anticonvulsants, rifamycins, certain HIV meds, herbal supplements (e.g., St. John’s wort)
- Statins (myopathy risk with certain combinations)

**Action:** Perform meticulous medication reconciliation including OTCs, supplements, and “vitamins,” then consult interaction resources per protocol.

### 10.9.25 Teach adherence in a way that sticks
DAAs can feel “too easy,” leading patients to underestimate adherence importance.

**Patient education script**
> “These pills can cure hepatitis C, but only if you take them every day. Missing doses gives the virus a chance to survive and become harder to treat. If you miss a dose, call us so we can tell you what to do—don’t double up unless we instruct you.”

### 10.9.26 Follow-up testing and SVR confirmation
Patients may feel “done” once pills end. Nursing follow-up ensures:
- End-of-treatment HCV RNA testing if ordered
- SVR testing at the required timepoint (often 12 weeks post-treatment)
- Counseling on reinfection risk (especially with ongoing exposure risks)

---

### 10.9.27 Adverse effects: what to expect
Most modern DAAs are well tolerated. Common issues may include:
- Fatigue
- Headache
- Nausea
- Insomnia (varies)

When ribavirin is used (less common now), adverse effects can be significant (hemolytic anemia; teratogenicity) and require intensive monitoring. If ribavirin is part of the plan, follow specialist guidance and strict pregnancy prevention counseling.

---

### 10.9.28 HBV reactivation: nursing watch-outs
**Risk:** In patients with current or prior HBV infection, treating HCV with DAAs can be associated with HBV reactivation, which may lead to severe hepatitis.

**Nursing actions**
- Ensure HBV serologies are documented before starting DAAs.
- Verify the plan for HBV monitoring and/or prophylactic HBV therapy if indicated.
- Educate patients to report jaundice, dark urine, severe fatigue, RUQ pain.

**Escalation cues**
- Rapid rise in ALT/AST
- New jaundice, confusion, bleeding, ascites
- Severe abdominal pain with vomiting

---

### 10.9.29 Nursing workflow: HCV DAA start-to-finish (clinic pathway)
1. **Pre-start**
   - Full medication reconciliation + interaction screening
   - Baseline labs and HBV screen
   - Assess barriers: housing stability, food access, insurance, transportation
2. **Start visit**
   - Teach dosing schedule, missed-dose plan, interaction avoidance (acid reducers, supplements)
   - Provide adherence tools: pillbox, phone reminders, linkage to specialty pharmacy
3. **During therapy**
   - Check-in calls: side effects, adherence, refill timing
   - Lab monitoring per protocol
4. **End of therapy**
   - Reinforce: “Treatment completed ≠ cured until follow-up test confirms.”
5. **Post-therapy**
   - SVR testing appointment scheduling
   - Harm reduction/reinfection prevention counseling as appropriate

## 10.10 Antiretroviral Therapy (ART) for HIV

### 10.10.1 Expanded Clinical Practice Framework: Hiv Art Continuity Care

### 10.10.2 Bedside risk stratification workflow
Nursing teams should use a repeatable risk workflow that starts with timeline, host vulnerability, organ reserve, and medication complexity. In HIV ART continuity care, triage errors usually occur when one of these dimensions is skipped.

1. **Timeline precision**
   - Determine exact start of sentinel symptoms, not just presentation day.
   - Ask when function changed (missed work/school, reduced intake, confusion onset).
   - Capture exposure timing and prophylaxis history where relevant.
   - Record previous partial treatment that may alter resistance risk.
2. **Host vulnerability profile**
   - Age extremes, pregnancy/postpartum status, frailty, and chronic diseases.
   - Immune status, transplant history, chemotherapy, steroid burden, or advanced HIV.
   - Baseline renal/hepatic reserve and recent organ-function trends.
   - Social determinants that affect timely treatment completion.
3. **Medication safety profile**
   - Full reconciliation: prescriptions, OTC products, minerals, antacids, herbals.
   - Identify narrow-therapeutic-index medications with interaction danger.
   - Clarify prior intolerance or severe hypersensitivity events.
   - Confirm swallowing ability, emesis burden, or need for non-oral route.
4. **Escalation profile**
   - Define red flags that require urgent clinician reassessment.
   - Pre-plan after-hours contact route and emergency thresholds.
   - Include caregiver observation instructions when cognition is impaired.

### 10.10.3 Contraindication and caution checklist
For HIV ART continuity care, nurses should screen consistently for contraindication domains before first administration:
- pregnancy/teratogenic considerations when regimen-specific
- severe renal impairment without adjusted plan
- severe hepatic impairment when metabolism is liver-dependent
- unresolved interaction conflicts that may produce toxicity or treatment failure
- inability to obtain full course due to access barriers

When any domain is positive, nursing documentation should clearly state the concern, action taken, and clinician notification response time.

### 10.10.4 Dosing logic teaching (non-prescriptive)
A practical patient-facing explanation model:
- “The dose is chosen for your kidney/liver function so the medicine is strong enough to work and safe enough to tolerate.”
- “Taking extra doses does not speed cure and can increase harm.”
- “Taking doses late or skipping doses can reduce effectiveness.”
- “If vomiting or missed-dose events occur, call us for same-day advice.”

Nursing error traps:
- outdated renal function used for current dosing
- confusion between different formulations/prodrugs
- unclear interval instructions in shift-to-shift handoff
- discharge medication list that omits temporary interaction holds

### 10.10.5 Monitoring matrix for first 72 hours, first 2 weeks, and long-term follow-up
**First 72 hours (acute safety):**
- tolerance and administration success
- evolving adverse effects (GI, neurocognitive, rash, hemodynamic changes)
- hydration status and urine output
- early response trend versus deterioration

**First 2 weeks (treatment execution):**
- adherence integrity and refill access
- lab follow-up completion
- dose adjustments after changing renal/hepatic function
- emerging interaction effects after medication additions

**Long-term (when chronic therapy applies):**
- virologic/laboratory response sustainability
- cumulative organ toxicity surveillance
- prevention counseling refresh (vaccines, safer sex, safer injection)
- quality-of-life and mental health support

### 10.10.6 Adverse effect communication model
Use a three-tier script to avoid underreporting and over-alarm.

- **Expected/manageable effects:** mild nausea, headache, transient fatigue; provide self-management guidance.
- **Call same day:** persistent vomiting, severe diarrhea, reduced intake, rash progression, new dizziness.
- **Emergency:** breathing difficulty, chest pain, severe confusion, seizure, anuria/near-anuria, signs of anaphylaxis.

Document patient teach-back: “Patient accurately repeated emergency warning signs and callback process.”

### 10.10.7 Interaction prevention protocol
In HIV ART continuity care, interaction harm can be prevented by making reconciliation operational, not optional.

Protocol elements:
1. one standardized med-list source of truth per encounter
2. mandatory supplement/herbal prompt
3. pharmacy review trigger for high-risk combinations
4. explicit temporary hold/restart instructions in writing
5. callback within 48–72 hours if major medication changes were made

### 10.10.8 Equity and access lens
Clinical efficacy depends on completion feasibility. Nurses should assess:
- transportation reliability and pharmacy distance
- insurance status and prior authorization delays
- housing security for medication storage
- literacy/language barriers requiring translated instructions
- ability to attend laboratory and infusion appointments

Mitigation strategies include mail-order pharmacy, social work referral, interpreter-supported counseling, and simplified reminder systems.

### 10.10.9 Transition-of-care bundle
Before discharge or care transfer in HIV ART continuity care, confirm the following “no-miss” items:
- diagnosis and indication clearly stated
- exact start/stop dates or chronic continuation plan
- dose rationale if adjusted for organ function
- pending labs with owner and due date
- interaction plan and temporary medication holds
- emergency thresholds and contact numbers
- follow-up appointment date/time and transport plan

### 10.10.10 Nursing documentation phrases that improve safety
- “Antiviral start eligible per symptom timeline and risk profile; provider notified at [time].”
- “Comprehensive med reconciliation completed including OTC/supplement/herbal products.”
- “Renal/hepatic function reviewed; dose-verification concern escalated before administration.”
- “Teach-back completed: patient verbalized purpose (long-term suppression reliability, interaction prevention, and whole-person adherence coaching), schedule, and red-flag symptoms.”
- “Discharge handoff includes pending labs, refill logistics, and interaction hold/restart plan.”

### 10.10.11 Case-based micro scenarios
1. **Access barrier scenario:** patient cannot pay at pickup. Nursing intervention: same-day financial navigation and alternative dispensing path.
2. **Cognitive barrier scenario:** patient cannot recall instructions. Nursing intervention: caregiver-inclusive counseling and written pictorial schedule.
3. **Interaction scenario:** patient starts new OTC remedy. Nursing intervention: immediate review and clarified timing/avoidance instructions.
4. **Toxicity scenario:** new confusion and oliguria. Nursing intervention: urgent escalation, hold-per-order, and repeat renal labs.
5. **Adherence scenario:** repeated missed evening doses. Nursing intervention: schedule redesign to stable morning anchor and reminder support.

### 10.10.12 Patient-centered communication examples
- “We are choosing this plan because your risk factors suggest benefit if we act quickly.”
- “Your questions about side effects are important; we will monitor together and adjust if needed.”
- “If cost or transport becomes a problem, tell us immediately so we can keep treatment on track.”
- “This treatment goal is long-term suppression reliability, interaction prevention, and whole-person adherence coaching; today’s plan is one step in that larger goal.”

### 10.10.13 Quality-improvement opportunities for nursing units
Unit-level improvements in HIV ART continuity care can include:
- protocolized symptom-onset documentation fields
- automatic high-risk alerts for renal dosing review
- pharmacist cosign triggers for interaction-heavy regimens
- standard discharge checklist embedded in EHR
- follow-up call programs for high-risk outpatients

These improvements reduce preventable delays, interaction injuries, and readmissions while improving antiviral effectiveness in real-world care.


### 10.10.14 Advanced adherence science in everyday nursing practice
Adherence barriers are rarely “motivation only.” They are usually structural plus emotional plus pharmacologic.

Nursing interventions with high practical impact:
- align dosing with stable daily anchors (wake time, dinner, bedtime)
- use refill synchronization and automatic reminders
- identify side effects early before they become nonadherence drivers
- address stigma and disclosure fears that interfere with routine dosing

### 10.10.15 Lab interpretation support for patients
Nurses can translate labs clearly:
- **viral load** shows current replication activity
- **CD4 count** reflects immune status trend
- improving viral load often precedes robust CD4 recovery

Teach-back prompt:
“Can you tell me what ‘undetectable’ means for your health and prevention plan?”

### 10.10.16 Toxicity and interaction surveillance
- renal effects with tenofovir-containing regimens in susceptible patients
- metabolic changes (weight, lipids, glucose)
- CNS effects with selected agents
- boosted regimen interactions across cardiology, psychiatry, and transplant medications

### 10.10.17 IRIS recognition and nursing escalation
IRIS may appear after ART initiation in patients with advanced immunosuppression and occult infections.

Clues suggesting IRIS rather than pure treatment failure:
- initial virologic improvement with paradoxical inflammatory worsening
- focal inflammatory findings linked to known opportunistic pathogens

Escalate for specialist-guided evaluation; do not independently stop ART unless directed.

### 10.10.18 Long-acting injectable ART workflows
Nursing reliability tasks include:
- appointment cadence protection
- missed-dose contingency pathways
- injection-site counseling
- confidentiality-sensitive scheduling

### 10.10.19 Case pearls
**Case: stable suppression, rising LDL and weight gain**
Nonstigmatizing counseling plus cardiometabolic risk management can preserve adherence without framing ART as “failure.”

**Case: patient with recurrent missed clinic visits**
Outreach that includes transportation and peer-navigation support may be more effective than repeated generic adherence warnings.


### 10.10.20 Overview: ART is lifelong, and adherence is clinical safety
HIV attacks CD4+ T lymphocytes, leading to progressive immune dysfunction if untreated. **Antiretroviral therapy (ART)** suppresses viral replication, allowing immune recovery and preventing progression to AIDS.

Key goals of ART:
- Achieve and maintain viral suppression (undetectable HIV RNA)
- Restore and preserve immune function (increase/maintain CD4 count)
- Reduce HIV-related morbidity and mortality
- Prevent transmission (Undetectable = Untransmittable, U=U, under defined conditions)

**Nursing emphasis:** ART is powerful—but it is also a **high-adherence, high-interaction** medication category. Supportive nursing workflows improve outcomes.

---

### 10.10.21 Core ART drug classes (conceptual)
Most first-line regimens use a combination of agents from different classes:

1. **NRTIs (nucleoside/nucleotide reverse transcriptase inhibitors)**
   - Backbone drugs in many regimens (e.g., tenofovir + emtricitabine; abacavir + lamivudine)

2. **INSTIs (integrase strand transfer inhibitors)**
   - Often first-line due to potency and tolerability (e.g., dolutegravir, bictegravir, raltegravir, elvitegravir)

3. **NNRTIs (non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors)**
   - e.g., efavirenz (older), rilpivirine, doravirine (varies)

4. **Protease inhibitors (PIs)**
   - e.g., darunavir (often boosted)

5. **Entry/fusion/attachment inhibitors and other agents**
   - Used in specific scenarios (resistance, intolerance, advanced disease)

> **Practice note:** Regimens evolve. Nurses should reference current institutional guidelines for preferred combinations and monitoring.

---

### 10.10.22 Nursing priorities at ART initiation
### 10.10.23 Normalize and support rapid start (where applicable)
Many programs emphasize rapid ART start after diagnosis to improve outcomes and reduce transmission, when safe and appropriate. Nurses help by:
- Coordinating labs and follow-up
- Addressing fear, stigma, misinformation
- Ensuring a clear plan for adherence support and side-effect management

### 10.10.24 Baseline assessments (typical categories)
- HIV viral load, CD4 count
- Resistance testing (genotype) when indicated
- Hepatitis coinfection screening (HBV/HCV)
- Renal function, hepatic function
- Pregnancy status and reproductive goals
- STI screening, TB screening as ordered
- Medication reconciliation including OTC/supplements

### 10.10.25 Adherence assessment: make it practical, not moral
Adherence barriers are often logistical:
- Work schedule, shift work
- Housing instability
- Food insecurity (some meds require food)
- Mental health conditions
- Substance use disorder
- Intimate partner violence or privacy concerns

**Nursing actions**
- Use nonjudgmental questions:
  - “What might get in the way of taking a pill every day?”
  - “Do you have a safe place to keep medication?”
- Provide tools:
  - Discreet pill containers, reminder apps, linking to daily routines
  - Pharmacy delivery, synchronized refills
  - Case management referrals

---

### 10.10.26 Key ART adverse effects & monitoring themes
Because ART is class- and drug-specific, nurses often monitor by **pattern**:

### 10.10.27 GI intolerance (early, often transient)
- Nausea, diarrhea can occur during initiation.
- Encourage hydration, small meals, symptom control per protocol.
- Assess for dehydration and weight loss.

### 10.10.28 Neuropsychiatric effects
- Some agents have been associated with insomnia, vivid dreams, mood changes.
- Screen baseline mental health, follow up after initiation.

### 10.10.29 Metabolic and weight changes
- Weight gain has been observed with some modern regimens; causality and mechanisms are complex. Provide supportive counseling and monitor cardiometabolic risk factors without stigmatizing.

### 10.10.30 Renal and bone considerations (tenofovir-containing regimens)
- Monitor renal function; counsel on hydration.
- Consider bone health risk (especially with TDF).

### 10.10.31 Hypersensitivity reactions
- **Abacavir hypersensitivity** is a classic high-stakes example (associated with HLA-B*57:01). Screening is standard where available; do not restart abacavir if hypersensitivity is suspected.
- Teach patients to report rash, fever, malaise, GI symptoms early.

### 10.10.32 Hepatotoxicity
- Monitor LFTs, particularly in hepatitis coinfection or with certain agents.
- Teach warning signs: jaundice, RUQ pain, dark urine, severe fatigue.

---

### 10.10.33 Drug–drug interactions: high-yield nursing checks
ART can interact with many common meds. Frequent interaction hotspots include:
- Acid-reducing agents (affect absorption for certain agents)
- Anticonvulsants (enzyme induction)
- Rifamycins (TB treatment)
- Anticoagulants and antiplatelets
- Statins
- Hormonal contraception (varies by regimen)
- Herbal products (notably St. John’s wort)

**Nursing workflow**
- Reconcile all meds at every visit, including “as needed,” OTC, and supplements.
- Encourage patients: “Call before starting any new medication—even vitamins.”

**Patient education script**
> “HIV meds can interact with common drugs like heartburn medicines, seizure medicines, and supplements. Before you start anything new, check with us—even if it’s over-the-counter.”

---

### 10.10.34 Immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS)
When ART is started in patients with advanced immunosuppression and an underlying opportunistic infection (OI), improving immune function can trigger an inflammatory response that **worsens symptoms** temporarily.

**Nursing significance**
- IRIS can be mistaken for medication failure or new infection.
- Requires careful clinical assessment and coordination with prescribers.

**Escalation cues**
- New fever, worsening respiratory symptoms, neurologic changes after starting ART
- Enlarging lymph nodes, worsening focal symptoms (e.g., headaches, vision changes)
- Rapid clinical deterioration

Management is condition-specific and should follow specialist protocols.

---

### 10.10.35 Long-acting injectable ART (selected patients)
Some ART options are long-acting injectables administered on a schedule (e.g., monthly or every 2 months in some protocols).

**Nursing implications**
- Appointment adherence becomes medication adherence.
- Injection technique, site management, and managing missed appointments are critical.
- Assess for transportation barriers; implement reminders.

**Patient education script**
> “With injections, staying on schedule is the key. If you think you’ll miss an appointment, call as soon as possible so we can reschedule safely.”

---

### 10.10.36 Nursing workflow: ART initiation visit (structured checklist)
1. **Confirm readiness and understanding**
   - “What do you understand about what ART does?”
2. **Assess barriers**
   - Work schedule, food, privacy, mental health, substance use, insurance
3. **Medication reconciliation**
   - OTCs/supplements; interaction screening
4. **Baseline labs**
   - Ensure ordered labs are drawn or scheduled
5. **Education**
   - Dosing time, food requirements, missed-dose instructions
   - Warning signs requiring urgent evaluation
6. **Follow-up plan**
   - Set first follow-up within a defined interval per clinic protocol
7. **Prevention counseling**
   - Vaccinations, safer sex, STI prevention, U=U counseling per policy

### 10.11.21 Nirmatrelvir/ritonavir
**Mechanism:** Nirmatrelvir inhibits viral protease; ritonavir boosts drug levels via CYP3A inhibition.

**Nursing high-yield point: drug interactions**

- Increase levels of certain drugs → toxicity
- Decrease levels of others (complex)

Common interaction categories include:
- Antiarrhythmics
- Anticoagulants
- Antiseizure meds
- Immunosuppressants (e.g., transplant meds)
- Certain statins
- Benzodiazepines
- Certain antipsychotics

**Nursing workflow**
- Perform a “red flag” medication screen:
  - “Do you take heart rhythm medications?”
  - “Any transplant medications?”
  - “Blood thinners?”
  - “Seizure medications?”
  - “Cholesterol medicines?”
- Escalate to pharmacist/prescriber for interaction management **before dispensing**.

**Renal/hepatic considerations**
- Dose adjustments may be needed with renal impairment; severe renal or hepatic impairment may be contraindications per labeling/guidelines.

**Adverse effects**
- Dysgeusia (metallic taste)
- GI upset
- Elevated liver enzymes (rare)
- “Rebound” symptoms have been reported after completion; clinical significance varies and guidance evolves.

**Escalation cues**
- Signs of significant drug toxicity from interactions (e.g., bleeding on anticoagulants, severe sedation)
- Severe allergic reaction
- Worsening hypoxia, chest pain, confusion

**Patient education script**
> “This medication can interact with many common prescriptions. Tell us every medicine you take, including supplements. If you feel worse breathing-wise, develop chest pain, or feel confused, seek urgent care.”

### 10.11.22 Remdesivir
**Mechanism:** Nucleoside analog inhibiting viral RNA-dependent RNA polymerase.

**Setting:** IV; used in some outpatient high-risk pathways (multi-day infusion) and in hospitalized patients per protocols.

**Nursing considerations**
- IV access and infusion monitoring
- Baseline and follow-up labs (liver enzymes; renal function as guided)
- Monitor for infusion reactions

**Adverse effects**
- Elevated transaminases
- Nausea
- Infusion-related reactions (hypotension, nausea, diaphoresis)

**Escalation cues**
- Marked rise in ALT/AST or symptoms of hepatitis (jaundice, RUQ pain)
- Signs of infusion reaction during administration

### 10.11.23 Molnupiravir
**Mechanism:** Nucleoside analog causing lethal mutagenesis in viral RNA replication (conceptual summary).

**Role:** Typically an alternative option when preferred therapies are not appropriate/available.

**Nursing considerations**
- Pregnancy avoidance guidance may apply due to theoretical fetal risk; confirm current recommendations.
- Reinforce adherence and completion of full course.

**Adverse effects**
- Diarrhea, nausea, dizziness (varies)

### 10.12.2 Expanded Clinical Practice Framework: Special-Population Antiviral Safety

### 10.12.3 Bedside risk stratification workflow
Nursing teams should use a repeatable risk workflow that starts with timeline, host vulnerability, organ reserve, and medication complexity. In special-population antiviral safety, triage errors usually occur when one of these dimensions is skipped.

1. **Timeline precision**
   - Determine exact start of sentinel symptoms, not just presentation day.
   - Ask when function changed (missed work/school, reduced intake, confusion onset).
   - Capture exposure timing and prophylaxis history where relevant.
   - Record previous partial treatment that may alter resistance risk.
2. **Host vulnerability profile**
   - Age extremes, pregnancy/postpartum status, frailty, and chronic diseases.
   - Immune status, transplant history, chemotherapy, steroid burden, or advanced HIV.
   - Baseline renal/hepatic reserve and recent organ-function trends.
   - Social determinants that affect timely treatment completion.
3. **Medication safety profile**
   - Full reconciliation: prescriptions, OTC products, minerals, antacids, herbals.
   - Identify narrow-therapeutic-index medications with interaction danger.
   - Clarify prior intolerance or severe hypersensitivity events.
   - Confirm swallowing ability, emesis burden, or need for non-oral route.
4. **Escalation profile**
   - Define red flags that require urgent clinician reassessment.
   - Pre-plan after-hours contact route and emergency thresholds.
   - Include caregiver observation instructions when cognition is impaired.

### 10.12.4 Contraindication and caution checklist
For special-population antiviral safety, nurses should screen consistently for contraindication domains before first administration:
- pregnancy/teratogenic considerations when regimen-specific
- severe renal impairment without adjusted plan
- severe hepatic impairment when metabolism is liver-dependent
- unresolved interaction conflicts that may produce toxicity or treatment failure
- inability to obtain full course due to access barriers

When any domain is positive, nursing documentation should clearly state the concern, action taken, and clinician notification response time.

### 10.12.5 Dosing logic teaching (non-prescriptive)
A practical patient-facing explanation model:
- “The dose is chosen for your kidney/liver function so the medicine is strong enough to work and safe enough to tolerate.”
- “Taking extra doses does not speed cure and can increase harm.”
- “Taking doses late or skipping doses can reduce effectiveness.”
- “If vomiting or missed-dose events occur, call us for same-day advice.”

Nursing error traps:
- outdated renal function used for current dosing
- confusion between different formulations/prodrugs
- unclear interval instructions in shift-to-shift handoff
- discharge medication list that omits temporary interaction holds

### 10.12.6 Monitoring matrix for first 72 hours, first 2 weeks, and long-term follow-up
**First 72 hours (acute safety):**
- tolerance and administration success
- evolving adverse effects (GI, neurocognitive, rash, hemodynamic changes)
- hydration status and urine output
- early response trend versus deterioration

**First 2 weeks (treatment execution):**
- adherence integrity and refill access
- lab follow-up completion
- dose adjustments after changing renal/hepatic function
- emerging interaction effects after medication additions

**Long-term (when chronic therapy applies):**
- virologic/laboratory response sustainability
- cumulative organ toxicity surveillance
- prevention counseling refresh (vaccines, safer sex, safer injection)
- quality-of-life and mental health support

### 10.12.7 Adverse effect communication model
Use a three-tier script to avoid underreporting and over-alarm.

- **Expected/manageable effects:** mild nausea, headache, transient fatigue; provide self-management guidance.
- **Call same day:** persistent vomiting, severe diarrhea, reduced intake, rash progression, new dizziness.
- **Emergency:** breathing difficulty, chest pain, severe confusion, seizure, anuria/near-anuria, signs of anaphylaxis.

Document patient teach-back: “Patient accurately repeated emergency warning signs and callback process.”

### 10.12.8 Interaction prevention protocol
In special-population antiviral safety, interaction harm can be prevented by making reconciliation operational, not optional.

Protocol elements:
1. one standardized med-list source of truth per encounter
2. mandatory supplement/herbal prompt
3. pharmacy review trigger for high-risk combinations
4. explicit temporary hold/restart instructions in writing
5. callback within 48–72 hours if major medication changes were made

### 10.12.9 Equity and access lens
Clinical efficacy depends on completion feasibility. Nurses should assess:
- transportation reliability and pharmacy distance
- insurance status and prior authorization delays
- housing security for medication storage
- literacy/language barriers requiring translated instructions
- ability to attend laboratory and infusion appointments

Mitigation strategies include mail-order pharmacy, social work referral, interpreter-supported counseling, and simplified reminder systems.

### 10.12.10 Transition-of-care bundle
Before discharge or care transfer in special-population antiviral safety, confirm the following “no-miss” items:
- diagnosis and indication clearly stated
- exact start/stop dates or chronic continuation plan
- dose rationale if adjusted for organ function
- pending labs with owner and due date
- interaction plan and temporary medication holds
- emergency thresholds and contact numbers
- follow-up appointment date/time and transport plan

### 10.12.11 Nursing documentation phrases that improve safety
- “Antiviral start eligible per symptom timeline and risk profile; provider notified at [time].”
- “Comprehensive med reconciliation completed including OTC/supplement/herbal products.”
- “Renal/hepatic function reviewed; dose-verification concern escalated before administration.”
- “Teach-back completed: patient verbalized purpose (pregnancy, geriatrics, immunocompromise, and transition-of-care risk control), schedule, and red-flag symptoms.”
- “Discharge handoff includes pending labs, refill logistics, and interaction hold/restart plan.”

### 10.12.12 Case-based micro scenarios
1. **Access barrier scenario:** patient cannot pay at pickup. Nursing intervention: same-day financial navigation and alternative dispensing path.
2. **Cognitive barrier scenario:** patient cannot recall instructions. Nursing intervention: caregiver-inclusive counseling and written pictorial schedule.
3. **Interaction scenario:** patient starts new OTC remedy. Nursing intervention: immediate review and clarified timing/avoidance instructions.
4. **Toxicity scenario:** new confusion and oliguria. Nursing intervention: urgent escalation, hold-per-order, and repeat renal labs.
5. **Adherence scenario:** repeated missed evening doses. Nursing intervention: schedule redesign to stable morning anchor and reminder support.

### 10.12.13 Patient-centered communication examples
- “We are choosing this plan because your risk factors suggest benefit if we act quickly.”
- “Your questions about side effects are important; we will monitor together and adjust if needed.”
- “If cost or transport becomes a problem, tell us immediately so we can keep treatment on track.”
- “This treatment goal is pregnancy, geriatrics, immunocompromise, and transition-of-care risk control; today’s plan is one step in that larger goal.”

### 10.12.14 Quality-improvement opportunities for nursing units
Unit-level improvements in special-population antiviral safety can include:
- protocolized symptom-onset documentation fields
- automatic high-risk alerts for renal dosing review
- pharmacist cosign triggers for interaction-heavy regimens
- standard discharge checklist embedded in EHR
- follow-up call programs for high-risk outpatients

These improvements reduce preventable delays, interaction injuries, and readmissions while improving antiviral effectiveness in real-world care.


### 10.12.15 Geriatric considerations
Older adults face higher adverse-event risk due to physiologic reserve changes, polypharmacy, and cognitive load.

Nursing priorities:
- simplify schedules where possible
- use large-print, plain-language instructions
- involve caregivers with patient consent
- monitor for delirium as an early toxicity signal

### 10.12.16 Pregnancy and lactation considerations
Pregnancy introduces dual-patient risk/benefit analysis. Nursing roles include:
- rapid collaboration across obstetric and infectious disease teams
- clear counseling that avoids undertreatment from fear alone
- medication-specific lactation counseling using current references

### 10.12.17 Immunocompromised hosts
These patients may have prolonged viral shedding and atypical presentation.

Nursing implications:
- lower threshold for early testing/treatment escalation
- prolonged monitoring plans
- infection-prevention reinforcement in home and clinic settings

### 10.12.18 Antiviral stewardship in nursing practice
Stewardship means right patient, right drug, right timing, right duration.

Nurse-enabled stewardship actions:
- confirm indication and timing before first dose
- prevent duplicate or unnecessary antiviral therapy
- flag interaction risks early
- advocate for de-escalation/stop when indication expires
- document adverse events to improve future protocols

### 10.12.19 Safety-event prevention bundle
- standardized renal dose verification
- hard-stop medication reconciliation for herbals/supplements
- discharge checklist requiring follow-up labs before final sign-off
- high-risk counseling script for teratogenic regimens

### 10.12.20 Transition-of-care handoff template
Include:
- diagnosis certainty and date of onset/exposure
- antiviral chosen and rationale
- dose-adjustment rationale
- active interaction plan
- pending labs and ownership
- patient understanding via teach-back outcome

### 10.12.21 Case pearls
**Case: multilingual patient discharged with interaction-sensitive regimen**
Professional interpreter plus translated medication schedule prevents avoidable readmissions.

**Case: homeless patient on chronic suppression therapy**
Linkage to outreach pharmacy services and flexible refill pathways can be as critical as the prescription itself.


### 10.12.22 Why this section exists
Antivirals are often perceived as “safer than antibiotics” because they target viruses and are sometimes short-course. In practice, antivirals can be **high-risk** due to:
- Narrow windows for efficacy (timing-sensitive)
- Renal/hepatic dosing complexity
- Serious drug–drug interactions (notably with ART and ritonavir-boosted therapies)
- Resistance risk with incomplete adherence
- Special population considerations (pregnancy, pediatrics, geriatrics, immunocompromise)

This section consolidates nursing safety practices that apply across antiviral categories.

---

### 10.12.23 Pregnancy and lactation considerations (general nursing approach)
Because pregnancy safety data vary widely by drug and indication, nurses should:
- **Avoid assumptions.** “Antiviral” does not automatically mean safe or unsafe.
- Confirm pregnancy status when clinically relevant and appropriate.
- Use current guidelines for the specific infection (e.g., influenza in pregnancy often has strong treatment recommendations; some COVID and HIV therapies have specific preferred regimens).
- Coordinate with obstetrics and pharmacy when there is uncertainty.

**Education themes**
- Risks of untreated infection to pregnant patient and fetus
- Importance of adherence
- Reporting symptoms such as decreased fetal movement, persistent high fever, or dehydration

---

### 10.12.24 Pediatrics: dosing, formulations, and family education
**Nursing priorities**
- Weight-based dosing accuracy; verify units and concentration (especially suspensions).
- Teach caregivers:
  - How to measure liquid doses (oral syringe preferred)
  - Missed-dose plan
  - Storage requirements
- Monitor for dehydration with influenza/COVID due to vomiting/poor intake.

**Family education script**
> “Use the dosing syringe, not a kitchen spoon. If your child can’t keep fluids down, has trouble breathing, turns blue, or becomes unusually sleepy or confused, go to urgent care or the ER.”

---

### 10.12.25 Older adults: polypharmacy and renal function
Older adults are more likely to:
- Have renal impairment (requiring dose adjustment)
- Take interacting medications
- Experience severe disease

**Nursing workflow**
- Prioritize medication reconciliation and interaction screening.
- Confirm renal dosing and recent labs.
- Assess fall risk if dizziness occurs.

---

### 10.12.26 Immunocompromised patients: prolonged shedding and atypical presentations
In immunocompromised patients (transplant, oncology, advanced HIV, long-term steroids):
- Viral shedding may be prolonged
- Disease may be more severe
- Resistance may emerge with partial suppression

**Nursing actions**
- Early escalation for worsening symptoms even if mild initially
- Reinforce strict adherence
- Ensure follow-up labs and specialist involvement

---

### 10.12.27 Renal and hepatic impairment: “two-lab rule” mindset
Many antivirals require adjustment by renal function and/or caution in hepatic impairment.

**Practical nursing practice**
- Do not rely on “normal creatinine” alone—use eGFR/CrCl.
- Confirm:
  - **Renal function** for dosing
  - **Liver enzymes and synthetic function** (INR, albumin) if relevant for hepatic impairment severity
- Encourage patients to avoid nephrotoxins and dehydration during illness (within clinical appropriateness).

---

### 10.12.28 Adherence and resistance: teach “why,” not just “how”
Resistance can develop when drug pressure is inconsistent (missed doses, incomplete courses, drug interactions lowering levels).

**High-impact education phrasing**
- “Viruses learn. Every missed dose gives the virus practice.”
- “Call us before you stop—stopping suddenly can be dangerous for some infections (like hepatitis B and HIV).”

---

### 10.12.29 Antiviral stewardship: appropriate use and equity
Stewardship is not only about preventing resistance; it is also about:
- Getting the right antiviral to the right patient within the right time window
- Avoiding unnecessary therapy when risk is low
- Reducing harm from interactions and mis-dosing
- Ensuring access (insurance navigation, pharmacy coordination, language services)

**Nursing contributions**
- Screening for eligibility quickly (timing + risk)
- Medication reconciliation
- Patient education for adherence and warning signs
- Follow-up coordination to verify cure/suppression (HCV SVR, HIV viral load, HBV DNA)

---

### 10.12.30 “Call the provider now” red flags (cross-cutting antiviral safety)
Escalate urgently for:
- New or worsening hypoxia, chest pain, severe shortness of breath
- Altered mental status, seizures, or severe confusion
- Signs of severe allergic reaction
- Persistent vomiting with inability to hydrate
- Marked jaundice, dark urine, confusion (possible acute liver injury/decompensation)
- Significant bleeding or bruising (possible drug interaction/toxicity)
- Severe rash with systemic symptoms (fever, mucosal involvement)

## 10.13 Review Questions (10.7–10.12)

### 10.13.1 Suggested answer rationales and teaching points
> These rationales are written for nursing education review and should be aligned to local policy and current guidelines.

**1. Why can delayed antiviral initiation reduce benefit in acute respiratory viruses?**  
Because replication is highest early in illness for many respiratory viruses. Antiviral effect size is usually greatest before host inflammatory injury dominates. Nursing implication: accurate symptom-onset history is a treatment determinant.

**2. Why is renal function central to many antiviral dosing plans?**  
Many antivirals are renally cleared; reduced clearance can increase toxicity risk (neurotoxicity, nephrotoxicity, GI intolerance), while inappropriate underdosing risks treatment failure and resistance.

**3. Why must medication reconciliation include supplements and herbals?**  
Polyvalent cations can reduce absorption of some antivirals, and herbals such as St. John’s wort can alter metabolism and lower exposure for interaction-sensitive regimens.

**4. Why might treatment still be reasonable in severe influenza after 48 hours?**  
In severe or high-risk presentations, guideline-based recommendations often support treatment despite delayed presentation because potential reduction in complications may still be clinically meaningful.

**5. How should nurses explain suppression versus cure?**  
Suppression means controlling viral replication long term (e.g., HIV/HBV). Cure means sustained eradication marker after treatment (e.g., HCV SVR). Counseling should align expectations to the actual therapeutic goal.

**6. What is a practical way to distinguish possible IRIS from pure treatment failure?**  
IRIS often appears with improving HIV virologic markers but paradoxical inflammatory worsening of occult/known opportunistic processes after ART initiation.

**7. Why are care transitions high-risk in antiviral therapy?**  
Errors occur with omitted interaction holds, unclear stop dates, missing follow-up labs, or unresolved access barriers. Structured handoff checklists reduce these failures.

**8. What makes post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) time-critical?**  
Viral establishment can occur rapidly after exposure; earlier initiation is associated with better preventive potential. Nursing triage speed directly influences eligibility and outcome.

**9. Why do nurses monitor both efficacy and tolerability simultaneously?**  
A regimen cannot succeed if it is not tolerated or completed. Early symptom management preserves adherence while maintaining therapeutic effect.

**10. Why should stigma-sensitive communication be considered a safety intervention?**  
Shame and fear decrease disclosure and adherence, leading to missed doses, delayed care, and poor outcomes. Respectful communication improves engagement and continuity.

## 10.14 Key Takeaways (10.7–10.12)

### 10.14.1 High-reliability nursing checklist (concise practice tool)
Use this checklist across antiviral encounters:

1. **Confirm indication and syndrome fit**
   - Does the clinical picture match the target virus?
   - Is empiric treatment supported by risk profile and local guidance?

2. **Capture timing accurately**
   - First symptom time/date
   - Exposure time/date when relevant
   - Prior doses or partial outside treatment

3. **Assess host risk and organ reserve**
   - age, pregnancy, immunocompromise, major comorbidities
   - renal/hepatic function and recent trends

4. **Complete full medication reconciliation**
   - prescriptions, OTC, antacids, minerals, herbals
   - identify high-risk interaction pairs and escalate early

5. **Verify dose logic and route feasibility**
   - renal/hepatic adjustments as ordered
   - oral tolerance versus need for IV/inhaled options

6. **Deliver clear patient education with teach-back**
   - treatment goal: cure, suppression, or prevention
   - dosing schedule and missed-dose plan
   - common effects versus urgent red flags

7. **Plan monitoring proactively**
   - labs due and owner identified
   - follow-up timeframe scheduled before discharge

8. **Harden transitions of care**
   - stop dates, hold/restart interaction plans
   - pharmacy access and insurance barriers resolved
   - emergency thresholds and contact route documented

9. **Use stigma-sensitive communication**
   - factual, nonjudgmental language
   - confidentiality-aware counseling
   - reinforce that help is available for adherence barriers

10. **Practice stewardship**
    - right patient, right time, right agent, right duration
    - discontinue when indication expires and document rationale

### 10.14.2 Final integrative summary
Across influenza, herpesviruses, hepatitis viruses, HIV, and COVID-19, antiviral success is rarely determined by the prescription alone. Outcomes depend on whether the care team converts pharmacology into dependable real-world execution. Nurses make that conversion possible by obtaining accurate timelines, recognizing high-risk phenotypes, preventing interaction-related harm, tracking organ-function changes, and translating complex plans into practical daily routines patients can follow.

A high-performing antiviral nursing practice is therefore:
- **rapid** (time-sensitive triage and initiation),
- **precise** (dose and interaction safety),
- **longitudinal** (follow-up and adherence durability),
- **equitable** (access barriers actively addressed), and
- **human-centered** (stigma-sensitive counseling and trust-building).

When these elements are present, antiviral therapy is safer, more effective, and more likely to achieve its intended goals of cure, suppression, or prevention.


### 10.14.3 Expanded high-yield summary
- Antiviral nursing care is **clock-aware** (timing), **organ-aware** (renal/hepatic function), and **interaction-aware** (polypharmacy reality).
- For acute respiratory viruses, early treatment decisions are often made before perfect certainty; high-quality nursing triage improves appropriateness.
- In chronic viral infections, adherence durability and follow-up reliability are clinical outcomes, not administrative details.
- Medication reconciliation must explicitly include nonprescription products, minerals, and herbals.
- Teach-back is essential: patients should be able to explain purpose (cure vs suppression vs prevention), schedule, red flags, and follow-up labs.
- Safety failures commonly occur at transitions of care; structured antiviral discharge checklists prevent avoidable harm.
- Stigma-sensitive communication is not optional in HIV, HSV, HBV, and HCV care; trust supports adherence.
- Stewardship is a nursing activity: confirm indication, timing, dose adjustment, and stop criteria.


- **Influenza antivirals are time-sensitive.** Start treatment as early as possible—especially in high-risk and severely ill patients—often without waiting for confirmatory testing when guidelines support empiric therapy.

- **Route and patient comorbidities matter.** Inhaled zanamivir can precipitate bronchospasm; IV peramivir/remdesivir can be options when oral therapy is not feasible.

- **HBV therapy is typically long-term.** Nucleos(t)ide analogs suppress replication but stopping abruptly can trigger dangerous **HBV flares**. Patient teaching should emphasize not discontinuing without medical guidance.

- **HCV DAAs can cure hepatitis C, but adherence and follow-up are nonnegotiable.** Nurses play a central role in interaction screening, adherence coaching, and ensuring SVR testing is completed.

- **HIV ART is lifelong and highly effective when taken consistently.** Nursing care focuses on medication reconciliation, barrier assessment, stigma-sensitive adherence support, and early recognition of adverse effects and IRIS.

- **COVID antivirals require rapid triage and interaction checks.** Nirmatrelvir/ritonavir has major drug–drug interaction risks; nurses should treat medication reconciliation as a patient safety intervention.

- **Special populations need structured safety monitoring.** Pregnancy, pediatrics, older adults, renal/hepatic impairment, and immunocompromise require tailored dosing, counseling, and escalation thresholds.

- **Escalation cues should be explicit.** Teach patients to seek urgent care for worsening breathing, chest pain, confusion, anaphylaxis symptoms, inability to hydrate, severe rash, or signs of liver injury.

- **Antiviral stewardship is nursing work too.** The right patient, right drug, right time window, and right monitoring plan prevents harm and improves outcomes.
