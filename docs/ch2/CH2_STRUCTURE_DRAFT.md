## LEARNING OBJECTIVES

By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

1. **Define** the four core processes of pharmacokinetics (absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion) and explain their clinical significance in drug efficacy and safety.

2. **Analyze** how route of administration (oral, IV, IM, topical, inhalation, sublingual, rectal) affects drug absorption and bioavailability.

3. **Evaluate** the factors that influence drug distribution (protein binding, lipid solubility, blood-brain barrier, placental transfer) and predict potential drug localization.

4. **Describe** hepatic metabolism (Phase I, II, III reactions) and explain the clinical implications of CYP450 enzyme interactions.

5. **Compare** different renal elimination pathways and assess when dose adjustments are necessary based on kidney function.

6. **Determine** steady-state concentration, half-life, and loading doses—and apply these concepts to clinical dosing decisions.

7. **Assess** how patient factors (age, weight, genetics, disease state, organ function) alter pharmacokinetics and require dose individualization.

8. **Apply** pharmacokinetic principles to real-world clinical scenarios involving drug interactions, contraindications, and patient safety.

## KEY TAKEAWAYS (5–10 Bullets)

- **ADME is the foundation:** Pharmacokinetics encompasses four sequential processes that determine drug concentration at the site of action and elimination.
  
- **Bioavailability varies by route:** IV administration = 100% bioavailability; oral administration typically = 30–80% due to first-pass metabolism and GI absorption variability.

- **Protein binding matters:** Only unbound (free) drug produces therapeutic and toxic effects; high protein-binding drugs have more drug interactions.

- **The liver is the main metabolizer:** Phase I (oxidation), Phase II (conjugation), and Phase III (transport) processes convert lipophilic drugs to water-soluble forms for excretion.

- **Half-life determines dosing intervals:** Understanding half-life predicts steady-state concentration and guides dosing frequency—too frequent = toxicity; too infrequent = subtherapeutic levels.

- **Renal function is critical in older adults:** Reduced GlFR in elderly patients increases drug accumulation; regular dose adjustments prevent toxicity.

- **Drug interactions are pharmacokinetic events:** CYP450 enzyme induction (increases metabolism) or inhibition (decreases metabolism) can render a drug ineffective or toxic.

- **Genetics influence drug metabolism:** Pharmacogenomics explains why some patients are "slow" or "fast" metabolizers—affecting safety and efficacy.

- **First-pass metabolism can eliminate drugs:** Oral drugs undergo hepatic metabolism before reaching systemic circulation; some drugs (e.g., nitroglycerin) bypass this via sublingual/topical routes.

### **2.2 ABSORPTION: GETTING THE DRUG INTO THE BODY** ⏱️ 50 minutes

**Section Objectives:**
- Explain the mechanisms of drug absorption across biological membranes
- Compare bioavailability across different routes of administration
- Evaluate how GI factors (pH, food, motility) affect oral absorption
- Analyze first-pass metabolism and its clinical implications

**Content Focus:**
- Passive diffusion, active transport, and carrier-mediated transport
- Routes of administration and their bioavailability:
  - **IV (Intravenous):** 100%, immediate onset, irreversible
  - **IM (Intramuscular):** 50–90%, depot formations, variable absorption
  - **Oral (PO):** 20–90%, first-pass metabolism, affected by food/pH
  - **Sublingual/Buccal:** Bypasses first-pass, rapid onset, good for emergency drugs
  - **Transdermal:** Steady-state levels, minimizes GI variability
  - **Inhalation:** Rapid onset, local/systemic effects
  - **Rectal:** Variable, used when PO unavailable
- First-pass metabolism (hepatic and GI wall)
- Factors affecting absorption: pH, food, motility, disease states (diarrhea, malabsorption), drug formulation (extended-release vs. immediate-release)

**Clinical Scenario Example:**
Mrs. Johnson takes her metformin with a high-fat meal, and her blood glucose control deteriorates. Investigation reveals fat delays metformin absorption. Timing with food is adjusted, resolving the issue.

**Safety Callout:**
⚠️ **Exam Alert:** Sublingual nitroglycerin works because it bypasses first-pass metabolism and reaches systemic circulation rapidly via oral mucosa blood vessels.

### **2.3 DISTRIBUTION: WHERE THE DRUG GOES IN THE BODY** ⏱️ 55 minutes

**Section Objectives:**
- Explain the determinants of drug distribution (blood flow, tissue affinity, protein binding)
- Evaluate protein binding and its impact on drug efficacy and interactions
- Describe special barriers to distribution (blood-brain barrier, placental transfer)
- Assess tissue reservoir effects and redistribution

**Content Focus:**
- Volume of distribution (Vd) concept: "virtual space" the drug occupies
  - Small Vd = concentrated in plasma (e.g., warfarin, highly protein-bound)
  - Large Vd = distributed to tissues (e.g., chloroquine, lipophilic drugs)
- Protein binding (albumin, alpha-1 acid glycoprotein):
  - Free (unbound) drug = active form
  - Bound drug = inactive reservoir
  - Displacement interactions (warfarin + NSAIDs)
- Lipophilicity vs. hydrophilicity:
  - Lipophilic drugs penetrate cell membranes and tissues; accumulate in fatty tissues
  - Hydrophilic drugs stay in plasma and extracellular fluid
- Special barriers:
  - **Blood-brain barrier (BBB):** Tight junctions, p-glycoprotein efflux pump; blocks many drugs
  - **Placental transfer:** Most drugs cross; risk to fetus varies by gestational age and drug properties
  - **Breast milk:** Lipophilic drugs accumulate; affects breastfeeding mothers
- Tissue localization and reservoir effects (e.g., digoxin in skeletal muscle, tetracycline in bone)

**Clinical Scenario Example:**
A pregnant patient with epilepsy takes phenytoin. The nurse reviews: phenytoin is lipophilic and crosses the placenta, carrying risk of fetal hydantoin syndrome. Therapeutic drug monitoring ensures adequate control while minimizing fetal exposure.

**Safety Callout:**
⚠️ **Medication Safety Alert:** Highly protein-bound drugs (warfarin, phenytoin) are vulnerable to displacement interactions. When adding NSAIDs or other protein-binding drugs, monitor for increased free drug concentration and toxicity.

### **2.4 METABOLISM: HOW THE BODY BREAKS DOWN DRUGS** ⏱️ 60 minutes

**Section Objectives:**
- Explain hepatic metabolism phases (I, II, III) and their biochemical mechanisms
- Identify major CYP450 enzymes and their clinical significance
- Evaluate induction and inhibition of metabolic enzymes
- Assess genetic and non-genetic factors affecting metabolism
- Analyze pharmacokinetic drug interactions at the metabolic level

**Content Focus:**
- **Phase I Metabolism (Oxidation, Reduction, Hydrolysis):**
  - Primary site: Liver (microsomes, smooth endoplasmic reticulum)
  - Main enzyme family: Cytochrome P450 (CYP450)
  - Key isoforms: CYP3A4 (most common, ~50% of drugs), CYP2D6, CYP2C9, CYP1A2, CYP2B6
  - Goal: Add functional groups or expose hidden groups to facilitate conjugation
  - Products: Often more active initially, then conjugated

- **Phase II Metabolism (Conjugation):**
  - Types: Glucuronidation (most common), sulfation, acetylation, methylation
  - Location: Liver, kidneys, GI tract, other tissues
  - Goal: Increase water solubility for renal excretion
  - Products: Usually inactive

- **Phase III Metabolism (Transporter-Mediated):**
  - Active transport proteins: MDR1 (P-glycoprotein), OATP, OCT
  - Function: Actively pump drugs out of hepatocytes or into biliary/renal excretion
  - Clinical relevance: Drug-transporter interactions

- **CYP450 Enzyme Induction & Inhibition:**
  - **Induction:** Increases metabolism, reduces drug levels → subtherapeutic
    - Common inducers: Rifampin, phenytoin, St. John's Wort, carbamazepine
    - Clinical example: Oral contraceptive + rifampin = increased metabolism → contraception failure
  
  - **Inhibition:** Decreases metabolism, increases drug levels → toxicity
    - Common inhibitors: Ketoconazole, grapefruit juice, erythromycin, ritonavir
    - Clinical example: Warfarin + fluconazole = decreased metabolism → bleeding risk

- **Pharmacogenomics:**
  - Genetic variation in CYP450 genes
  - Poor metabolizers: Accumulate drugs, increased toxicity (e.g., codeine poor metabolizers don't get pain relief)
  - Ultra-rapid metabolizers: Require higher doses (e.g., some patients need more warfarin)
  - Testing available (GeneSight, Promethease) for personalized dosing

- **Non-hepatic Metabolism:**
  - GI wall metabolism (some drugs)
  - Blood esterases (succinylcholine)
  - Kidney metabolism (some drugs)

**Clinical Scenario Example:**
Mr. Patel, on warfarin for atrial fibrillation, is prescribed ketoconazole for a fungal infection. The pharmacist alerts: ketoconazole inhibits CYP3A4 and CYP2C9, both involved in warfarin metabolism. Warfarin levels rise, increasing bleeding risk. INR is monitored closely; warfarin dose is reduced.

**Safety Callouts:**
⚠️ **Drug Interaction Alert:** Grapefruit juice inhibits CYP3A4. Patients on statins, beta-blockers, or immunosuppressants should avoid grapefruit to prevent toxicity.

⚠️ **Exam Alert:** Rifampin is a potent CYP450 inducer. It reduces levels of oral contraceptives, warfarin, and many other drugs—backup contraception is advised.

### **2.5 EXCRETION: HOW THE BODY ELIMINATES DRUGS** ⏱️ 50 minutes

**Section Objectives:**
- Explain renal excretion pathways (glomerular filtration, active secretion, reabsorption)
- Compare renal vs. biliary/fecal excretion
- Assess how renal function affects drug elimination
- Evaluate dose adjustments based on creatinine clearance
- Explain why elderly patients are at high risk for drug accumulation

**Content Focus:**
- **Renal Excretion (90% of drugs):**
  - **Glomerular Filtration:** Unbound (free) drugs filtered based on molecular weight
    - Only drugs <65 kDa typically filtered; protein-bound drugs retained
  - **Active Secretion:** Active transport of drugs into renal tubules (organic anion/cation transporters)
    - Competes with other drugs (e.g., penicillin + probenecid)
    - Saturable process → drug interactions
  - **Passive Reabsorption:** Lipophilic drugs reabsorbed in distal tubules
    - Influenced by urinary pH (acidic urine traps weak bases; alkaline urine traps weak acids)
    - Clinical application: Alkalinizing urine increases aspirin excretion (overdose treatment)

- **Biliary/Hepatic Excretion:**
  - Drugs with molecular weight >300 Daltons preferentially excreted in bile
  - Enterohepatic circulation: Drug excreted in bile, reabsorbed in small intestine → recirculates
  - Clinical implication: Reduced bile flow (liver disease, biliary obstruction) → drug accumulation

- **Pulmonary Excretion:**
  - Volatile drugs (alcohol, anesthetics) exhaled via lungs
  - Minor route for most drugs

- **Renal Function Assessment:**
  - Creatinine Clearance (CrCl): Most practical measure for dose adjustments
    - Cockcroft-Gault equation: CrCl = (140 − age) × weight(kg) × (0.85 if female) / (72 × Scr)
    - Normal: >90 mL/min
    - Mild impairment: 60–89 mL/min
    - Moderate impairment: 30–59 mL/min
    - Severe impairment: <30 mL/min
  - eGFR (estimated glomerular filtration rate): Newer, more accurate estimate
  - Importance in aging: Elderly patients often have reduced GFR without elevated serum creatinine

- **Dose Adjustment Strategies:**
  - Reduce dose, extend interval, or both
  - Use drug handbooks and pharmacokinetic equations
  - Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM) for drugs with narrow therapeutic windows

**Clinical Scenario Example:**
An 85-year-old patient with normal serum creatinine (1.0 mg/dL) receives digoxin. Creatinine-based dosing appears safe, but when actual CrCl is calculated (using Cockcroft-Gault), it's only 45 mL/min due to age-related loss of muscle mass. Digoxin accumulates → toxicity (arrhythmias, nausea). CrCl calculation prevents this error.

**Safety Callout:**
⚠️ **Critical Alert:** Serum creatinine is NOT a reliable indicator of renal function in elderly patients. Always calculate creatinine clearance or eGFR before dosing renally-cleared drugs (aminoglycosides, digoxin, many others).

### **2.6 HALF-LIFE, STEADY STATE, AND CLEARANCE** ⏱️ 55 minutes

**Section Objectives:**
- Define and calculate half-life and its relationship to steady state
- Determine time to steady state and accumulation ratio
- Explain clearance and its relationship to half-life
- Calculate loading doses and maintenance doses
- Assess how alterations in clearance affect steady-state concentration

**Content Focus:**
- **Half-Life (t½):**
  - Definition: Time required for plasma concentration to reduce by 50%
  - Calculation: t½ = 0.693 / ke (elimination rate constant) or t½ = 0.693 × Vd / CL
  - Independent of dose: Same half-life whether taking 1 mg or 100 mg (first-order kinetics)
  - Clinical significance: Determines dosing intervals
  - Examples:
    - Penicillin: ~1 hour (dosing Q4–6H)
    - Warfarin: ~40 hours (dosing once daily)
    - Digoxin: ~36–48 hours (dosing once daily; accumulates with frequent dosing)

- **Steady State (Css):**
  - Definition: Plasma concentration reached when drug elimination equals drug input
  - Time to reach: ~5 half-lives (e.g., warfarin takes 5 × 40 hours = 8 days)
  - Clinical implication: Initial therapeutic effect may take days; loading dose may be needed
  - Accumulation: With repeated dosing, drug levels rise until steady state
  - Accumulation ratio = 1 / (1 − e^−ke×τ) where τ = dosing interval

- **Clearance (CL):**
  - Definition: Volume of plasma completely cleared of drug per unit time (mL/min or L/hr)
  - Relationship: CL = Vd × ke; CL = Dose / (AUC)
  - Total clearance = Hepatic CL + Renal CL + Other CL
  - Directly affects steady-state concentration: Css = (Dose/τ) / CL
  - Altered in disease states (liver disease ↓ hepatic CL; kidney disease ↓ renal CL)

- **Bioavailability (F):**
  - Definition: Fraction of administered dose reaching systemic circulation
  - IV = 100%; oral typically 20–90%
  - Accounts for first-pass metabolism and absorption variability

- **Dosing Calculations:**
  - **Loading Dose (LD):** LD = (Css × Vd) / F
    - Achieves therapeutic effect rapidly
    - Used when rapid onset is needed (digoxin, warfarin loading)
  - **Maintenance Dose (MD):** MD = (Css × CL × τ) / F
    - Sustains steady-state level
    - Calculated based on clearance and desired concentration

- **Practical Application:**
  - Scenario: Warfarin dosing
    - Desired Css = 2–3 (INR therapeutic range)
    - Typical maintenance = 5 mg daily
    - But clearance varies (CYP2C9 genetics, interactions)
    - INR monitored to titrate dose
    - Reaches steady state in ~8 days (5 half-lives)

**Clinical Scenario Example:**
A patient starts digoxin for heart failure. The rapid-acting IV loading dose (500 mcg) achieves therapeutic levels immediately, then daily maintenance doses (250 mcg) sustain the level. Without loading dose, the patient would need 5 days to reach therapeutic concentration—unacceptable for acute heart failure. The nurse explains the rationale to the patient, ensuring compliance.

**Safety Callout:**
⚠️ **Dosing Alert:** Drugs with long half-lives (warfarin, methotrexate, digoxin) accumulate with overdosing. A patient who doubles the dose by accident may not develop toxicity immediately—but toxicity will develop over days as drug accumulates. Educate patients about dosing accuracy.

### **2.7 SPECIAL POPULATIONS: PHARMACOKINETICS IN CHILDREN, ELDERLY, PREGNANCY, AND RENAL/HEPATIC DISEASE** ⏱️ 60 minutes

**Section Objectives:**
- Evaluate how age (neonates, children, elderly) affects pharmacokinetics
- Assess pharmacokinetic changes in pregnancy and their implications for fetal risk
- Analyze pharmacokinetic alterations in liver disease and renal disease
- Determine when dose adjustments and increased monitoring are necessary
- Apply pharmacokinetic principles to individualize therapy for vulnerable populations

**Content Focus:**
- **Pediatric Pharmacokinetics:**
  - Neonates (0–30 days):
    - Immature liver metabolism (CYP450 enzymes underdeveloped)
    - Reduced renal function (GFR = 20–30% of adults)
    - Higher body water content (60–70% vs. 55–60% in adults)
    - Larger Vd for water-soluble drugs
    - Lower protein binding (bilirubin displaces drugs)
    - Dosing: Based on gestational age, not weight alone
    - Examples: Avoid sulfonamides (kernicterus from bilirubin displacement); reduce phenytoin dose

  - Infants/Children (1 month–12 years):
    - CYP450 maturation by ~3–4 years, faster metabolism thereafter
    - Higher metabolic rate than adults (mg/kg basis)
    - May require higher mg/kg doses
    - Renal maturation by ~12 months (GFR approaches adult levels)

  - Adolescents: Approach adult pharmacokinetics by ~13 years

  - Practical: Weight-based dosing (mg/kg), organ function assessment, therapeutic drug monitoring for narrow-window drugs

- **Elderly Pharmacokinetics:**
  - Absorption: Reduced GI blood flow, higher gastric pH, reduced GI motility → variable absorption
  - Distribution: Increased body fat (↑ Vd for lipophilic drugs); decreased body water (↓ Vd for water-soluble drugs); reduced serum albumin (↑ free drug concentration)
  - Metabolism: 30–50% reduction in hepatic metabolism due to reduced liver mass and enzyme activity; CYP450 variability increases
  - Excretion: GFR declines 1% per year after age 30; by age 85, GFR may be 50% of normal despite normal creatinine
  - Net effect: Prolonged drug half-lives, increased risk of accumulation and toxicity
  - Clinical practice: Start low, go slow; monitor closely; use GFR (not creatinine) for dosing; reduce dose by 25–50%
  - Polypharmacy increases interaction risk

- **Pregnancy Pharmacokinetics:**
  - Absorption: Nausea/vomiting may reduce bioavailability; GI motility reduced; gastric pH increased
  - Distribution: Increased blood volume (40–50%), increased Vd for water-soluble drugs, decreased plasma albumin
  - Metabolism: Increased hepatic metabolism (CYP3A4, 2D6, 2C9 induction), shortened half-lives of many drugs
    - Some drugs require dose increases to maintain levels
  - Excretion: Increased GFR (40–50%), increased renal excretion
  - Placental Transfer: Most drugs cross; risk depends on molecular weight, lipophilicity, protein binding, gestational age
  - FDA Pregnancy Categories (historically A, B, C, D, X; now replaced by Pregnancy and Lactation Labeling Rule):
    - Category A: Safe (prenatal vitamins)
    - Category B: Likely safe; animal studies safe, limited human data (acetaminophen, penicillin)
    - Category C: Use if benefit outweighs risk; limited data (many drugs)
    - Category D: Evidence of fetal risk but benefit may justify use (phenytoin for seizure control)
    - Category X: Contraindicated (isotretinoin, warfarin first trimester, ACE inhibitors)
  - Clinical practice: Minimize drug exposure, use lowest effective dose, avoid teratogens, monitor therapeutic response
  - Breastfeeding: Most drugs appear in breast milk; lipophilic drugs accumulate; monitor infant

- **Hepatic Disease Pharmacokinetics:**
  - Absorption: Portal hypertension causes shunting of blood (bypassing hepatic metabolism)
  - Distribution: Reduced protein synthesis (↓ albumin), ascites increases Vd
  - Metabolism: Significantly reduced hepatic metabolism; CYP450 enzymes are impaired
  - Excretion: May be impaired if also hepatorenal syndrome
  - Clinical implication: Drugs metabolized by liver accumulate → toxicity; reduce dose by 25–75%
  - Examples: Warfarin, acetaminophen, benzodiazepines, antiarrhythmics
  - Drugs to avoid: Hepatotoxic drugs (NSAIDs, some antibiotics)
  - Monitor: Coagulation times (INR), albumin, bilirubin, mental status (encephalopathy)

- **Renal Disease Pharmacokinetics:**
  - Absorption: Usually unaffected
  - Distribution: Uremia affects protein binding (reduced albumin, uremic toxins displace drugs)
  - Metabolism: Reduced for renally-excreted metabolites (active metabolites accumulate)
  - Excretion: Reduced GFR (most impacted)
  - Clinical implication: Renally-cleared drugs accumulate → toxicity
  - Strategies: Reduce dose, extend dosing interval, or both (see CrCl-based dosing guidelines)
  - Examples: Aminoglycosides (nephrotoxic), ACE inhibitors (contraindicated with severe hyperkalemia), NSAIDs (avoid in kidney disease)
  - Dialysis: Removes some drugs (depends on molecular weight, protein binding); timing of dosing relative to dialysis important

**Clinical Scenario Example:**
An 78-year-old female (weight 55 kg, creatinine 1.1 mg/dL) is prescribed gentamicin for urosepsis. Creatinine appears normal, but her calculated CrCl is 28 mL/min (severe impairment). Standard dosing would cause nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity. Extended-interval dosing (higher dose less frequently) is used instead, with plasma concentration monitoring. This exemplifies why age, weight, and renal function must guide dosing.

**Safety Callouts:**
⚠️ **Geriatric Pharmacology Alert:** Beers Criteria lists drugs to avoid in older adults due to pharmacokinetic changes and increased risk of adverse effects (benzodiazepines, anticholinergics, NSAIDs). Deprescribing (reducing unnecessary medications) is often warranted.

⚠️ **Pregnancy Alert:** Warfarin causes fetal bleeding and skeletal abnormalities in the first trimester; heparin (doesn't cross placenta) is preferred for anticoagulation in pregnancy.

### **2.8 DRUG-DRUG INTERACTIONS AT THE PHARMACOKINETIC LEVEL** ⏱️ 55 minutes

**Section Objectives:**
- Classify pharmacokinetic drug interactions (absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion)
- Predict interactions based on drug properties and mechanisms
- Evaluate clinical significance and manage common interactions
- Apply screening strategies to identify potential interactions
- Explain why some interactions contraindicate drugs while others require monitoring

**Content Focus:**
- **Absorption Interactions:**
  - Chelation: Tetracycline + calcium (forms chelate complex → reduced tetracycline absorption)
  - GI pH alteration: H2-blockers, PPIs ↓ absorption of ketoconazole (needs acidic pH), levothyroxine (needs specific pH)
  - GI motility: Anticholinergics slow motility → increased absorption of some drugs; metoclopramide speeds motility → decreased absorption of some
  - Food interactions: Fat-soluble drugs require fat for absorption (take with food); others absorbed better on empty stomach
  - Antacids: Bind many drugs, reducing absorption

- **Distribution Interactions:**
  - Protein binding displacement: High protein-binding drugs (warfarin 99%, phenytoin 90%) displaced by other drugs
    - Clinical example: Warfarin + NSAIDs → ↑ free warfarin → ↑ INR → bleeding risk
  - Competition for transporters: P-glycoprotein substrates (digoxin, verapamil) compete for active transport
  - Tissue competition: Drugs competing for storage sites (e.g., digoxin stored in muscle; aminoglycosides reduce storage)

- **Metabolism Interactions (CYP450):**
  - Enzyme induction (↓ drug levels → therapeutic failure):
    - Inducers: Rifampin, phenytoin, carbamazepine, phenobarbital, St. John's Wort
    - Substrates affected: Oral contraceptives (failure), warfarin (need ↑ dose), theophylline, some statins
    - Time course: 1–2 weeks to reach maximum induction
  
  - Enzyme inhibition (↑ drug levels → toxicity):
    - Inhibitors: Ketoconazole, macrolide antibiotics (erythromycin), fluconazole, ritonavir, grapefruit juice
    - Substrates affected: Statins (myopathy), calcium channel blockers (hypotension), immunosuppressants (toxicity)
    - Time course: Hours to days depending on inhibitor potency

  - Specific CYP450 interactions:
    - CYP3A4 (major): Statins, macrolides, antifungals, protease inhibitors
    - CYP2D6: Codeine (prodrug, needs CYP2D6 activation), antiarrhythmics, some antidepressants
    - CYP2C9: Warfarin (major), NSAIDs, some sulfonylureas
    - CYP2C19: Clopidogrel (prodrug), omeprazole, some antidepressants

  - Phase II interactions: Less common but important (e.g., methotrexate + NSAIDs both compete for renal secretion)

- **Excretion Interactions:**
  - Renal competition: Penicillin + probenecid (probenecid blocks secretion → ↑ penicillin levels, allowing less frequent dosing)
  - Alkalinization/acidification of urine: Altering pH changes reabsorption of weak acids/bases
    - Example: Alkalinizing urine (sodium bicarbonate) speeds salicylate (aspirin) excretion (used in overdose)

- **Clinical Assessment & Management:**
  - Severity: Contraindicated (avoid completely), significant (requires dose adjustment or monitoring), minor
  - Timing: Some interactions are immediate; others develop over days (enzyme induction)
  - Screening tools: UpToDate, Lexicomp, Micromedex drug interaction checkers
  - Strategies:
    - Choose alternative drug without interaction
    - Adjust dose based on predicted changes in clearance
    - Monitor therapeutic response or serum levels
    - Separate administration time (if absorption interaction)
    - Educate patient (e.g., warfarin + NSAIDs: why combination is risky)

**Clinical Scenario Example:**
Mr. Jackson starts simvastatin (CYP3A4 substrate) for cholesterol. He then develops a fungal infection and is prescribed ketoconazole (CYP3A4 inhibitor). Simvastatin levels rise 15-fold → muscle pain (myopathy) and rhabdomyolysis risk. The pharmacist recommends stopping simvastatin during ketoconazole therapy or switching to pravastatin (not metabolized by CYP3A4).

**Safety Callouts:**
⚠️ **Critical Interaction:** Warfarin + NSAIDs is contraindicated (↑ INR + antiplatelet effect = bleeding). Acetaminophen is the safer alternative.

⚠️ **Drug Interaction Alert:** Grapefruit juice inhibits CYP3A4 for 24+ hours. Patients on statins, calcium channel blockers, or immunosuppressants should avoid grapefruit completely.

- Comparison chart: Common pharmacokinetic interactions

### **2.9 PHARMACOGENOMICS: GENETIC VARIATION AND PERSONALIZED DOSING** ⏱️ 50 minutes

**Section Objectives:**
- Define pharmacogenomics and explain its clinical relevance
- Identify major genetic polymorphisms affecting drug metabolism and response
- Describe how genetic testing guides personalized dosing
- Evaluate ethical and practical considerations in pharmacogenomic testing
- Apply pharmacogenomic principles to prevent adverse effects and optimize therapy

**Content Focus:**
- **Pharmacogenomics Defined:**
  - Science studying how genetic variation affects drug response
  - Combines pharmacology + genomics
  - Goal: Predict which patients will benefit from a drug and which are at risk for adverse effects
  - Personalized medicine: Dosing and drug selection based on individual genetics

- **Major Genetic Polymorphisms:**
  - **CYP2D6:**
    - Highly variable gene (~100 variants identified)
    - Phenotypes:
      - Poor metabolizers (PM): ~7–10% of Caucasians; slow metabolism → drug accumulation, toxicity
        - Example: Codeine is inactive in poor metabolizers (needs CYP2D6 to convert to morphine)
        - Risk: Opioid-naive poor metabolizers on standard codeine dose → respiratory depression risk
      - Extensive metabolizers (EM): Standard metabolism
      - Ultra-rapid metabolizers (UM): ~1–5% of some populations; very fast metabolism → subtherapeutic levels
        - Example: Some UM patients require 2–3× standard warfarin dose
    - Affected drugs: Codeine, tramadol, some antidepressants (fluoxetine, paroxetine), antiarrhythmics (flecainide), beta-blockers (metoprolol)

  - **CYP2C19:**
    - Phenotypes: Poor, intermediate, extensive, rapid metabolizers
    - Key drug: Clopidogrel (Plavix)
      - Prodrug: Needs CYP2C19 activation
      - Poor metabolizers: ↓ activation → ↓ antiplatelet effect → increased stent thrombosis risk
      - FDA black box warning: CYP2C19 poor metabolizers should use alternative P2Y12 inhibitor (prasugrel, ticagrelor) instead of clopidogrel
    - Other drugs: Omeprazole, some antidepressants (sertraline, escitalopram), antiepileptics

  - **CYP2C9:**
    - Variants: *1/*1 (wild-type), *1/*2, *1/*3, *2/*2, *2/*3, *3/*3
    - Affected drug: Warfarin (major substrate)
      - *1/*1: Standard warfarin dosing (5–7 mg daily typical)
      - *1/*2 or *1/*3: Require lower warfarin doses (3–4 mg daily)
      - *2/*3 or *3/*3: Require much lower doses (2–3 mg daily) and achieve INR faster
    - Genotype-guided warfarin dosing: FDA approves CYP2C9 + VKORC1 testing to guide initial dosing
    - Reduction in adverse events (bleeding, thromboembolism) with genotype-guided approach

  - **VKORC1:**
    - Vitamin K oxidoreductase complex subunit 1; target of warfarin
    - Variants affect warfarin sensitivity
    - Patients with -1639G>A polymorphism require lower warfarin doses
    - Used in combination with CYP2C9 genotyping for warfarin dosing

  - **TPMT (Thiopurine S-methyltransferase):**
    - Metabolizes thiopurine drugs (mercaptopurine, azathioprine)
    - Variant phenotypes:
      - Deficient (~0.3%): Risk of severe myelosuppression on standard doses
      - Intermediate (~11%): Reduced metabolism; require dose reduction
      - Normal (~89%): Standard dosing
    - Clinical practice: TPMT testing recommended before starting thiopurines in cancer/immunosuppressive therapy

  - **SLCO1B1:**
    - Affects statin metabolism and transport
    - *521C>T variant increases myopathy risk with simvastatin
    - FDA recommends genetic testing to determine simvastatin dose or use alternative

  - **HLA-B*5701:**
    - Risk of severe hypersensitivity reaction to abacavir (antiretroviral)
    - FDA recommends HLA-B*5701 screening before starting abacavir
    - Reduces hypersensitivity reactions from 8% to <2%

  - **NAT2 (N-Acetyltransferase 2):**
    - Acetylates drugs (isoniazid, sulfonamides)
    - Phenotypes: Slow acetylators (risk of isoniazid peripheral neuropathy), fast acetylators (reduced drug levels)

- **Pharmacogenomic Testing:**
  - Common tests: GeneSight (Myriad), Genomind, Tempus, etc.
  - Timing: Before starting drug (prospective) vs. after adverse event (reactive)
  - Cost: $1,000–3,000; increasingly covered by insurance
  - Clinical utility: Strongest evidence for warfarin, clopidogrel, thiopurines, simvastatin; growing evidence for many others
  - Limitations: Many genes involved; environmental factors also matter; gene-gene interactions complex

- **Ethical & Practical Considerations:**
  - Patient privacy: Genetic testing results may reveal unintended information
  - Equity: Testing access varies by socioeconomic status; underrepresentation of non-European ancestry in genetics research
  - Clinical implementation: Not all healthcare systems have pharmacogenomic-guided dosing protocols
  - Cost-benefit analysis: Testing may be cost-effective for frequently-used drugs (warfarin) or high-risk populations

**Clinical Scenario Example:**
A 35-year-old woman of African descent is prescribed clopidogrel after cardiac stent placement. Genetic testing reveals she is a CYP2C19 poor metabolizer. Switching to prasugrel (metabolized differently) provides better antiplatelet effect and reduces stent thrombosis risk compared to standard clopidogrel therapy.

**Safety Callout:**
⚠️ **Exam Alert & Practice Point:** Always ask about genetic ancestry and check pharmacogenomic testing recommendations—especially for warfarin, clopidogrel, and codeine. A "standard dose" may be dangerous or ineffective based on genotype.

### **2.10 CLINICAL APPLICATIONS & THERAPEUTIC DRUG MONITORING (TDM)** ⏱️ 55 minutes

**Section Objectives:**
- Explain when therapeutic drug monitoring is indicated
- Interpret serum drug levels and adjust doses appropriately
- Apply pharmacokinetic principles to real-world clinical decision-making
- Develop a plan for safe, effective drug therapy based on patient-specific factors
- Communicate with the healthcare team regarding pharmacokinetic considerations

**Content Focus:**
- **Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM) Indications:**
  - Narrow therapeutic window: Small difference between therapeutic and toxic levels
    - Examples: Digoxin (therapeutic 0.5–2 ng/mL; toxic >2 ng/mL), theophylline, aminoglycosides, phenytoin, warfarin (INR), lithium
  - Unpredictable absorption: Phenytoin, some anticonvulsants (saturable metabolism)
  - Patient factors affecting levels: Renal/hepatic disease, drug interactions, age, weight
  - Poor adherence: Identifying whether patient is actually taking medication
  - Optimization: Ensuring dose achieves steady state without toxicity

- **Sampling Timing:**
  - **Peak level:** 1–2 hours after IV dose; 1–3 hours after oral dose (depends on route and drug)
    - Indicates maximum concentration
    - Used to assess absorption and ensure therapeutic effect
  - **Trough level:** Just before next dose
    - Indicates minimum concentration
    - Used to assess accumulation and risk of subtherapeutic levels
  - **Steady state:** After 5 half-lives (allows time for drug to plateau)
  - Clinical example: Aminoglycosides require peak and trough levels (peak for efficacy, trough to avoid nephrotoxicity)

- **Common Drugs Requiring TDM:**
  - **Digoxin:** Therapeutic 0.5–2 ng/mL; narrow window; increased risk in older adults and renal disease
  - **Aminoglycosides:** Peak/trough monitoring to ensure efficacy and prevent toxicity; adjusted for renal function
  - **Vancomycin:** Trough monitoring (15–20 mcg/mL); adjusted in renal disease
  - **Phenytoin:** Therapeutic 10–20 mcg/mL; protein binding variability
  - **Theophylline:** Therapeutic 10–20 mcg/mL; narrow window; food and drug interactions
  - **Warfarin:** INR (not serum level); target varies by indication (2–3 for atrial fibrillation)
  - **Lithium:** Therapeutic 0.6–1.2 mEq/L; narrow window; affected by salt intake, renal function

- **Interpreting TDM Results:**
  - High levels (above therapeutic range):
    - Assess dose, interval, kidney/liver function
    - Symptoms of toxicity?
    - Drug interactions?
    - Reduced clearance?
    - Action: Reduce dose, extend interval, or both; monitor for toxicity; recheck level in 3–5 days (or after 5 half-lives of new regimen)

  - Low levels (below therapeutic range):
    - Assess adherence: Is patient taking the medication?
    - Increased metabolism? (inducers, fast metabolizer phenotype)
    - Increased elimination? (renal disease—wait, that ↑ levels!)
    - Malabsorption? (GI disease, food interactions)
    - Action: Increase dose, check adherence, assess for interactions; recheck in 5 half-lives

  - At therapeutic range but no effect:
    - Is the diagnosis correct?
    - Is the drug given at sufficient concentration at the target site? (e.g., poor CNS penetration)
    - Is timing appropriate? (allow time for steady state)
    - Is there an underlying issue being missed?

- **Case-Based Clinical Decision-Making:**
  - Scenario 1: Elderly patient on digoxin develops nausea/arrhythmias
    - Assess: Serum digoxin level (likely elevated due to reduced renal clearance)
    - Symptoms suggest digoxin toxicity
    - Action: Check level, reduce dose, monitor K+ (hypokalemia worsens digoxin toxicity), consider digoxin-specific antibody (Digibind) if severe
  
  - Scenario 2: Hypertensive patient on warfarin with INR 6.0
    - Assess: Is patient on interacting drug (aspirin, NSAID)? Any medication changes?
    - Symptoms: Any bleeding?
    - Action: If minor elevation, reduce dose; recheck in 3–4 days; educate about avoiding NSAIDs
    - If INR >9 with bleeding: Vitamin K + FFP or prothrombin complex concentrate

  - Scenario 3: Diabetic patient on vancomycin for MRSA pneumonia; renal function worsens (Cr ↑ from 1.0 to 2.0)
    - Assess: Clearance reduced; standard dosing will cause accumulation
    - Action: Obtain trough level; increase dosing interval or reduce dose based on CrCl; monitor SCr and vancomycin levels
    - Vancomycin is nephrotoxic; monitor urine output, BUN/Cr trends

- **Adherence Assessment:**
  - Direct: Ask patient about medication taking (though patients may overreport adherence)
  - Indirect: Refill frequency, serum drug levels, disease control
  - Barriers: Cost, side effects, complex regimens, lack of understanding, depression
  - Interventions: Simplify regimen (once-daily dosing), educate, pill organizers, phone reminders, address side effects

**Clinical Scenario Example:**
Mr. Kim, 72 years old, admitted with atrial fibrillation. Started on digoxin 250 mcg daily and lisinopril. After 5 days, he reports dizziness, nausea, and "yellow-green halos" around lights (classic digoxin toxicity symptoms). Digoxin level = 3.2 ng/mL (toxic). K+ = 3.1 mEq/L (low—worsens toxicity). Actions: Stop digoxin; give potassium supplement; check EKG for arrhythmias; repeat digoxin level in 24 hours; restart at lower dose (187.5 mcg daily) after confirming level has fallen. This case exemplifies why close monitoring is essential in older adults.

**Safety Callouts:**
⚠️ **Critical Safety Point:** Digoxin toxicity is life-threatening. Symptoms overlap with heart failure (dyspnea, weakness) but require opposite treatment—so TDM and clinical correlation are essential.

⚠️ **Warfarin Monitoring:** INR should be monitored regularly. Fluctuations indicate need for dose adjustment. Educate patients about consistent vitamin K intake (doesn't mean avoiding it—means consistent amounts).
