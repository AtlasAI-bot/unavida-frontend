## 1. Chapter Identity & Learning Design

### Chapter Overview
Chapter 2 is foundational to all pharmacology practice. It explains the four phases of drug movement through the body (ADME: Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion) and provides the scientific rationale for dosing adjustments in special populations (elderly, renal/hepatic impairment, pregnancy/lactation, pediatric). Nurses must understand pharmacokinetics to predict drug behavior, prevent toxicity, and educate patients on medication timing and administration.

### Learning Objectives (Target: 5-8 Measurable)
By completion, students will:
1. **Define and explain** the four phases of pharmacokinetics (ADME)
2. **Identify factors** that affect drug absorption across different routes (oral, IV, IM, subcutaneous, topical, inhalation, rectal)
3. **Describe** how drugs distribute to tissues, plasma protein binding, and the blood-brain barrier's role
4. **Explain** drug metabolism (Phase I, II, III reactions) and the liver's primary role
5. **Calculate** drug elimination and half-life, understanding steady-state and accumulation
6. **Apply** pharmacokinetic principles to nursing decisions: dose adjustments in renal/hepatic impairment, elderly patients, and special populations
7. **Analyze** how drug-drug interactions affect pharmacokinetics (enzyme induction/inhibition)

### Key Takeaways (5-10 concise bullets)
- ADME (Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion) is the framework for understanding how drugs move through the body
- Drug absorption varies by route; IV provides 100% bioavailability, but oral is most common and affected by GI factors
- Plasma protein binding determines free (active) drug; high protein-binding drugs compete for binding sites
- First-pass metabolism (hepatic) can drastically reduce drug bioavailability; prodrugs rely on this activation
- Half-life predicts steady-state achievement (~5 half-lives) and guides dosing interval selection
- Elderly patients have decreased liver/renal function → lower doses often needed; start low, go slow
- Renal function (CrCl, eGFR) determines renally-eliminated drug dosing; hepatic impairment affects metabolized drugs
- Enzyme induction (rifampin, phenytoin) increases drug metabolism → lower plasma levels; inhibition (ketoconazole, grapefruit juice) increases levels
- Pregnancy/lactation alter pharmacokinetics; some drugs are teratogenic or concentrated in breast milk

### Clinical Competencies (3-5 applied)
1. **Assess patient factors** (age, renal function, liver function, genetics) and predict drug response
2. **Adjust dosing** based on renal impairment using CrCl formulas
3. **Counsel patients** on medication timing relative to food, other drugs, and missed doses
4. **Recognize toxicity signs** when steady state is reached or in overdose scenarios
5. **Communicate** with healthcare team about contraindicated drug combinations due to metabolism interactions

| 2.7 | Half-Life, Steady-State, & Drug Accumulation | 1200-1400 | • Definition of half-life (t½) • Calculation of half-life from elimination rate • Steady-state achievement (~5 half-lives) • Time to steady-state table • Drug accumulation curves • Loading doses vs. maintenance doses • Therapeutic drug levels | Digoxin: narrow therapeutic window; warfarin: INR takes days to stabilize |
| 2.8 | Pharmacokinetics in Special Populations | 1200-1400 | • Elderly (decreased liver/renal function, body composition changes) • Pediatric (immature metabolism, different dosing formulas: BSA, weight-based) • Pregnancy (increased volume, altered metabolism, placental transfer) • Lactation (milk as fat-soluble drug reservoir) • Hepatic impairment (reduced metabolism, portal hypertension effects) • Renal impairment (reduced excretion, accumulation, toxicity) • Genetic polymorphisms (CYP2D6, CYP2C9, TPMT) | Start low, go slow in elderly; avoid ACE-I in pregnancy; renally dose gentamicin |
| 2.9 | Enzyme Induction, Inhibition, & Drug-Drug Interactions | 1000-1200 | • CYP450 enzyme induction (rifampin, phenytoin, phenobarbital, St. John's Wort) • Effects: increased drug metabolism → lower plasma levels • CYP450 enzyme inhibition (ketoconazole, grapefruit juice, cimetidine, ritonavir) • Effects: decreased drug metabolism → higher plasma levels, toxicity risk • Timing: induction takes days/weeks; inhibition immediate • Major substrates, inducers, inhibitors table | Rifampin reduces oral contraceptive efficacy; grapefruit juice ↑ statin levels |
| 2.10 | Bioavailability, Clearance, & Volume of Distribution | 1000-1200 | • Bioavailability (F) = fraction reaching systemic circulation • Factors: route, first-pass effect, formulation • Clearance (Cl) = volume of plasma cleared of drug per time unit • Renal clearance vs. hepatic clearance vs. total body clearance • Volume of distribution (Vd) = theoretical volume drug would occupy at plasma concentration • Lipophilic drugs (large Vd); hydrophilic drugs (small Vd) | Why lipophilic amiodarone distributes throughout tissues |
| 2.11 | Clinical Application: Dosing Adjustments & Monitoring | 1200-1400 | • CrCl calculation (Cockcroft-Gault, MDRD, CKD-EPI) • Renal dosing guidelines • Hepatic dosing (no universal formula, clinical judgment) • Monitoring strategies (serum drug levels, therapeutic drug monitoring) • Labs to assess organ function before/during therapy • Signs of accumulation and toxicity | Gentamicin peak/trough levels; INR monitoring for warfarin |
| 2.12 | Pharmacokinetic Modeling & Practical Examples | 800-1000 | • Single-compartment vs. multi-compartment models • Zero-order vs. first-order kinetics • Practical case studies: loading a patient on digoxin; achieving stable warfarin levels | Complex dosing scenarios; why washout periods needed when switching drugs |
