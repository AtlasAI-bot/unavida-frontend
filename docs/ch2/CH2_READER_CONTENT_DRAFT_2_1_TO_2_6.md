## 2.1 OVERVIEW: PHARMACOKINETICS IN CONTEXT

### What Is Pharmacokinetics?

When you give a patient a medication, your job as a nurse extends far beyond handing them the pill or starting the IV. You need to understand what happens *after* the drug enters the body. **Pharmacokinetics** is the science that explains exactly that: *what the body does to the drug*.

This differs from **pharmacodynamics**, which you'll explore in Chapter 3. While pharmacokinetics is about the drug's *journey* through the body, pharmacodynamics is about the drug's *effect* once it reaches its target. Both are essential to safe, effective medication administration.

### The ADME Framework: The Drug's Four-Stage Journey

All pharmacokinetic processes can be organized into four sequential stages, known by the acronym **ADME**:

1. **Absorption** — Getting the drug into the body
2. **Distribution** — Where the drug goes and how it reaches target tissues
3. **Metabolism** — How the body chemically transforms the drug
4. **Excretion** — How the body eliminates the drug and its metabolites

Think of ADME as a pipeline. A drug enters through one route (absorption), travels to different body compartments (distribution), undergoes chemical transformation (metabolism), and finally exits the body (excretion). Understanding each stage helps you predict how a drug will behave in your patient and anticipate potential problems.

### Why Pharmacokinetic Variability Matters Clinically

Not all patients respond the same way to the same dose of medication. A 5 mg dose of metoprolol might effectively lower one patient's heart rate but have minimal effect on another. Why? Because pharmacokinetic processes vary from person to person based on:

- **Age** (neonates, children, and elderly have very different ADME)
- **Weight and body composition** (affects distribution)
- **Kidney and liver function** (affects metabolism and excretion)
- **Genetics** (influences drug-metabolizing enzymes)
- **Drug interactions** (other medications alter ADME)
- **Disease states** (liver disease, kidney disease, heart failure affect drug handling)

As a nurse, you must recognize these patient-specific factors and advocate for dose adjustments or closer monitoring when needed.

### The Therapeutic Window: The "Goldilocks Zone" of Drug Dosing

Every drug has a range of plasma concentrations where it is therapeutic (effective) without being toxic (harmful). This range is called the **therapeutic window**.

- **Below the therapeutic window:** The drug concentration is too low. The patient receives no clinical benefit. We call this a **subtherapeutic** dose.
- **Within the therapeutic window:** The drug concentration is right. The patient experiences the desired therapeutic effect with minimal adverse effects.
- **Above the therapeutic window:** The drug concentration is too high. Toxicity develops. This is a **toxic** dose.

The width of this window varies dramatically by drug:

- **Wide therapeutic window** (e.g., penicillin): A large margin between effective and toxic doses. Safe to use with less frequent monitoring.
- **Narrow therapeutic window** (e.g., digoxin, warfarin, aminoglycosides): A small margin between effective and toxic doses. Requires close monitoring, accurate dosing, and sometimes therapeutic drug level measurement.

**Clinical Example:** A patient with heart failure is prescribed digoxin for rate control and increased contractility. Digoxin has a very narrow therapeutic window:
- Therapeutic level: 0.5–2 ng/mL
- Toxic level: >2 ng/mL

A patient serum digoxin level of 1.5 ng/mL is safe and effective. But if the level rises to 2.5 ng/mL (just a 0.5 ng/mL increase), toxicity develops: nausea, vomiting, visual disturbances (yellow-green halos), and dangerous arrhythmias. As a nurse, you monitor for these toxicity symptoms and recognize that a small dose increase can shift a patient from safe to dangerous.

### Pharmacokinetic Parameters: Speaking the Language

Healthcare providers use specific terms to describe how drugs move through the body. As a nurse, you don't need to calculate these, but you should understand them because you'll see them in patient charts and hear them in clinical discussions:

- **Half-life (t½):** The time it takes for the plasma drug concentration to drop by half. Example: If digoxin has a half-life of 40 hours, and a patient's level is 2 ng/mL today, it will be 1 ng/mL in 40 hours without additional doses.
- **Clearance (CL):** The volume of plasma completely cleared of drug per unit time (e.g., mL/min or L/hr). Affected by kidney and liver function.
- **Volume of Distribution (Vd):** A theoretical concept representing the space the drug occupies in the body. A small Vd means the drug stays mostly in the blood; a large Vd means it distributes widely to tissues.
- **Bioavailability (F):** The fraction of an administered dose that reaches systemic circulation. IV drugs have 100% bioavailability; oral drugs typically 20–90% due to absorption and first-pass metabolism.

### Time-to-Peak, Duration of Action, and Cumulative Effects

Understanding the *timing* of drug action is essential for patient education and safety:

- **Time-to-peak:** How long it takes for drug plasma concentration to reach its maximum after administration. Varies by route and drug. Example: IV penicillin peaks in 30 minutes; oral metformin peaks in 2–3 hours.
- **Duration of action:** How long the drug exerts its therapeutic effect. Often depends on how long the concentration stays above the minimum effective level.
- **Cumulative effects:** Drugs with long half-lives accumulate with repeated dosing. If you dose too frequently, drug levels rise above the therapeutic window → toxicity. This is especially dangerous with digoxin, warfarin, and methotrexate.

**Patient Education Implication:** If a patient misses a dose of a long-acting drug (like digoxin or warfarin) and then takes a double dose to "catch up," they risk toxicity. Teach patients to take the next scheduled dose without doubling, and to contact their healthcare provider for specific guidance.

### Steady State: When Drug Input Equals Drug Output

With repeated dosing, the body eventually reaches a balance where the amount of drug being absorbed and given equals the amount being eliminated. This is called **steady state** (or **steady-state concentration**, abbreviated Css).

- **At steady state:** Drug levels plateau. The peak and trough concentrations become predictable and consistent with each dose.
- **Time to reach:** Approximately **5 half-lives** of the drug.

**Example:** Warfarin has a half-life of ~40 hours. So steady state is reached in about 5 × 40 = 200 hours, or roughly 8–9 days. If a patient starts warfarin on Monday, therapeutic INR (the measure of warfarin effect) likely won't be reached until the following Wednesday. This is why warfarin dosing requires patience and serial INR monitoring rather than a "one-and-done" approach.

**Clinical Implication for Nursing:** When a patient is started on a drug with a long half-life, don't expect immediate therapeutic effect. Educate the patient about the timeline. If rapid therapeutic effect is needed (e.g., digoxin for acute heart failure), a **loading dose** is given first to quickly achieve therapeutic concentration, then smaller **maintenance doses** sustain it.

### Section Recap

Pharmacokinetics is the foundation of safe medication administration. The **ADME framework** (absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion) organizes how drugs move through the body. Recognizing **variability** in pharmacokinetics among patients helps you anticipate when dosing adjustments or closer monitoring are needed. Understanding the **therapeutic window** for your patient's drugs ensures you dose appropriately and recognize toxicity early. Finally, grasping concepts like **half-life**, **steady state**, and **duration of action** enables you to educate patients and collaborate effectively with the healthcare team.

## 2.2 ABSORPTION: GETTING THE DRUG INTO THE BODY

### What Is Absorption?

**Absorption** is the process by which a drug moves from its site of administration into the bloodstream. The route of administration—oral (by mouth), intravenous (IV), intramuscular (IM), topical, inhalation, and others—dramatically affects how much drug reaches the systemic circulation and how quickly it gets there.

### Routes of Administration and Bioavailability

Different routes produce vastly different bioavailabilities. Here's a comparison of the main routes:

#### **1. Intravenous (IV)**
- **Route:** Drug injected directly into a vein.
- **Bioavailability:** **100%**—all of the drug reaches systemic circulation immediately.
- **Onset:** Immediate (seconds to minutes).
- **Advantages:** Rapid onset, predictable dosing, suitable for emergency situations.
- **Disadvantages:** Irreversible (once given, can't be retrieved); risk of vein irritation, thrombophlebitis, infection.
- **Nursing implications:** Verify IV line placement, check for extravasation, monitor for reactions during infusion.

#### **2. Intramuscular (IM)**
- **Route:** Drug injected into muscle tissue.
- **Bioavailability:** **50–90%**—variable depending on muscle blood flow and drug formulation.
- **Onset:** 15 minutes to 1 hour (slower than IV but faster than oral).
- **Advantages:** Can be sustained-release (depot injections); good for patients who can't take oral medications.
- **Disadvantages:** Painful; variable absorption based on muscle perfusion; risk of nerve/vessel injury.
- **Nursing implications:** Use correct injection sites; avoid areas with reduced blood flow (e.g., in shock); monitor for pain and abscess formation.

#### **3. Oral (PO)**
- **Route:** Drug taken by mouth, swallowed, and absorbed through the GI tract.
- **Bioavailability:** **20–90%**—highly variable due to first-pass metabolism and GI absorption factors.
- **Onset:** 30 minutes to 2+ hours depending on the drug and GI status.
- **Advantages:** Convenient, non-invasive, can use sustained-release formulations.
- **Disadvantages:** Variable absorption affected by food, pH, GI motility, and disease; slower onset; subject to first-pass metabolism.
- **Nursing implications:** Give with or without food per drug guidelines; assess GI function (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea reduce absorption); note if patient has swallowing difficulties.

#### **4. Sublingual (Under the Tongue)**
- **Route:** Drug placed under the tongue where it dissolves and is absorbed via oral mucosa blood vessels.
- **Bioavailability:** **High** (bypasses first-pass metabolism).
- **Onset:** Very rapid (minutes).
- **Advantages:** Rapid onset without injection; bypasses GI variability and first-pass metabolism.
- **Disadvantages:** Limited to small doses; can cause local irritation.
- **Nursing implications:** Teach patient to place tablet under tongue and not swallow. Classic example: sublingual nitroglycerin for acute angina.

#### **5. Transdermal (Skin Patch)**
- **Route:** Drug slowly released from patch and absorbed through skin.
- **Bioavailability:** Variable but generally steady.
- **Onset:** Slow (hours to days for initial effect); steady-state levels maintained over days/weeks.
- **Advantages:** Steady drug levels; bypasses GI variability; convenient (weekly or monthly patches).
- **Disadvantages:** Slow onset (not suitable for acute conditions); potential for skin irritation.
- **Nursing implications:** Apply to clean, dry skin; rotate sites to prevent irritation; remove old patch before applying new one.

#### **6. Inhalation (Inhaled/Aerosol)**
- **Route:** Drug inhaled via mouth or nose into lungs.
- **Bioavailability:** High in lungs (local effect); variable systemic absorption.
- **Onset:** Rapid for local effect (asthma rescue inhalers work in minutes); slower for systemic effect.
- **Advantages:** Rapid onset for respiratory conditions; can target lungs directly; reduces systemic dose needed.
- **Disadvantages:** Requires proper inhalation technique; potential airway irritation.
- **Nursing implications:** Teach correct inhaler technique; have patient demonstrate back; consider spacers for better drug delivery.

#### **7. Rectal (Suppository)**
- **Route:** Drug inserted into rectum where it dissolves and is absorbed.
- **Bioavailability:** Variable (20–60%) but partially bypasses first-pass metabolism (lower rectal blood drains directly to systemic circulation).
- **Onset:** 15–60 minutes.
- **Advantages:** Used when oral route unavailable (vomiting, unconsciousness); useful for pediatrics.
- **Disadvantages:** Variable absorption; patient discomfort; limited drug selection.
- **Nursing implications:** Privacy and dignity essential; assess contraindications (diarrhea, rectal disease).

### The Mechanisms of Drug Absorption: Crossing Cell Membranes

For a drug to be absorbed, it must cross biological membranes (e.g., GI epithelium). Three main mechanisms accomplish this:

#### **1. Passive Diffusion**
- Drug molecules move across the membrane from high concentration to low concentration without energy.
- Depends on: lipid solubility (lipophilic drugs cross easily), concentration gradient, and membrane area.
- Most common absorption mechanism.
- **Clinical example:** Aspirin (lipophilic, small molecule) is rapidly absorbed via passive diffusion.

#### **2. Active Transport**
- Drug molecules move across the membrane *against* the concentration gradient, using energy (ATP).
- Requires a specific transporter protein.
- Saturable (if too much drug, the transporter gets overwhelmed and absorption plateaus).
- **Clinical example:** Certain antibiotics (e.g., fluoroquinolones) use active transport in the GI tract.

#### **3. Carrier-Mediated Transport (Facilitated Diffusion)**
- Drug binds to a carrier protein that helps it across the membrane.
- Faster than passive diffusion but doesn't require energy (unlike active transport).
- **Clinical example:** Some vitamins and glucose use carrier-mediated transport.

### First-Pass Metabolism: Why Oral Drugs Often Disappear

Here's a critical concept that explains why some oral drugs have surprisingly low bioavailability:

When you take an oral medication, it's absorbed from the GI tract into the portal blood, which goes directly to the **liver** before reaching the general circulation. In the liver, the drug may be partially or completely metabolized (broken down) *before* it can exert its effect on the rest of the body. This process is called **first-pass metabolism** (or **first-pass effect** or **presystemic metabolism**).

**Example:** Nitroglycerin taken by mouth is >90% metabolized during first-pass, so a huge oral dose would be needed for an effect. That's why nitroglycerin for acute angina is given **sublingually** (under the tongue), bypassing the portal circulation and allowing rapid systemic effect at a low dose.

**Another example:** Propranolol (a beta-blocker) has oral bioavailability of only ~20% due to extensive first-pass metabolism. The same drug given IV has 100% bioavailability, so IV and oral doses must be very different.

**Nursing implication:** Always note the route on drug orders. An oral metoprolol dose is different from an IV metoprolol dose *for the same patient*, because the oral route experiences first-pass metabolism but IV does not.

### Factors Affecting Absorption: Making Oral Drugs Unpredictable

Even among patients taking the same oral drug at the same dose, absorption varies. Here's why:

#### **1. Gastric pH**
- Acidic environment (stomach) favors absorption of weak acids (e.g., aspirin).
- Neutral/alkaline environment (small intestine) favors absorption of weak bases.
- **Clinical problem:** H2-blockers or proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) reduce gastric acidity, impairing absorption of drugs that need acidic conditions (ketoconazole, an antifungal).

#### **2. Food**
- Fat-soluble drugs are better absorbed *with* food (fat enhances absorption).
- Some drugs are better absorbed on an *empty stomach* (food delays absorption).
- **Clinical example:** Metformin absorption is delayed by high-fat meals, potentially worsening blood glucose control if patient always takes it with food.

#### **3. GI Motility**
- Slower motility (constipation, anticholinergic drugs) increases absorption time but can delay onset.
- Faster motility (diarrhea) decreases absorption time and may reduce total absorption.
- **Clinical problem:** A patient with vomiting/diarrhea may not absorb oral antibiotics adequately; IV route may be needed.

#### **4. GI Disease**
- Malabsorption syndromes (celiac, Crohn's), short bowel syndrome, or gastric bypass surgery reduce drug absorption.
- **Nursing implication:** Assess for GI pathology before assuming oral dosing is appropriate.

#### **5. Drug Formulation**
- **Immediate-release:** Releases drug quickly → rapid onset, variable levels.
- **Extended-release (ER) or sustained-release (SR):** Releases drug slowly over hours → steady levels, longer dosing intervals.
- **Enteric-coated:** Protects drug from stomach acid, releases in small intestine. Example: aspirin EC (enteric-coated) for patients with stomach irritation.

**Clinical example:** Extended-release metformin is taken once daily and provides steady glucose control. Immediate-release metformin requires three times daily dosing and causes more variable blood glucose levels.

### First-Pass Metabolism Revisited: When and Why It Matters

Let's deepen the first-pass story:

**Sites of first-pass metabolism:**
- Primarily the **liver** (most important)
- Also the **GI wall** (some drugs metabolized by intestinal enzymes)

**Drugs heavily affected by first-pass metabolism:**
- Nitroglycerin (>90% metabolized)
- Propranolol (~80%)
- Morphine (~50%)
- Many others

**Why this matters clinically:**
1. **Oral bioavailability is unpredictable:** Even if absorption is complete, first-pass metabolism varies among patients (genetic differences, liver function, enzyme induction/inhibition).
2. **Route choice determines dosing:** An IV dose is much smaller than an oral dose for the same drug.
3. **Drug interactions at the hepatic level:** A drug that inhibits liver enzymes (e.g., ketoconazole) can increase first-pass metabolism of other drugs, raising their bioavailability and potentially causing toxicity.

### Clinical Scenario: Food-Drug Interaction Gone Wrong

**Mrs. Johnson** is prescribed metformin for type 2 diabetes. She takes it with breakfast (as she read "with food" on the label), which is high-fat. Over two weeks, her blood glucose control worsens. Her A1C rises, and she reports blurred vision (potential hyperglycemic symptom).

**What happened?** Fat delays metformin absorption, reducing peak concentration and potentially reducing total absorption. Her metformin levels are subtherapeutic. Combined with poor diet compliance, blood glucose control deteriorates.

**Solution:** Adjust timing—take metformin with a light meal or on an empty stomach per prescriber guidance. Recheck blood glucose patterns in 2 weeks.

**Nursing lesson:** Always review food-drug interactions. Don't assume "take with food" means high-fat food. Educate patients on *which* foods enhance or impair absorption.

### Section Recap

Absorption is the first critical stage of drug's ADME journey. The **route of administration** profoundly affects bioavailability and onset time. **IV gives 100% bioavailability immediately; oral gives 20–90% over 30 minutes to hours due to absorption variability and first-pass metabolism.** Understanding that **first-pass metabolism** can eliminate most of an oral drug (explaining why sublingual nitroglycerin works when oral nitroglycerin wouldn't) is key. As a nurse, assessing **GI factors** (food, pH, motility, disease) helps predict absorption and guides patient education on timing and food interactions.

## 2.3 DISTRIBUTION: WHERE THE DRUG GOES IN THE BODY

### What Is Distribution?

After a drug is absorbed into the bloodstream, it must travel to its site of action. **Distribution** is the process by which a drug leaves the bloodstream and enters various body tissues. Not all drugs go everywhere in the body; their distribution depends on their chemical properties and the body's anatomy.

### Volume of Distribution: A "Virtual Space" Concept

One way to describe distribution is through **volume of distribution (Vd)**, a theoretical concept that represents the space the drug occupies in the body. It's calculated as:

**Vd = Total amount of drug / Plasma concentration**

Think of it this way:
- **Small Vd** = Drug stays mostly in the blood (in plasma and proteins).
- **Large Vd** = Drug leaves the blood and distributes widely into tissues.

**Examples:**
- **Warfarin (Vd ~0.1–0.2 L/kg):** Highly protein-bound; stays mostly in the blood; small Vd.
- **Chloroquine (Vd ~50–100 L/kg):** Lipophilic; distributes widely to tissues, especially fatty tissue; very large Vd.

**Clinical relevance:** A large Vd means the drug has a long half-life (takes longer to eliminate). A small Vd means the drug clears quickly.

### Protein Binding: Active vs. Inactive Drug

Once a drug enters the bloodstream, some of it binds to plasma proteins (mainly **albumin** and **alpha-1 acid glycoprotein**). This is critical:

- **Bound drug:** Attached to a protein; cannot cross membranes; **pharmacologically inactive**.
- **Unbound (free) drug:** Not attached to protein; **pharmacologically active**; produces therapeutic and toxic effects.

**Example:** Warfarin is ~99% protein-bound:
- Of a 5 mg warfarin dose, ~4.95 mg is bound (inactive), and only ~0.05 mg is free (active).
- Despite this small active fraction, warfarin is very potent—the free drug is enough to affect blood coagulation.

**Why protein binding matters clinically:**

1. **Drug interactions:** If a patient is on two highly protein-bound drugs (e.g., warfarin + ibuprofen), the second drug can *displace* the first from protein binding. The warfarin suddenly has more free drug in circulation → increased INR → bleeding risk.

2. **Factors reducing protein binding:**
   - **Low plasma albumin** (liver disease, malnutrition, kidney disease): Fewer binding sites → more free drug → increased effect and risk of toxicity.
   - **Uremia** (kidney disease): Uremic toxins compete for protein binding → displacement of drugs.

**Nursing implication:** Patients with liver or kidney disease are at higher risk for drug toxicity, especially with protein-bound drugs. Monitor closely; expect dose reductions.

### Lipophilicity: Fat-Soluble vs. Water-Soluble Drugs

Whether a drug is **lipophilic** (fat-soluble) or **hydrophilic** (water-soluble) dramatically affects where it goes in the body:

- **Lipophilic drugs:** Readily cross cell membranes (which are made of lipid); distribute widely into body tissues, including fatty tissues.
  - **Examples:** Chloroquine, digoxin, anesthetics.
  - **Clinical consequence:** Long half-life (accumulate in fat); lipophilic drugs used in obese patients may require higher doses.

- **Hydrophilic drugs:** Don't cross cell membranes easily; stay mostly in plasma and extracellular fluid.
  - **Examples:** Aminoglycosides (water-soluble antibiotics), gentamicin.
  - **Clinical consequence:** Short half-life; don't penetrate tissues well, but good for bloodstream infections.

### Special Barriers to Distribution: The Blood-Brain Barrier and Placenta

Not all tissues are equally accessible to drugs. Some have special barriers:

#### **Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB)**
The BBB is a selective barrier formed by tight junctions between brain capillary endothelial cells, protecting the brain from foreign substances.

**How the BBB works:**
1. **Tight junctions:** Capillary cells are tightly connected, preventing most molecules from squeezing between cells.
2. **Efflux pumps:** Proteins like P-glycoprotein actively pump drugs *out* of the brain, further reducing CNS penetration.

**What crosses the BBB:**
- **Lipophilic drugs** (e.g., anesthetics, some antidepressants) cross easily.
- **Small, hydrophilic molecules** (e.g., glucose) cross via specific transporters.
- **Most large or hydrophilic drugs** do NOT cross the BBB.

**Clinical implications:**
- **Antibiotics for meningitis:** Standard antibiotics don't cross the BBB well. Specific antibiotics (ceftriaxone, vancomycin) that penetrate the BBB are used.
- **Pain relief in CNS disorders:** Many opioids penetrate the BBB; some don't. If a patient with chronic pain has poor pain control with morphine, morphine is reaching the brain. If pain control is still poor, the drug choice may be appropriate.
- **Psychiatric medications:** Antidepressants and antipsychotics must cross the BBB. Drugs unable to cross are useless for depression, even if they work systemically.

#### **Placental Transfer**
Most drugs cross the placenta to some degree, potentially exposing the fetus. The degree of transfer depends on:

1. **Molecular weight:** Drugs <1000 Daltons cross more readily.
2. **Lipophilicity:** Lipophilic drugs cross easily.
3. **Protein binding:** Highly protein-bound drugs transfer less.
4. **Gestational age:** Placental permeability increases with gestation.

**Clinical implications:**
- **First trimester:** Organs are developing; drug exposure risks birth defects (teratogenicity).
- **Warfarin in first trimester:** Associated with fetal warfarin syndrome (nasal hypoplasia, skeletal abnormalities, CNS effects). **Heparin** (doesn't cross placenta) is preferred.
- **ACE inhibitors in second/third trimester:** Associated with fetal renal dysfunction, oligohydramnios. Contraindicated in pregnancy.
- **Routine monitoring:** Pregnant patients on seizure medications, diabetes medications, or cardiovascular drugs require careful oversight and possible dose adjustments.

#### **Breast Milk**
Some drugs appear in breast milk, potentially affecting nursing infants:
- **Lipophilic drugs** accumulate in milk fat.
- **Examples:** Some antidepressants, beta-blockers, warfarin (warfarin in milk is minimal; breastfeeding generally safe).
- **Nursing consideration:** Educate mothers that some drugs are fine while nursing; others require switching to formula.

### Tissue Localization and Reservoir Effects

Some drugs accumulate in specific tissues:

- **Bone:** Tetracycline antibiotics bind to calcium in bone and teeth. In children, this causes discoloration and enamel damage; tetracycline is contraindicated in pediatric patients and pregnant women.
- **Fatty tissue:** Lipophilic drugs like chloroquine, DDT, and some anesthetics accumulate in fat. In obese patients, these drugs have a much larger Vd and longer half-life.
- **Skeletal muscle:** Digoxin localizes in skeletal muscle. In elderly or cachectic patients with reduced muscle mass, digoxin accumulates in remaining muscle, increasing risk of toxicity.

**Redistribution:** After IV administration, some drugs initially concentrate in highly perfused tissues (brain, heart, lungs), then redistribute to less perfused tissues over time. **Thiopental** (an IV anesthetic) exemplifies this: rapid onset (enters brain quickly) followed by redistribution to fat (ending the effect, even though the drug remains in the body).

### Clinical Scenario: Pregnancy and Drug Choice

**Sarah**, 28 years old, is 8 weeks pregnant and has epilepsy. She's currently on **phenytoin** for seizure control. At her obstetric visit, the team discusses risks:

- Phenytoin is **lipophilic** and **crosses the placenta**.
- Phenytoin is **FDA Category D** (evidence of fetal risk, but benefit may justify use).
- Risk of **fetal hydantoin syndrome** (facial abnormalities, cleft palate, growth retardation, CNS effects).

**Decision:** Continue phenytoin because uncontrolled seizures pose greater risk to fetus (maternal hypoxia, trauma from falls, premature labor) than the known risks of phenytoin. Plasma phenytoin levels are monitored closely throughout pregnancy to maintain seizure control while minimizing fetal exposure.

**Nursing lesson:** Distribution concepts affect clinical decision-making, especially in pregnancy. You must understand *why* some drugs are contraindicated (they cross to fetus and cause harm) and when benefits outweigh risks (like seizure control).

### Section Recap

**Distribution** determines where a drug goes and how long it stays in the body. **Protein binding** (active vs. inactive drug) explains drug interactions; **lipophilicity** explains why some drugs accumulate in fat. **Special barriers** like the blood-brain barrier and placenta prevent some drugs from reaching certain tissues—clinically important when treating meningitis (need BBB-penetrating antibiotics) or pregnancy (avoid teratogenic drugs). Understanding distribution helps you predict drug efficacy, anticipate drug interactions, and make informed clinical judgments about drug choices in vulnerable populations.

## 2.4 METABOLISM: HOW THE BODY BREAKS DOWN DRUGS

### What Is Metabolism and Why It Matters

**Metabolism** (also called **biotransformation**) is the process by which the body chemically transforms a drug into a form that can be excreted. Most drugs are lipophilic (fat-soluble), which allows them to cross cell membranes and reach their target sites—but also prevents them from being excreted in water-based urine. Metabolism converts lipophilic drugs into more water-soluble (hydrophilic) forms that the kidneys can eliminate.

Metabolism occurs in three phases, all primarily in the liver but also in kidneys, GI tract, and other tissues.

### Phase I Metabolism: Oxidation, Reduction, and Hydrolysis

**Phase I** uses a family of enzymes called **cytochrome P450 (CYP450)** to chemically modify drugs through oxidation, reduction, or hydrolysis.

#### **How Phase I Works:**

The main enzyme family is **CYP450**, located in the smooth endoplasmic reticulum of liver cells. CYP450 enzymes add or expose functional groups on drug molecules, preparing them for Phase II conjugation.

**Example:** 
- Codeine (a prodrug) undergoes Phase I metabolism via **CYP2D6** → converts codeine to **morphine** (the active form).
- In CYP2D6 poor metabolizers, codeine stays unchanged → no morphine formation → **no pain relief**.

#### **Major CYP450 Isoforms and Their Clinical Significance:**

| CYP450 Enzyme | % of Drug Metabolism | Major Substrates | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **CYP3A4** | ~50% | Statins, Ca2+ channel blockers, immunosuppressants, protease inhibitors, macrolide antibiotics | Most important CYP450; involved in metabolism of ~50% of all drugs |
| **CYP2D6** | ~30% | Codeine, tramadol, beta-blockers, antidepressants, antiarrhythmics | Highly variable genetically; poor metabolizers at risk for toxicity |
| **CYP2C9** | ~10% | Warfarin, NSAIDs, sulfonylureas | Involved in warfarin metabolism; genetic variants affect dosing |
| **CYP2C19** | ~10% | Clopidogrel, omeprazole, antidepressants | Important for clopidogrel activation; poor metabolizers at stent thrombosis risk |
| **CYP1A2** | Variable | Theophylline, some antipsychotics | Induced by smoking |
| **CYP2B6** | ~8% | Efavirenz (antiretroviral) | Less common but clinically important for specific drugs |

### Phase II Metabolism: Conjugation

**Phase II** involves adding water-soluble groups (conjugation) to drugs or their Phase I metabolites, making them more easily excreted.

**Common Phase II pathways:**

1. **Glucuronidation:** Most common Phase II reaction. Glucuronic acid is added to drug. Produces water-soluble metabolites.
   - **Example:** Morphine is glucuronidated → morphine glucuronide (water-soluble, excreted in urine).

2. **Sulfation:** Sulfate group added to drug.
   - **Example:** Some acetaminophen is sulfated (the rest is glucuronidated).

3. **Acetylation:** Acetyl group added. Genetically variable (slow vs. fast acetylators).
   - **Example:** Isoniazid is acetylated; slow acetylators on standard doses risk peripheral neuropathy.

4. **Methylation:** Methyl group added.

**Key point:** Phase II usually **inactivates** the drug, producing metabolites that are inactive and water-soluble for urinary excretion.

### Phase III Metabolism: Active Transport

**Phase III** involves transporter proteins that actively pump drugs and metabolites out of hepatocytes (liver cells) for excretion into bile or urine.

**Example transporter:**
- **P-glycoprotein (MDR1):** Actively pumps drugs OUT of the body (e.g., digoxin, certain cancer drugs).

**Clinical significance:** P-glycoprotein can be inhibited by some drugs (e.g., ritonavir), potentially increasing plasma concentrations of drugs that normally are pumped out.

### CYP450 Enzyme Induction: Speeding Up Drug Metabolism

Some drugs, foods, and environmental factors induce (increase) CYP450 enzyme expression. This causes faster drug metabolism, *lowering* plasma drug concentrations.

#### **Common CYP450 Inducers:**

| Inducer | CYP450s Induced | Clinical Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Rifampin** (antibiotic) | CYP3A4, 2C9, 2C19, and others (broad spectrum) | Dramatically speeds metabolism of oral contraceptives, warfarin, many others → therapeutic failure |
| **Phenytoin** (anticonvulsant) | CYP2C9, 2C19, 3A4 | Increases warfarin metabolism → need for higher warfarin doses |
| **Carbamazepine** (anticonvulsant) | Multiple CYPs | Similar broad induction as phenytoin |
| **Barbiturates** | Multiple CYPs | Broad-spectrum induction |
| **St. John's Wort** (herbal supplement) | CYP3A4, others | Reduces effectiveness of oral contraceptives, warfarin, protease inhibitors |

#### **Mechanism:**
Inducers *upregulate* (increase production of) CYP450 enzymes. More enzyme = faster drug metabolism = lower drug levels.

#### **Timeline:**
Induction develops over **1–2 weeks** as enzyme levels increase. It takes the same time to wear off after stopping the inducer.

#### **Clinical Example: Oral Contraceptive Failure**
A 25-year-old woman on oral contraceptives (which contain hormones metabolized by CYP3A4) is prescribed rifampin for tuberculosis. After 2 weeks, rifampin-induced CYP3A4 increases—hormonal contraceptive metabolism speeds up → hormonal levels drop → contraceptive failure → unintended pregnancy.

**Solution:** Switch to backup contraception (condoms) during rifampin therapy and for 1 month after completing it (to allow CYP3A4 levels to normalize).

### CYP450 Enzyme Inhibition: Slowing Down Drug Metabolism

Other drugs, foods, and supplements *inhibit* (decrease) CYP450 enzyme activity. This slows drug metabolism, *raising* plasma concentrations and risking toxicity.

#### **Common CYP450 Inhibitors:**

| Inhibitor | CYP450s Inhibited | Clinical Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Ketoconazole** (antifungal) | CYP3A4, 2C9 | Increases levels of statins → myopathy; warfarin → bleeding; immunosuppressants → toxicity |
| **Macrolide antibiotics** (erythromycin, clarithromycin) | CYP3A4 | Similar to ketoconazole; increases levels of theophylline, cardiac drugs |
| **Fluconazole** (antifungal) | CYP2C9, 2C19 | Increases warfarin levels → bleeding risk; clopidogrel → reduced antiplatelet effect |
| **Ritonavir** (protease inhibitor/HIV drug) | CYP3A4 | Potent inhibitor; increases levels of many drugs |
| **Grapefruit juice** | CYP3A4 | Irreversibly inhibits CYP3A4 in GI tract → increases statins, Ca2+ blockers, immunosuppressants |

#### **Mechanism:**
Inhibitors *block* CYP450 enzyme activity. Less active enzyme = slower drug metabolism = higher drug levels.

#### **Timeline:**
Inhibition develops **quickly** (hours to days) and reverses quickly after stopping the inhibitor—except grapefruit juice (inhibition persists 24+ hours).

#### **Clinical Example: Statin Myopathy**
A 60-year-old man on simvastatin (CYP3A4 substrate) develops a fungal infection and is prescribed ketoconazole (CYP3A4 inhibitor). Ketoconazole blocks CYP3A4 → simvastatin metabolism slows → simvastatin plasma levels rise 15-fold → statin concentration becomes toxic → muscle pain (myopathy), elevated CK, rhabdomyolysis risk.

**Solution:** Switch simvastatin to pravastatin (not metabolized by CYP3A4) or stop simvastatin during ketoconazole therapy.

#### **Clinical Example: Grapefruit Juice**
A patient on atorvastatin (a statin) for high cholesterol starts drinking grapefruit juice daily for breakfast (thinking it's healthy). Grapefruit juice inhibits CYP3A4 in the GI tract → atorvastatin absorption increases → plasma levels rise → myopathy develops.

**Patient education:** Patients on CYP3A4 substrates should avoid grapefruit completely.

### Pharmacogenomics and CYP450 Variation: Why "Standard" Doses Don't Work for Everyone

CYP450 genes are highly polymorphic (many variants exist). This genetic variation creates different **metabolizer phenotypes**:

1. **Poor metabolizers (PM):** Reduced enzyme activity → slow metabolism → drug accumulation → toxicity at standard doses.
2. **Extensive metabolizers (EM):** Normal enzyme activity → standard metabolism.
3. **Ultra-rapid metabolizers (UM):** Increased enzyme activity (gene duplication) → fast metabolism → subtherapeutic levels at standard doses.

#### **Example: CYP2D6 and Codeine**
- **CYP2D6 poor metabolizers (~7–10% of Caucasians):** Codeine cannot be converted to morphine (its active form) → codeine is ineffective for pain despite "standard dosing."
  - **Clinical consequence:** Patient receives codeine, expects pain relief, doesn't get it, reports "codeine doesn't work for me."
- **CYP2D6 ultra-rapid metabolizers (~1–5% of some populations):** Codeine is rapidly converted to morphine → morphine levels become very high → risk of respiratory depression, overdose even at "standard" codeine doses.
  - **Clinical consequence:** Patient at risk for opioid toxicity at standard doses.

**Solution:** Genetic testing (e.g., GeneSight, Genomind) identifies metabolizer phenotype → guides personalized dosing.

### Non-Hepatic Metabolism: When the Liver Isn't the Only Player

While the liver is the primary site, some drugs are metabolized in other tissues:

- **GI wall:** Some drugs metabolized by intestinal CYP450 enzymes (contributing to first-pass metabolism).
- **Blood esterases:** Succinylcholine (a muscle relaxant used during anesthesia) is metabolized by plasma esterases, not the liver.
- **Kidneys:** Some drugs metabolized in renal tissue.

**Clinical significance:** Patients with reduced GI blood flow (heart failure, shock), kidney disease, or genetic esterase deficiency may have altered drug metabolism.

### ⚠️ SAFETY CALLOUTS

> **⚠️ Medication Safety Alert: Grapefruit & CYP3A4 Inhibition**
> 
> Grapefruit juice inhibits CYP3A4 irreversibly for 24+ hours. Patients on CYP3A4 substrates (statins, calcium channel blockers, immunosuppressants, some cancer drugs) should **completely avoid grapefruit juice**—not just limit it. This includes grapefruit in salads, smoothies, and supplements. A single glass of grapefruit juice can increase drug levels significantly.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert: Rifampin as a Broad-Spectrum CYP450 Inducer**
> 
> Rifampin is a **potent, broad-spectrum CYP450 inducer**, affecting CYP3A4, 2C9, 2C19, and others. Key clinical consequence: **oral contraceptive failure**. Patients on rifampin require **backup contraception**. Also increases metabolism of warfarin (higher doses needed), protease inhibitors, and many others.

> **⚠️ Drug Interaction Alert: Warfarin + NSAIDs + CYP450 Inhibition**
> 
> NSAIDs like ibuprofen inhibit CYP2C9 (which metabolizes warfarin) *and* have antiplatelet effects *and* increase bleeding risk. This triple mechanism makes NSAIDs + warfarin a **contraindicated combination**. If pain relief is needed, use acetaminophen instead.

### Clinical Scenario: Polypharmacy and Metabolic Chaos

**Mr. Patel**, 72 years old, is on multiple medications:
- Warfarin for atrial fibrillation
- Atorvastatin for cholesterol
- Metoprolol for hypertension
- Lisinopril for heart failure

He develops a fungal toenail infection. His dermatologist prescribes **ketoconazole** (an antifungal CYP3A4 and 2C9 inhibitor).

**Metabolic consequences:**
1. Ketoconazole inhibits CYP2C9 → warfarin metabolism slows → warfarin levels rise → INR rises → bleeding risk increases.
2. Ketoconazole inhibits CYP3A4 → atorvastatin levels rise → statin myopathy risk.

**Clinical action:** Pharmacist alerts prescriber; ketoconazole is stopped and replaced with an antifungal that doesn't inhibit CYP450 (e.g., terbinafine). INR is closely monitored for the next week as ketoconazole clears.

**Nursing lesson:** Always screen for drug interactions, especially with inducers/inhibitors in elderly patients on multiple drugs. Polypharmacy + metabolic drug interactions = high toxicity risk.

### Section Recap

**Metabolism** transforms lipophilic drugs into water-soluble forms for excretion. **Phase I** (CYP450 oxidation), **Phase II** (conjugation), and **Phase III** (active transport) work sequentially. The **CYP450 enzyme family**—particularly CYP3A4, CYP2D6, CYP2C9, and CYP2C19—metabolizes ~50% of all drugs. **Induction** (faster metabolism) and **inhibition** (slower metabolism) of CYP450 cause major drug interactions. **Pharmacogenomics** explains why standard doses fail in some patients (poor metabolizers accumulate drugs; ultra-rapid metabolizers need higher doses). As a nurse, understanding these concepts allows you to **predict interactions, anticipate toxicity in vulnerable patients, and educate patients** about avoiding foods (grapefruit) and supplements (St. John's Wort) that interfere with drug metabolism.

## 2.5 EXCRETION: HOW THE BODY ELIMINATES DRUGS

### What Is Excretion?

**Excretion** is the process by which the body eliminates drugs and their metabolites, removing them from the body permanently. The **kidneys** are the primary route of excretion (90% of drugs), but the liver (biliary excretion), lungs (volatile drugs), and other routes also contribute.

### Renal Excretion: The Kidney's Three Processes

The kidney eliminates drugs through three main processes:

#### **1. Glomerular Filtration**
In the glomerulus (the filtering unit of the kidney), blood is filtered under pressure. Small, unbound molecules (including drugs) are filtered from the blood into the urine.

**What gets filtered:**
- Unbound (free) drug ✓
- Molecules <65 kilodaltons ✓

**What doesn't get filtered:**
- Protein-bound drug ✗ (too large to filter; stays in blood)
- Red blood cells ✗ (stay in blood)
- Large proteins ✗ (stay in blood)

**Clinical example:** 
- Warfarin is 99% protein-bound. Only the tiny unbound fraction is filtered. So despite high warfarin doses, little is lost in urine; warfarin stays in the body a long time (half-life ~40 hours).
- Gentamicin (an antibiotic) is water-soluble and not protein-bound. It's readily filtered → quickly excreted → short half-life (~2 hours).

#### **2. Active Secretion**
In the proximal tubule of the nephron, active transport pumps drugs *out of the blood* and *into the tubular lumen* (the channel where urine forms). This process is independent of filtration and can eliminate drug that wasn't filtered.

**Characteristics:**
- Active process (requires energy/ATP)
- Saturable (too much drug overwhelms the transporter)
- Selective (specific transporters for different drugs)

**Clinical significance:**
- Some drugs compete for the same secretory transporter → **drug interaction**.
- **Example:** Penicillin and **probenecid** both use the organic anion transporter. When taken together, probenecid blocks penicillin secretion → penicillin accumulates → higher levels. This combination was historically used to increase penicillin levels and allow less frequent dosing.

#### **3. Passive Reabsorption**
After filtration and secretion, the urine passes down the tubule. Some drug molecules in the urine are **lipophilic** and can cross the tubular cells to reenter the blood (reabsorption). This reduces net drug excretion.

**Influenced by:** Urinary pH
- **Acidic urine** (low pH): Weak bases are trapped (ionized) in urine → not reabsorbed → faster excretion.
- **Alkaline urine** (high pH): Weak acids are trapped in urine → not reabsorbed → faster excretion.

**Clinical application: Aspirin overdose treatment**
When a patient overdoses on aspirin (a weak acid), alkalinizing the urine with sodium bicarbonate increases urinary pH → aspirin ionizes in the acidic urine → cannot be reabsorbed → rapidly excreted. This clinical maneuver can speed up aspirin elimination.

### Biliary and Hepatic Excretion

Some drugs are excreted in **bile** (digestive fluid produced by the liver). These drugs are typically:
- **Large molecular weight** (>300 Daltons)
- **Highly protein-bound**

**Enterohepatic circulation:** Some drugs excreted in bile are reabsorbed in the small intestine and returned to the liver via portal blood—then filtered by the liver again. This circulation can extend a drug's half-life and cause accumulation.

**Clinical implication:** Patients with reduced bile flow (liver disease, biliary obstruction) can't excrete these drugs → accumulation and toxicity.

### Pulmonary Excretion

Volatile drugs (those with high vapor pressure) are exhaled through the lungs. **Alcohol** and **anesthetics** (like isoflurane) are eliminated this way. For most drugs, pulmonary excretion is minor.

### Renal Function Assessment: Why Serum Creatinine Isn't Enough

Serum creatinine is an indirect marker of renal function, but it's **unreliable**, especially in elderly patients. Why?

**Serum creatinine depends on:**
- Kidney function ✓
- Muscle mass (muscle produces creatinine)

**The problem:** Elderly patients often have reduced **muscle mass** with preserved **serum creatinine**. Their kidneys function poorly, but the serum creatinine appears "normal" because they produce less creatinine from their smaller muscle mass.

**Example:** An 85-year-old woman has serum creatinine = 1.0 mg/dL (appears normal). But she has severe renal impairment due to age-related nephron loss. If you dose her medications based on "normal" creatinine alone, you'll overdose her and cause toxicity.

#### **Solution: Calculate Actual Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR)**

**Cockcroft-Gault equation** (most commonly used for dose adjustments):

**CrCl (mL/min) = [(140 − age) × weight(kg) × (0.85 if female)] / (72 × serum creatinine)**

**Example:**
- 85-year-old woman, weight 55 kg, serum creatinine 1.0 mg/dL
- CrCl = [(140 − 85) × 55 × 0.85] / (72 × 1.0)
- CrCl = [55 × 55 × 0.85] / 72
- CrCl = 2563.75 / 72
- **CrCl ≈ 36 mL/min** ← Severe renal impairment!

Despite "normal" serum creatinine, her actual GFR is only 36 mL/min (normal is >90 mL/min). She needs significantly reduced doses of renally-cleared drugs.

**Categories of renal impairment (based on CrCl):**
- **Normal:** >90 mL/min
- **Mild impairment:** 60–89 mL/min
- **Moderate impairment:** 30–59 mL/min
- **Severe impairment:** <30 mL/min

### Dose Adjustments Based on Renal Function

When renal function is impaired, drugs that depend on renal excretion accumulate. Three strategies prevent toxicity:

#### **Strategy 1: Reduce the Dose**
Give a smaller dose at normal intervals.
- **Example:** Standard gentamicin dose = 1.5–2 mg/kg every 8 hours in normal renal function. In severe renal impairment (CrCl <30 mL/min), dose is reduced to 1–1.5 mg/kg every 24 hours.

#### **Strategy 2: Extend the Interval**
Give the standard dose but less frequently.
- **Example:** Standard acyclovir = 800 mg every 4 hours for herpes simplex. In moderate renal impairment, acyclovir = 800 mg every 8 hours. In severe impairment, acyclovir = 800 mg every 12 hours.

#### **Strategy 3: Reduce Both Dose and Interval**
For drugs with very narrow therapeutic windows.
- **Example:** Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin) use extended-interval dosing in renal impairment, adjusting both dose and interval.

**Drug handbooks** (UpToDate, Lexicomp, drug package inserts) provide dose adjustment tables. As a nurse, you must verify doses in patients with renal impairment.

### Why Elderly Patients Are at High Risk

Elderly patients face multiple renal risks:

1. **Age-related GFR decline:** After age 30, GFR declines ~1% per year.
   - By age 85, GFR may be 50% of normal despite "normal" serum creatinine.

2. **Reduced muscle mass:** Serum creatinine doesn't rise (less creatinine produced).
   - Disguises renal impairment.

3. **Polypharmacy:** Elderly patients on multiple drugs → high risk of drug interactions and accumulation.

4. **Disease states:** Comorbidities (heart failure, diabetes) worsen renal function.

**Clinical strategy:** **Always calculate CrCl in elderly patients before dosing renally-cleared drugs.** Never rely on serum creatinine alone.

Common examples:

|---|---|---|---|
| **Gentamicin** | 2–3 hours | Renally excreted; narrow therapeutic window | Reduce dose, extend interval; monitor levels |
| **Digoxin** | 36–48 hours | Renally excreted; narrow therapeutic window | Reduce dose by 50% or more |
| **Warfarin** | ~40 hours | Metabolized, but metabolite renally excreted | Monitor INR closely; may need dose reduction |
| **Lisinopril (ACE inhibitor)** | 12–13 hours | Renally excreted | Reduce dose; monitor for hyperkalemia |
| **Metformin** | 6 hours | Renally excreted; accumulates → lactic acidosis risk | Reduce dose or contraindicate if CrCl <30 mL/min |
| **Vancomycin** | 4–6 hours (normal) → 24–120 hours (severe renal failure) | Renally excreted | Extend interval dramatically; monitor trough levels |

Patients on hemodialysis or peritoneal dialysis have unique medication needs:

- **Timing of dose relative to dialysis:** Some drugs are removed by dialysis (smaller molecules). Dose may be given *after* dialysis to avoid removal.

> **⚠️ Critical Safety Alert: Serum Creatinine ≠ Renal Function in Elderly**
> 
> An elderly patient with "normal" serum creatinine (1.0 mg/dL) often has severe renal impairment. **Always calculate CrCl** using Cockcroft-Gault before dosing renally-cleared drugs (aminoglycosides, digoxin, ACE inhibitors, NSAIDs, metformin, etc.). Dosing based on serum creatinine alone is a common medication error causing toxicity in older adults.

> **⚠️ Medication Safety Alert: NSAIDs in Kidney Disease**
> 
> NSAIDs inhibit renal prostaglandins, further reducing GFR. **Contraindicated in moderate-to-severe renal disease**. NSAIDs also increase hyperkalemia risk in patients on ACE inhibitors. Use acetaminophen for pain instead.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert: Metformin and Renal Function**
> 
> Metformin is renally excreted and accumulates in renal disease → **lactic acidosis risk**. Contraindicated if CrCl <30 mL/min. Requires dose reduction if CrCl 30–59 mL/min. Always check renal function before prescribing or refilling metformin.

### Clinical Scenario: The Elderly Patient's Hidden Renal Impairment

**Mr. Kim**, 78 years old, is admitted to the hospital with urosepsis. He's started on gentamicin IV for gram-negative bacteremia.

**Medication order:** Gentamicin 1.5 mg/kg every 8 hours. Nurse calculates his dose as 130 mg every 8 hours (weight 87 kg, normal dosing).

**Labs show:**
- Serum creatinine: 1.0 mg/dL (appears normal)
- Age: 78 years, weight 87 kg

**Nurse calculates CrCl:**
- CrCl = [(140 − 78) × 87 × 0.85] / (72 × 1.0) [assuming male, so no 0.85 multiplier]
- CrCl = [(140 − 78) × 87] / 72
- CrCl = [62 × 87] / 72
- CrCl ≈ **75 mL/min** ← Mild-to-moderate impairment

**Action:** Gentamicin dose should be **reduced**. Instead of 130 mg every 8 hours, use gentamicin 130 mg every 12 hours or extended-interval dosing (e.g., 5 mg/kg every 24 hours). Peak and trough levels are obtained.

**Without this adjustment:** Gentamicin accumulates → plasma concentrations rise above therapeutic range → **ototoxicity** (hearing loss) and **nephrotoxicity** (worsening kidney function).

**Nursing lesson:** Calculate CrCl; never assume "normal" creatinine = normal function in elderly patients.

### Section Recap

**Excretion** eliminates drugs from the body. **Glomerular filtration** filters unbound, small drugs. **Active secretion** in the renal tubule pumps drugs into urine (saturable, can interact with other drugs). **Passive reabsorption** allows lipophilic drugs to reenter blood (affected by urinary pH). **Renal function assessment** requires calculating **CrCl** (Cockcroft-Gault), not relying on serum creatinine alone. Elderly patients with "normal" creatinine often have severely reduced GFR → high risk for drug accumulation and toxicity. **Dose adjustments in renal impairment** reduce dose, extend interval, or both. As a nurse, you must always assess renal function, calculate appropriate doses for renally-cleared drugs, and monitor for toxicity in patients with compromised kidney function.

## 2.6 HALF-LIFE, STEADY STATE, AND CLEARANCE

### What Is Half-Life?

**Half-life (t½)** is the time required for the plasma drug concentration to decrease by **half**. It's one of the most important pharmacokinetic parameters because it determines:
- How long the drug stays in the body
- How frequently you need to dose
- How long it takes to reach steady state
- When toxicity may develop if dosing is too frequent

#### **Calculating Half-Life (Optional—For Conceptual Understanding)**

**t½ = 0.693 / ke** (where ke = elimination rate constant)

Or, more practically:

**t½ = 0.693 × Vd / CL** (where Vd = volume of distribution, CL = clearance)

**Key concept:** Half-life is **dose-independent** and **metabolic-independent** in first-order kinetics (which applies to most drugs).

#### **What This Means:**
- If a patient takes 5 mg of digoxin and another takes 50 mg digoxin, *both have the same half-life* (~40 hours).
- Half-life depends on the drug's properties (Vd and clearance), not the dose.

### Examples of Half-Lives and Their Clinical Implications

| Drug | Half-Life | Dosing Interval | Clinical Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Penicillin** | ~1 hour | Every 4–6 hours | Short half-life; frequent dosing needed |
| **Cephalosporin** | 1–2 hours | Every 6–8 hours | Short; frequent dosing |
| **Metoprolol** | 3–7 hours | Every 6–12 hours | Intermediate; dosing 1–2× daily |
| **Warfarin** | 40 hours | Once daily | Long; reaches steady state in 8–9 days |
| **Digoxin** | 36–48 hours | Once daily | Long; accumulates with frequent dosing |
| **Amiodarone** (antiarrhythmic) | 40–60 days | Once daily (but loads first) | Very long; reaches steady state in 4+ weeks |

### Steady State: When Drug Input = Drug Output

With **repeated dosing** at regular intervals, drug levels rise with each dose until they plateau. At this plateau, the amount of drug being added (from each dose) equals the amount being eliminated → **equilibrium** = **steady state (Css)**.

#### **Time to Reach Steady State**

**Steady state is reached after approximately 5 half-lives of the drug.**

**Examples:**
- **Penicillin** (t½ = 1 hour) → steady state in ~5 hours
- **Metoprolol** (t½ = 5 hours) → steady state in ~25 hours
- **Warfarin** (t½ = 40 hours) → steady state in ~8–9 days
- **Amiodarone** (t½ = 50 days) → steady state in ~4 weeks

**Clinical implication:** When you start a patient on a drug with a long half-life, don't expect immediate therapeutic effect. Warfarin's anticoagulant effect takes days to develop; amiodarone's antiarrhythmic effect takes weeks.

#### **Loading Dose: Achieving Steady-State Concentration Quickly**

When rapid therapeutic effect is needed, a **loading dose** is given first, immediately achieving the steady-state concentration. Then **maintenance doses** sustain the level.

**Calculation:**

**Loading Dose = (Desired Css × Vd) / F**

Where:
- Css = desired plasma concentration (ng/mL or mcg/mL)
- Vd = volume of distribution (L or mL)
- F = bioavailability (fraction reaching systemic circulation; 1.0 for IV, less for oral)

**Maintenance Dose = (Css × CL × τ) / F**

Where:
- τ (tau) = dosing interval (hours)
- CL = clearance (L/hr)

#### **Clinical Example: Digoxin Loading and Maintenance**

A patient with acute heart failure needs digoxin urgently.

- **Loading dose:** 500 mcg IV (achieves therapeutic digoxin level immediately)
  - Without a loading dose, the patient would have to wait 5 × 40 hours = 200 hours (~8 days) to reach therapeutic levels—unacceptable for acute heart failure.

- **Maintenance dose:** 250 mcg IV daily (sustains therapeutic level)
  - The maintenance dose replaces the drug eliminated each day, keeping the patient at steady state.

**Patient education:** Explain to the patient: "Your initial dose is larger to quickly get the medication in your system. Then your daily doses keep you stable. Don't be alarmed by the bigger first dose."

### Clearance: The Rate at Which the Body Eliminates Drug

**Clearance (CL)** is defined as the **volume of plasma completely cleared of drug per unit time**. Units are typically mL/min or L/hr.

**Concept:** If a drug has a clearance of 100 mL/min, that means 100 mL of plasma loses all drug molecules per minute.

**Calculation:**

**CL = Dose / AUC** (where AUC = area under the curve, a measure of total drug exposure over time)

Or more practically:

**CL = Vd × ke** (volume of distribution × elimination rate constant)

#### **Relationship Between Clearance and Steady-State Concentration**

**Css = (Dose/τ) / CL**

This relationship is clinically crucial:
- If clearance decreases (liver disease, kidney disease, drug interaction), Css rises → toxicity risk.
- If clearance increases (enzyme induction), Css drops → therapeutic failure.

#### **Total Clearance = Multiple Components**

**Total CL = Hepatic CL + Renal CL + Other CL**

- **Hepatic clearance:** Drugs metabolized by liver.
- **Renal clearance:** Drugs excreted by kidneys (filtered + secreted − reabsorbed).
- **Other clearance:** GI metabolism, pulmonary, etc.

**Example:** Gentamicin is 95% renally excreted, so almost all of its clearance is renal. In kidney disease, gentamicin clearance drops dramatically.

### Bioavailability: How Much Drug Actually Reaches Circulation

**Bioavailability (F)** is the fraction of an administered dose that reaches systemic circulation in unchanged form.

- **IV administration:** F = 100% (by definition; all drug enters circulation).
- **Oral administration:** F = typically 20–90% (affected by absorption, first-pass metabolism).

**Example:**
- Nitroglycerin oral: F ~10% (due to extensive first-pass metabolism).
- Nitroglycerin sublingual: F ~90% (bypasses first-pass).
- So a 0.5 mg sublingual nitroglycerin dose is equivalent to a 5 mg oral dose in terms of bioavailable drug reaching systemic circulation.

### Practical Application: Dosing Scenarios

#### **Scenario 1: Starting Warfarin**

A patient with newly diagnosed atrial fibrillation needs anticoagulation.

- **Goal:** INR 2–3 (therapeutic for AFib).
- **Warfarin dosing:**
  - Initial dose: 5–10 mg daily (loading dose).
  - Steady state: Reached in ~8–9 days (5 half-lives).
  - Maintenance: Adjusted based on INR, typically 2–10 mg daily.

**Nursing considerations:**
- Patient won't have therapeutic INR immediately; it takes days.
- INR is monitored frequently initially (daily until stable, then weekly, then monthly).
- Tell patient: "This medication takes about a week to work. We'll monitor your blood regularly and adjust your dose."
- Until therapeutic INR is reached, patient may need bridging anticoagulation (e.g., LMWH or IV heparin).

#### **Scenario 2: Gentamicin Dosing in Renal Impairment**

A patient with CrCl = 20 mL/min (severe renal impairment) needs gentamicin for sepsis.

- **Normal dosing:** 1.5 mg/kg every 8 hours.
- **This patient:** Gentamicin clearance is severely reduced.
  - Dose adjusted to 1–1.5 mg/kg **every 24–48 hours** (extended-interval dosing).
  - Clearance (CL) is reduced → if normal dosing were used, Css would accumulate → toxicity.

**Nursing action:**
- Verify dose adjustment with pharmacy.
- Obtain gentamicin peak and trough levels (therapeutic: peak 5–10 mcg/mL, trough <2 mcg/mL).
- Monitor serum creatinine, urine output (gentamicin is nephrotoxic).

### Drugs with Altered Clearance in Disease States

| Disease State | Effect on Clearance | Clinical Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Liver disease** | ↓ Hepatic CL | Warfarin, benzodiazepines accumulate → reduce dose by 25–75% |
| **Kidney disease** | ↓ Renal CL | Gentamicin, digoxin accumulate → extend interval or reduce dose |
| **Heart failure** | ↓ Hepatic & renal CL | Reduced organ perfusion → drug accumulation |
| **Sepsis** | ↑ Clearance (sometimes) | Increased metabolism; some drugs need higher doses |
| **Obesity** | Variable | Lipophilic drugs have ↑ Vd; clearance varies |

### ⚠️ SAFETY CALLOUTS

> **⚠️ Critical Dosing Alert: Long-Acting Drugs + Overdosing = Delayed Toxicity**
> 
> Drugs with long half-lives (warfarin, methotrexate, digoxin) accumulate with overdosing. A patient who doubles their digoxin dose by accident may not develop immediate symptoms—but toxicity develops over days as drug accumulates. Educate patients: **if you accidentally double a dose, contact your doctor; don't wait for symptoms.** Serum levels can be checked before toxicity develops.

> **⚠️ Dosing Alert: Don't "Catch Up" on Missed Doses of Long-Acting Drugs**
> 
> Patient misses a dose of warfarin and then takes a double dose the next day to "catch up." **This risks overdose and bleeding.** Teach: "If you miss a dose of warfarin, take your next scheduled dose without doubling. Call your doctor for specific guidance." Same applies to digoxin, methotrexate, metformin, and other long-acting drugs.

> **⚠️ Exam Alert: Relationship Between Clearance and Steady-State Concentration**
> 
> **Css = (Dose/τ) / CL.** If clearance decreases (kidney disease, liver disease, drug interaction), Css increases → toxicity risk. If clearance increases (enzyme induction), Css decreases → therapeutic failure. This is one of the most tested relationships in pharmacokinetics.

### Clinical Scenario: Warfarin Dosing Variability

**James**, 62 years old, is started on warfarin for atrial fibrillation.

- **Day 1:** Warfarin 5 mg → INR still not therapeutic (expected; t½ = 40 hours).
- **Day 3:** INR = 1.5 (subtherapeutic). Dose increased to 7.5 mg.
- **Day 5:** INR = 2.8 (therapeutic). Continue 7.5 mg.
- **Day 7:** INR = 4.2 (supratherapeutic). Dose decreased to 5 mg.

**Why the variability?**
1. Warfarin has variable absorption and metabolism (CYP2C9 genetics, drug interactions).
2. Steady state isn't reached until day 8–9, so INR changes gradually as drug accumulates.
3. Initial INR fluctuations are expected and don't predict final maintenance dose.

**Nursing lesson:** Warfarin dosing requires **patience and frequent INR monitoring**. Don't adjust doses daily; wait for steady state. Communicate with patients that INR titration takes time.

### Section Recap

**Half-life** determines how long a drug stays in the body and how frequently you must dose. **Steady state** is reached after 5 half-lives, when drug input equals drug output. **Loading doses** achieve therapeutic effect immediately for drugs with long half-lives (warfarin, digoxin). **Maintenance doses** sustain steady-state concentration. **Clearance** is the rate at which the body eliminates drug; reduced clearance (in liver/kidney disease) causes accumulation and toxicity; this relationship is quantified as **Css = (Dose/τ) / CL**. Understanding these concepts allows you to anticipate how quickly drugs reach therapeutic effect, recognize why steady-state concentrations vary among patients, and prevent toxicity from overdosing or accumulation in patients with reduced clearance.

## CHAPTER 2.1–2.6: KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. **ADME is the framework:** Pharmacokinetics encompasses four sequential stages (absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion) that determine drug concentration at the site of action.

2. **Route of administration matters critically:** IV = 100% bioavailability, immediate; oral = 20–90%, affected by food/pH/disease; sublingual bypasses first-pass.

3. **Protein binding affects drug activity:** Only free (unbound) drug is active; displacement interactions raise free drug concentration → toxicity.

4. **The liver is the primary drug processor:** CYP450 enzymes (Phase I), conjugation (Phase II), and transporters (Phase III) metabolize drugs; induction speeds metabolism (subtherapeutic levels); inhibition slows it (toxicity).

5. **Kidneys eliminate 90% of drugs:** Glomerular filtration, active secretion, and reabsorption handle renal clearance; CrCl (not serum creatinine) predicts renal impairment in elderly patients.

6. **Half-life determines dosing intervals:** 5 half-lives = steady state; long half-life drugs need loading doses for rapid effect.

7. **Steady-state concentration depends on clearance:** Css = (Dose/interval) / Clearance; reduced clearance → accumulation → toxicity.

8. **Pharmacokinetics varies widely among patients:** Age, weight, genetics, disease, organ function, and drug interactions necessitate dose individualization and close monitoring.
